EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  3  The Office of Internal Audit and Investigations (OIAI) conducted an audit of the UNICEF Philippines Country Office, covering the period from 1 March 2024 to 30 May 2025. The audit was conducted from 12 to 29 May 2025 in conformance with the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global Internal Audit Standards. The objective of the audit was to assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the governance, risk management and control processes related to a selection of significant risk areas of the Country Office. The specific risks evaluated are set out in the Audit Objective, Scope and Approach section of this report.  UNICEF supports the Government of the Philippines in the implementation of the country programme through partnerships with the Government and with civil society organizations (CSOs). The Philippines Country Office operates in a dynamic and risk-prone environment marked by recurring natural disasters, localized conflicts and persistent socio-economic and regional disparities. In response to these challenges, the Country Office’s programming spans both upstream system-strengthening and downstream service-delivery support, with operations tailored to the evolving risks and needs of different geographic areas. During the audited period, the Country Office transferred approximately US$14.1 million to 45 implementing partners. In planning for this audit, several risks were identified related to cash transfers to implementing partners, emergency preparedness and response, programmatic monitoring and assurance activities, management of private sector fundraising operations, and the governance and monitoring of complex and innovative programme models. The audit sought to determine whether and how the Country Office managed those risks.  Overall Conclusion  Based on the audit work performed, OIAI concluded that the assessed governance, risk management, or control processes were Partially Satisfactory, Improvement Needed, meaning that the weaknesses or deficiencies identified were unlikely to have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process (see the Appendix for   definitions of the conclusion ratings .) C ONTEXT  8  The Philippines is a lower middle-income country with a population exceeding 114 million, including a high proportion of children and youth. It is one of the most disaster-prone countries globally, regularly experiencing typhoons, earthquakes, floods and volcanic eruptions. The country has experienced strong macroeconomic growth and is expected to attain upper middle-income status in the coming years. 3   However, significant disparities persist, and child poverty remains high, affecting more than one in four children as of 2021. Inequality is deepening, with a Gini coefficient of 40.7, 4   and regional and socio- economic differences continue to affect access to services and opportunities—particularly in areas such as the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM).  The Philippines operates under a devolved governance structure, with substantial responsibilities for basic service delivery, social welfare and development planning assigned to local government units (LGUs). This decentralization presents both opportunities and challenges, with variability in local capacity contributing to unequal outcomes across regions.  The establishment of BARMM in 2019 represented a significant milestone in decentralized governance and unique opportunities for development. As the region continues to strengthen its governance structures, it faces ongoing challenges related to service delivery and child protection, including addressing the risks of violence and online exploitation.  UNICEF Country Programme and Operating Context  The 2024–2028 Country Programme Document (CPD) builds on the results and lessons of the previous 2019–2023 programme, aiming to reduce child vulnerability and promote equity through systems strengthening, digital transformation, private sector engagement and community-based service delivery. The current programme comprises six components: health and nutrition, education, child protection, child-sensitive public policy, climate resilience and programme effectiveness. It is designed to respond to both chronic development challenges and acute humanitarian risks.  The total budget for the 2024–2028 CPD is US$147.6 million, with a regular resources ceiling of US$17.3 million and other resources (non-emergency) ceiling of US$130.3 million. Additional emergency funding is mobilized as needed through humanitarian appeals. AUDIT OBJECTIVES ,   SCOPE   AND APPROACH  11  The objective of the audit was to assess the adequacy and effectiveness of governance, risk management and control processes related to selected high- and medium-risk areas of the UNICEF Philippines Country Office.  The audit scope was determined during the planning process based on an assessment of the inherent risks of the Country Office and included the following key areas:  •   Governance and accountability  •   Risk management  •   Protection from sexual exploitation and abuse (PSEA)  •   Partnership management  •   Programme monitoring  •   Private sector fundraising (PSFR)  •   Cash transfers to implementing partners  •   HACT assurance activities  •   Service contract management.  The audit fieldwork was conducted from 12 to 29 May 2025, in conformance with the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global Internal Audit Standards and UNICEF’s internal audit methodology. For the purpose of audit testing, the audit covered the period from March 2024 to May 2025.  The audit employed a combination of methods, tools and techniques, including interviews with senior management and key staff; document and system reviews (e.g., VISION, eTools, Insight); data analytics for sample selection and risk identification; transaction testing; validation of compliance with policies and procedures; site visits to programme locations and implementing partners; and triangulation of sources to validate preliminary findings.

OBSERVATIONS AND MANAGEMENT ACTION PLAN  12  The key areas where actions are needed are summarized below.  1. Risk/Fraud Risk Management   Medium  While the Country Office had taken steps to enhance risk management, such as designating Risk Champions and conducting training, risk management was not fully embedded in day-to-day operations. Specific risks related to large or innovative projects were not systematically assessed, and fraud risks were not adequately assessed or integrated into risk management activities. Partner-level awareness of fraud risks was inconsistent, and prevention messaging did not consistently reach communities or beneficiaries. These gaps increase the likelihood of undetected exposure to operational and fiduciary risks, especially in decentralized or high-risk programming contexts.  Country offices should identify, assess, monitor and respond to key risks to achievement of their objectives and ensure that the risk environment is regularly scanned for changes and that mitigating actions are assessed for their ongoing effectiveness. The audit reviewed the adequacy of the Country Office’s approach to ensuring regular assessment and management of significant and emerging risks, including fraud-related risks, and made the following observations:  The Country Office took meaningful steps to improve the quality of its risk management, including designating Risk Champions (responsible for facilitating risk identification and monitoring within their sections), and conducting office-wide risk training in 2024. The 2024 risk registers reviewed by the audit were more complete than in 2023, with a greater number of risks across multiple areas. However, the audit could not substantiate that risk awareness or mitigation ownership was consistently embedded across all sections. Risk management practices remained uneven, were not fully integrated into day-to-day decision-making or project-level oversight, and did not systematically capture project-level risks or track the progress of mitigation actions.  The review of risk registers was not a standard agenda item for senior management meetings, and there were no dedicated risk review sessions. While some operational risks were addressed, it was not clear how management ensured that all key risks were captured in the Country Office’s risk register for systematic review and monitoring of mitigation actions. In particular, for large, high-value, or innovative projects, including those with decentralized implementation or multiple partners, the audit did not find evidence of structured, ongoing risk analysis or documentation that differentiated project- specific risks from broader programme-level risks.  Fraud-related risk management was not adequate. In 2025, all fraud risks were rated 'low,' with no documented mitigation measures, despite the complex operating environment, which includes large-scale cash transfers, decentralized implementation, and vulnerability

OBSERVATIONS AND MANAGEMENT ACTION PLAN  14  2. Business Continuity Planning   Medium  The Country Office’s Business Continuity Plan, last updated in 2022, was not aligned with current operational realities. Testing and staff awareness activities were not conducted, and several designated staff roles were outdated. These gaps, which were acknowledged by Country Office management prior to the audit, reduced the Country Office’s readiness to respond effectively to potential disruptions, posing risks to staff safety, programme continuity and operational resilience.  The Philippines Country Office operates in a hazard-prone environment marked by frequent typhoons, earthquakes, and localized insecurity in areas such as BARMM. These risks demand a robust, current and tested Business Continuity Plan (BCP) to ensure uninterrupted delivery of critical services during disruptions. The existing BCP was last updated in 2022 and included key components such as essential functions, relocation protocols and emergency contact procedures. The Country Office itself acknowledged this area as a weakness during audit planning.  The audit found that the BCP covered the fundamental design elements required by UNICEF’s Policy on Business Continuity Management (2020). It identified minimum operating levels, staff responsibilities, relocation strategies and internal coordination mechanisms. However, the plan had not been updated in more than two years, despite changes in staff and the operating context. Additionally, there was no evidence of any Training, Testing, and Exercising (TT&E) activities, for example, records of drills, tabletop exercises or activation tests, since the plan’s creation. Several staff named in critical roles had left or changed functions, and there was no formal mechanism to trigger routine review or testing.  As a result, the BCP was technically compliant but not operationally ready. Without regular testing and updates, there was a risk of delayed decision-making and poor coordination in the event of an actual disruption. In a context like the Philippines, where sudden-onset events and security threats are realistic scenarios, this could seriously affect staff safety and programme continuity.  Root causes: While the initial development of the BCP met policy requirements, the absence of defined accountability for updates and lack of integration into annual risk or operational planning cycles led to its stagnation. The Country Office deprioritized BCP maintenance due to competing demands during the pandemic recovery and CPD roll-out, without embedding follow-through into routine management processes.  These issues are consistent with the findings of OIAI’s 2025 Internal Audit Report on Business Continuity Management (BCM), which highlighted that business continuity across UNICEF is often approached as a compliance task rather than a strategic, risk-

Programme Implementation Oversight   Medium  The Country Office had strengthened its oversight of programme implementation, for example, through section-level monitoring plans, standardized templates and partner support mechanisms. However, there was a need to improve the integration of programmatic and financial oversight, particularly in relation to the timeliness and quality of cash transfer processing and the structured joint review of programme delivery and expenditure.  UNICEF’s policies and procedures require Country Offices to ensure that programme implementation is regularly monitored for both results and resource use. These policies emphasize the importance of ensuring adequate programmatic and financial assurance through tools such as programmatic visits, partner reporting, and financial spot-checks and audits, to ensure that planned activities are delivered, funds are used as intended, and risks are promptly identified and addressed. The audit assessed whether the Country Office had established effective mechanisms to monitor programme implementation and financial performance, and whether delays, anomalies or non-compliance were systematically detected and followed up.

OBSERVATIONS AND MANAGEMENT ACTION PLAN  16  Integration of Programmatic and Financial Monitoring:   While technical monitoring was routinely conducted by programme teams, the audit identified instances where financial exceptions were not detected through routine programmatic visits or reporting. In one significant case, a reimbursement request exceeding US$3 million was submitted late in the programme cycle without prior authorization and was not previously identified through regular monitoring mechanisms. The request related to a large-scale, decentralized project, where implementation progress had been confirmed through programmatic visits.  The case highlighted underreporting of expenditure on FACE 5   forms over a prolonged period, which remained undetected due to the absence of mechanisms to compare financial progress with programmatic delivery in real time. While programme officers are expected to review FACE forms from a programmatic perspective, ensuring that reported expenditure is broadly aligned with implementation status, this review is typically not a financial verification and may not detect underreporting if activities are being delivered as planned. Under current HACT procedures, financial assurance typically occurs after implementation, and programmatic visits are focused on verification of results. In this instance, the absence of tools or triggers to triangulate implementation data with expenditure reporting may have contributed to the delay in detection. This limited the Country Office’s ability to detect and act on emerging financial risks in a timely manner, potentially delaying reallocations, affecting donor reporting accuracy and exposing UNICEF to reputational and compliance risks.  Root cause: This case points to a possible blind spot in UNICEF’s assurance procedures, which may not adequately facilitate early detection of financial underreporting, especially in large, decentralized programmes.  Given the potential relevance of this issue to other country offices, OIAI has brought it to the attention of UNICEF Headquarters with a view to assessing whether adjustments to HACT procedures or tools are warranted to better integrate financial and programmatic oversight. In the meantime, while this material exception appears to be an isolated case, the Country Office should mitigate the risk of any recurrence by establishing mechanisms to compare reported expenditure against planned disbursements or expected implementation timelines, as well as joint reviews between programme and finance teams to identify unusual trends in financial reporting. This will minimize exposure to delayed corrective action, compliance issues and reputational risk.  Direct Cash Transfer (DCT) and Expenditure Certification: A review of a sample of 12 FACE forms revealed multiple cases of overspending by implementing partners without prior approval, delayed system entries, and certification of expenditure that lacked any documented reference to programmatic progress or results. These issues indicate a disconnect between the verification of programme implementation and financial liquidation and increase the risk of ineligible or fraudulent expenditure.

OBSERVATIONS AND MANAGEMENT ACTION PLAN  17  UNICEF’s HACT Framework requires increased scrutiny and potential follow-up of any DCTs outstanding for more than six months. The audit team’s review of DCT ageing revealed that approximately US$1.3 million in DCTs remained outstanding for more than nine months. While the Country Office demonstrated that follow-up actions had been taken in most cases, the categorization of DCTs in VISION and related dashboards lacked clarity and consistency. Re-opened refund cases (such as those under further review due to unresolved overspending) were not distinguished from genuinely overdue transactions, resulting in inflated overdue totals and potentially misleading performance reports. In some instances, budget overspending appeared compliant because supporting documentation or justification was not properly uploaded or linked in the system. This limited the ability of managers to analyse trends, understand the underlying causes of delays and take timely and targeted corrective actions. Root causes: The observed issues appear to stem from insufficient integration between programmatic and financial monitoring processes, combined with gaps in staff understanding of certification responsibilities and inconsistent system usage. The absence of structured protocols for reconciling implementation progress with expenditure data, along with unclear categorization practices in VISION, may have contributed to delayed detection of overspending and limited the effectiveness of performance reporting and trend analysis. These include regular micro-assessments, spot-checks, audits and programmatic visits based on risk levels and cash transfer volumes. The audit assessed whether the Country Office complied with these standards and whether its HACT implementation processes, including planning, execution, documentation and follow-up, were effective in providing timely and reliable assurance.  The audit observed positive practices such as monthly assurance meetings, efforts to maintain updated risk ratings in eTools, and sustained planning support to programme sections. Third-party spot-checks were completed for several high-risk partners, and the Country Office used eTools for logging action points and documenting assurance activities. These efforts reflect commitment to the HACT framework, though the quality of implementation varied across functions. However, the following gaps were identified:  Spot-check gaps and report quality: In 2024, three implementing partners each received more than US$50,000 in direct cash transfers but were not subject to spot-checks, contrary to UNICEF’s minimum HACT assurance requirements. This was due to over- reliance on automated system tags. A manual review of transfer volumes and partner risk profiles, as a secondary control, was not conducted to identify and correct such omissions.  The audit reviewed a sample of 10 spot-check reports. Six did not include the required signatures, seven lacked classification of findings by risk level, and none clearly quantified the ineligible amounts identified, despite several referring to unallowable costs. The total value of potentially ineligible expenditure across the sample could not be reliably

While the Country Office demonstrated informal follow-up in some cases, the absence of quantified values and standardized reporting limited the effectiveness of financial recovery efforts and reduced the basis for partner risk reassessment.  Ineligible expenditure tracking and action points closure: Spot-check findings revealed inconsistent use of expenditure categories in eTools when recording ineligible costs. For instance, the generic ’Other’ classification was frequently selected instead of specifying clear types such as unsupported receipts, duplicate payments or out-of-scope expenses. This lack of specificity hindered the Country Office’s ability to analyse the nature and frequency of ineligible expenditure, reducing visibility of emerging risk patterns or possible fraud risks and limiting opportunities for targeted risk mitigation.  Twelve of 165 action points from spot-check reports remained overdue at the time of the audit fieldwork, including 11 flagged as high priority. High-priority action points typically relate to significant financial risks such as unsupported expenditure, repeated non- compliance by partners, or potential fraud indicators requiring urgent follow-up. The observed delays were linked to unassigned responsibilities, unclear escalation mechanisms and system input errors. In several cases, discrepancies between internal trackers and eTools data, caused by misapplied filters or incomplete entries, further hindered timely resolution and oversight.  Inadequate spot-check coverage and inconsistent classification of ineligible expenditure reduce the effectiveness of UNICEF’s assurance framework, limiting the organization’s ability to detect fraud and other financial irregularities, recover ineligible costs and adjust partner risk ratings based on evidence. Ultimately, this exposes the Country Office to fiduciary, reputational and compliance risks, particularly in high-risk implementation environments.  Root Causes: The observed weaknesses stemmed from fragmented assurance planning across Programme and Finance teams, over-reliance on automated system tags without adequate manual validation, and insufficient enforcement of quality standards for spot- check reporting. Additionally, gaps in staff understanding of ineligible expenditure classifications and limitations in eTools’ functionality contributed to inconsistent documentation and delayed follow-up. Travel Management   Medium  Travel transactions were frequently misclassified in general ledger accounts, with more than 100 instances of non travel-related expenses (e.g., IT equipment, training) charged to travel codes. This distorted financial reporting, created compliance risks, and undermined the integrity of expenditure data used for management decision-making and external reporting.  While the Country Office established travel authorization and cost control mechanisms, the audit noted significant misclassifications of travel-related expenditure, both in staff travel processing and implementing partner financial reporting. These issues affected the accuracy of financial records, obscured actual travel costs, and reduced the reliability of financial analysis.  The audit identified 104 cases where travel-related expenditure was misclassified in terms of General Ledger (GL) codes or location tags. Examples included international travel undertaken by Country Office staff at the request of UNICEF Headquarters but recorded under the Philippines Country Office, and national staff international travel recorded as in- country due to default routing settings. Vendor and Contract Management   Medium  While the Country Office had established procedures for contract issuance and vendor oversight, the audit noted deficiencies in the timely closure of service contracts, alignment of contract validity with grant periods, and enforcement of risk mitigation measures in vendor engagements. These gaps may affect financial accuracy, compliance with donor requirements, and UNICEF’s reputation.  UNICEF offices are expected to ensure that vendor and contract management practices support timely service delivery, financial compliance and risk mitigation, in line with the relevant UNICEF Policies.  The audit reviewed the Country Office’s service and institutional contracting processes and noted positive practices in vendor engagement and contract administration. For example, the Country Office maintained proper control over the procurement process by ensuring that all procurement cases followed established procedures and were reviewed and endorsed by the Contract Review Committee (CRC), reflecting adherence to UNICEF procurement policies and reinforcing transparency and accountability in decision-making. However, gaps were identified in the timely closure of contracts, alignment of contract

OBSERVATIONS AND MANAGEMENT ACTION PLAN  23  validity with grant periods, and enforcement of risk mitigation measures. These issues may affect financial accuracy, donor compliance and reputational safeguards.  As part of vendor due diligence procedures in the Supply Manual, UNICEF requires that any risk mitigation measures identified during the contracting process are implemented and tracked. The audit reviewed nine institutional service contracts to assess whether such measures were followed-up post-award. In two significant cases, the required mitigation actions were not implemented. One Long-Term Agreement (LTA) with a financial service provider did not include the exit strategy outlined in the procurement documentation, and another vendor had been identified during the screening process as requiring additional scrutiny due to potential reputational or compliance concerns but was nonetheless contracted without documented follow-up or mitigation measures.  In parallel, an analysis of service contract-related data identified 11 cases that remained open in VISION beyond the expected three-month closure period, including three that were still active during the audit. While some delays were due to valid reasons, such as outstanding invoices, pending vendor assessments, or unresolved extensions, delayed closure increases the risk of incomplete financial reconciliation and late recognition of obligations. It also complicates financial reporting and hinders timely reallocation of unspent funds.  Additionally, 43 vendor contracts had end-dates extending more than 30 days beyond the validity period of their corresponding grants. This increased the risk of ineligible expenditure or donor non-compliance, particularly in the absence of documented grant extensions or budget alignment.  Root cause: These issues stemmed from the absence of a contract management mechanism that integrates risk mitigation tracking, closure protocols and alignment with grant timelines across programme, supply and finance functions. This resulted in inconsistent follow-up of flagged risks, delayed contract closures and weak enforcement of mitigation measures.  AGREED ACTIONS 7  The Country Office agrees to:  i.   Clarify and reinforce roles and timelines for contract closure, vendor  evaluation, and grant alignment, supported by joint programme, supply, and  finance oversight;  ii.   Ensure that mitigation measures for vendors are formally tracked and  enforced throughout the contract lifecycle, with clear documentation  requirements incorporated into contract files and CRC minutes.

25  Definition s   of Audit   Observation   Ratings  To assist management in prioritizing the actions arising from the audit, OIAI ascribes a rating to each audit observation based on the potential consequence or residual risks to the audited entity, area, activity or process or to UNICEF as a whole. Individual observations are rated as follows:  Low  The observation concerns   a potential opportunity for improvement in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes. Low- priority observations are reported to management during the audit but are not included in the audit report. Action in response to the observation is desirable.  Medium  The observation relates to a weakness or deficiency in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes that requires resolution within a reasonable period to avoid adverse consequences for the audited entity, area, activity or process.  High  The observation concerns a fundamental weakness or deficiency in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes that requires prompt/immediate resolution to avoid severe/major adverse consequences for the audited entity, area, activity or process, or for UNICEF as a whole.
