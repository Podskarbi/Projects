EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  2  The Office of Internal Audit and Investigations (OIAI) conducted an audit of the Ukraine Regional  Refugee Response, covering the period from February 2022 to July 2023.   The audit was  conducted from March to July 2023 in accordance with the Code of Ethics and the International  Standards for the Professional Practice of Internal Auditing. The overarching objective of the audit  was to assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the governance, risk management and control  processes over a selection of significant risk areas of the refugee response, including   partnership  management, cash transfers and prevention of sexual exploitation and abuse (PSEA). The  descriptions of the specific risks evaluated are provided in the Audit Objectives, Scope and  Approach section of this report.  Since the escalation of hostilities in Ukraine in February 2022, nearly one-third of the Ukrainian  population   have   been   forced   from   their   homes,   making   this   one   of   the   largest   human  displacement crises in the world. 1   Although Ukrainian refugees fled across Europe, many stayed  in neighbouring countries, including Bulgaria, the Czech Republic, Hungary, Poland, the Republic  of Moldova, Romania and Slovakia. UNICEF provided humanitarian response support to the  governments in these and other countries in the region. UNICEF budgeted US$377.2 million and  US$229.5 million in 2022 and 2023, respectively, for the Ukraine Regional Refugee Response in  the 19 refugee-receiving countries.  Overall Conclusion  Based   on   the   audit   work   performed,   OIAI  concluded that the assessed governance, risk  management   and   control   processes   were  Partially Satisfactory, Improvement Needed ,  meaning   that   the   weaknesses   or   deficiencies  identified   were   unlikely   to   have   a   materially  negative   impact   on   the   audited   entity,   area,  activity   or   process. CONTEXT  5  The   Ukrainian   refugee   crisis   is  among the largest worldwide. Within  two   weeks   of   the   escalation   of  hostilities in November 2022, over 2  million people fled Ukraine, and by  the   end   of   December   2022,   7.9  million   refugees   were   recorded  across   Europe,   with   women   and  children making up approximately 86  per cent of the fleeing population  and the majority having crossed the  border within the first few weeks of  the conflict.  In   response   to   this   large-scale  refugee situation, the European Union triggered the application of the Temporary Protection  Directive on 4 March 2022. This directive was later extended to March 2024. As of September  2023, over 5.8 million refugees 3   from Ukraine had registered for temporary protection or similar  national protection schemes across Europe. Thousands of children had been killed or injured,  while millions had been uprooted from their homes, separated from their families or put at risk of  violence. At the time of the audit, the war in Ukraine remained fluid and unpredictable; therefore,  it was expected that refugees would continue to arrive due to the ongoing conflict.  The United Nations agreed that the United Nations High Commission for Refugees would lead  the coordination of almost 250 partner organizations, including United Nations agencies and  national and international non-governmental organizations, as part of the Regional Refugee  Response Plan (RRP) focusing on the countries that had faced the largest influx. An inter-agency  Regional Refugee Coordination Forum was established, specific working groups were activated  to   ensure   efficient   situational   information   management   and   country-specific   support,   and  additional inter-agency Regional Refugee Coordination Forums were established in each of the  refugee-hosting countries in support of government-led coordination mechanisms. AUDIT OBJECTIVES, SCOPE AND APPROACH  7  The overarching objective of the audit was to assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the  governance, risk management and control processes over a selection of significant risk areas of  the   Ukraine   Regional   Refugee   Response   in   the   refugee   response   countries,   5   including  partnerships, supplies, PSEA and accountability to affected populations (AAP). Specifically, the  audit sought to determine whether:     The   refugee   response   governance   structure   provided   clarity   on   accountabilities   and  authorities, monitored and managed the risks arising from the response and optimized  performance of the response;     Controls over the creation, updating and changing of beneficiary lists for HCT were adequate  and being implemented as designed to ensure that only bona fide beneficiaries were included  and functioning occurred as intended;     Controls over supply distribution were adequate and being implemented as designed to  objectively confirm that beneficiaries were receiving the correct amounts at the correct time.  The audit scope included key areas, set out in following table, that were selected during the audit  planning process based on an assessment of inherent risks. 6   The table below briefly describes  the inherent risks covered in the audit.  RISK AREA   KEY INHERENT RISKS EVALUATED DURING THE AUDIT  Governance and  regional coordination  The Ukraine crisis affected different countries in the region, some without a  previous   country   programme   presence.   This   response   had   specific  peculiarities as it did not always have the usual structures (e.g., country  programme   document,   staff)   that   underpin   an   emergency   response.  Therefore, there was a risk that an uncoordinated emergency scale-up and  gaps in the governance framework could fail to deal with potential threats to  the timely delivery of the Core Commitments for Children.  Organizational structure  and staffing  The   resulting   delayed   or   hurried   creation   of   national   and   sub-national  response   structures   might   have   an   adverse   impact   on   response  management,   performance   and   delivery.   Additionally,   insufficient   surge  capacity at the onset of the response, onboarding of a high volume of staff  (many without United Nations experience) and language barriers could  impact the speed and efficacy of the response.  Fraud risk management   An emphasis on delivering the emergency response and managing the risk  of failure to deliver can dilute the focus on efficient and effective management  of disbursements and distributions, which could increase the risk of fraud  and/or aid diversion.  Humanitarian response  planning and  implementation  The need to respond immediately, particularly in countries where UNICEF  did not have a programmatic presence before, might impair the adequacy of  the   assessment   of   needs   and   determination   of   programme   priorities.  Programme strategies and interventions that are inadequately informed by  the needs on the ground may increase the likelihood of weaknesses in  response design and implementation, causing inadvertent harm to targeted  communities.  Budget and funds  management  Resource and budget allocation was centralized in ECARO, but separate  cost centres for the response countries and new results frameworks in the  programme countries were created and managed by the individual offices. Procurement, supplies  and logistics  There was a significant increase in the procurement of supplies, service  contracts and consultants. In addition, the rapid emergency scale-up might  lead to an inadequate definition of needs in the procurement of goods and  services and insufficient logistics arrangements as logistics were being set  up simultaneously to the response. These risks could result in aid diversion  and/or fraud or misuse.  Results reporting   In an emergency response that is approximately 60 per cent funded by  private donors, situation reports (SitReps) are the main source of information  on how the contributed funds are being used and whether the planned results  are being achieved.  Any   disclosure   of   poor-quality   or   incorrect   information   in   the   SitReps  increases UNICEF’s exposure to reputational damage that may impact both  current and future contributions of unearmarked funds.  The audit was conducted using remote and in-person methods from March to June 2023. It  included a preliminary document review, a sample of transactions, data analytics and controls  testing, followed by on-site activities in three response countries (Poland, the Czech Republic and  the Republic of Moldova) from 12 to 23 June in accordance with the Code of Ethics and the  International Standards for the Professional Practice of Internal Auditing. For the purpose of audit  testing, the audit covered the period from February 2022 to May 2023.

OBSERVATIONS AND MANAGEMENT ACTION PLAN  9  The key areas where actions are needed are summarized below.  1. Strategy and legal framework for responding in non-  programme   countries   High  There was no properly approved legal framework or formal strategy for UNICEF cooperation in  programmes related to the response to the Ukrainian refugee and migrant crises in some  countries, creating elevated risks to the effectiveness of the response.  Regulation 8.13 of the UNICEF Financial Regulations and Rules states that UNICEF cooperation  in programmes shall be carried out on the basis of: (a) A basic agreement concluded between  UNICEF and the Government concerned, establishing the general terms and conditions for  mutual cooperation in the country, the respective commitments of UNICEF and the Government  and the administrative and technical arrangements for the implementation of UNICEF-supported  programmes and (b) Plans of operation or country programme documents concluded between  UNICEF, the Government concerned and, when appropriate, other organizations participating in  the programme. Rule 108.9 states that the Executive Director may, by written instrument,  delegate to the UNICEF Head of Office in the country or to another Official, the authority to sign,  on behalf of UNICEF, the basic agreement provided for under Regulation 8.13 (a). Under the  UNICEF Financial and Administrative Policy, the opening of zone and project offices requires the  approval of the deputy executive director, management.  On 5 March 2022, UNICEF activated a Level 3 corporate emergency to scale up its response to  the Ukrainian refugee and migrant crises in seven countries: Bulgaria, Romania, the Republic of  Moldova, Poland, the Czech Republic, Slovakia and Hungary. At the time of the activation,  UNICEF had offices in three of the countries: Bulgaria, Romania and the Republic of Moldova.  The audit team noted that Regulation 8.13 of the UNICEF Financial Regulations and Rules as  well as UNICEF’s Financial and Administrative Policy were not fully complied with, as shown  below, creating elevated risks to the effectiveness of UNICEF’s response to the Ukrainian refugee  crises in the seven countries.     The instruments establishing the general terms and conditions for UNICEF cooperation in  programmes carried out in Poland, the Czech Republic, Slovakia and Hungary – that is, the  respective commitments of UNICEF and the governments, etc. – were signed by staff  members who were not formally delegated the authority to do so. OIAI also noted that at  ministry and municipality levels, additional instruments, such as memoranda of understanding  and/or letters of exchange, used to operationalize the terms and conditions for cooperation at  those locations were also signed by staff members from the regional office and the response  office (temporary/project offices) who were not formally delegated the authority to do so.     The audit team was not provided with evidence of the formal approval of the deputy executive  director or of formal assessment which underpinned the opening of the response offices in  Poland, the Czech Republic, Slovakia and Hungary. Nor was any evidence provided of any  assessments or formal organizational response strategy like the one developed and signed  by the deputy executive directors in respect of UNICEF’s response to the migrant crises in  certain European countries in 2015.  The above occurred because there was no corporate guidance on the practical application of  Regulation   8.13   of   UNICEF’s   Financial   Regulations   and   Rules   and   the   Financial   and  Administrative Policy during emergencies in high-income, non-programme countries where  UNICEF does not have country programmes.

Monitoring and oversight of response activities   Medium  The governance and oversight systems required to ensure the effectiveness and efficiency of  UNICEF’s response to Ukraine refugee and migrant crises were not established. As a result, there  was an elevated risk that ECARO would be unable to promptly identify and address performance  issues of response offices.  A decentralized structure needs a robust governance system to manage organizational structures  and controls, how risk is monitored and addressed, and how goals are set and performance is  optimized. The audit reviewed the systems in place to ensure effective and efficient coordination  and implementation of UNICEF’s response to the Ukraine refugee crisis.  Following activation of a Level 3 corporate emergency to scale up its response to the Ukrainian  refugee and migrant crisis, ECARO created response offices in the impacted countries bordering  Ukraine – Poland, the Czech Republic, Slovakia and Hungary – where UNICEF did not already  have offices. Although ECARO was participating in the UNICEF Global Emergency Management  Team, the audit team noted the need for additional mechanisms to ensure effective execution of  its unique accountabilities across the response offices. Additionally, none of the internal meetings  that ECARO was having included a specific agenda to review the programmatic and financial  performance of the response offices and their risk management practices, notwithstanding the  fact that overall accountability for UNICEF’s response remained with ECARO.  The audit team noted that in the early days of the response, ECARO had limited capacity to  effectively discharge its monitoring and oversight accountabilities. A protracted recruitment  process meant that it initially lacked the capacity it needed to implement effective monitoring and  oversight mechanisms.

Planning for the response and   transition strategy   High  There was no transition strategy in place even though the response offices were set up in non-  programme countries for an initial period of two years. In addition, there was a lack of oversight  at a sub-national level of the commitments being made in the signed work plans, resulting in  shortfalls between the commitments made and the funds available. Due to the lack of oversight,  the commitments in work plans signed at the sub-national level sometimes exceeded the funds  available.  Country-specific response plans were developed based on the regional response plan, and work  plans were signed with government implementing partners at national and sub-national levels.  Operationalization of the response was dissimilar across the region, as various factors such as  the political environment, bureaucracy and lengthy negotiations were said to have contributed to  the delays in formalizing the agreements with governments. Some countries were able to deliver  only from the last months of 2022, which affected the overall response performance for that year.  The following was noted during the audit:  Planning of response activities:   The offices’ response work plans for 2023 were informed by  the needs assessments that were conducted at the end of 2022. Even though changes (informed  by the needs assessment) were made to the 2022 responses’ HAC indicators, however, the  offices’ 2023 response plans were not appropriately adjusted. Furthermore, the detailed work  plans (memoranda of understanding and letters) signed with government partners including  ministries and municipalities were not aligned with the offices’ response plans. For example, the  audit noted that signed work plans with government partners had timeframes that were beyond  the timeframes of the annual emergency plans or HAC appeal. There was thus the risk that  UNICEF was not addressing the needs of refugees and migrants effectively.  The audit also noted that one office signed work plans with government partners before the  umbrella agreement between UNICEF and the government was put in place, the expected  implementation dates of some signed work plans were after the end date of the umbrella  agreement, and there were insufficient funds for UNICEF to meet its commitments in signed work  plans. The nonalignment of the work plans with umbrella agreements and the establishment of  commitments without funds to meet those commitments violate UNICEF requirements and create  a potential reputational risk.  Transition strategy:   A premature and unplanned phasing out from a humanitarian assistance  programme may undermine the objectives of the response as either government or other actors  are unable to fill the void or the targeted population has not built an appropriate level of resilience,  thereby reversing any gains made. On the other hand, delays can be equally damaging as the

12  response may create a dependency on assistance or result in losses for the organization.  Importantly, the audit noted that the set-up of the offices was on a temporary basis and based on  the   humanitarian   response,   as   the   Executive   Board   had   not   approved   any   development  programmes in those countries.  The audit’s review found that the regional office had not developed a phased adaptive transition  strategy as part of its planning, nor had it ensured that the offices considered and implemented  such strategies to ensure better outcomes for the targeted refugees. In May 2023, ECARO  prepared a strategy, essentially to transition from the use of simplified emergency procedures  under   Level 3 to   regular   programming.   A holistic medium-to-long-term   transition   strategy,  however, has not been formalized and appropriately approved. Further, because the response  offices did not have their own phased adaptive strategies at the time, restructuring exercises in  those   offices   were   driven   mainly   by   reduced   funding   rather   than   by   changes   in   needs,  programming and type of skills required. The lack of a formal transition strategy leaves the  regional office and response offices unprepared as to how the emergency response will adapt to  the changing needs and coping capacities of the targeted populations. Implement appropriate controls to ensure that financial commitments set out in signed  work plans are based on the availability of resources.  iii.   Develop a strategy that will be executed, if needed, when transitioning from immediate  humanitarian response in each response country. Staff management   Medium  There were significant increases in staffing of response offices without a formal staffing capacity  and affordability analysis. There were also inadequate staff welfare mechanisms in response  offices.  Staffing:   Post management enables an office to effectively keep the staffing structure and related  expenditures within reasonable parameters and aligned to the programme. The audit found that  alongside the formal programme and budget review processes for the response offices, there  were several mail polls 7   that resulted in the creation of additional posts across the responding  offices with inadequate rigour in terms of cost analysis and stakeholder consultation on the  propriety of these additional posts. In addition, as the offices were seeking skills that were not  available in the response locations, they predominantly opted for competitive sourcing and, in  some instances, it took up to three months before staff were onboarded. This is contrary to the  7   Mail polls, or ad hoc reviews by email, are requests for post changes made and exceptionally approved outside the scheduled  programme and budget review processes.

13  requirement of four weeks in an emergency. Further, in anticipation of a large-scale emergency  response, some offices opted for mainly fixed-term contracts, contrary to the fluid nature of an  emergency resourced with short-term funding.  Although the regional office approved the posts on the basis that total staffing costs were less  than 15 per cent of the HAC appeal, the calculation of the 15 per cent was based on a level of  funding that was not guaranteed. As a result, a June 2023 rightsizing exercise undertaken by the  regional office was mainly informed by the reduction in funding and aimed at minimizing UNICEF  corporate liability, with some of the staff posts marked to be delimited or abolished shortly after  staff were recruited.  Staff welfare:   A number of the staff members recruited were either new to the United Nations  system or unfamiliar with emergencies. The remainder had emergency experience and/or limited  experience of working through national systems in high-income countries. Hence, although the  response offices conducted onboarding, the induction programmes provided were not tailored to  the context and did not encompass all emergency pre-deployment requirements as per UNICEF  emergency procedures.  Additionally, the response offices did not have the statutory staff welfare structure, and staff  spoken to noted that although they were aware of and had access to the regional office staff  counsellors, locally there was limited knowledge on staff welfare matters and little advance  warning on the rightsizing exercise, which had an impact on staff morale and well-being.  Moreover, for international staff at duty stations in countries without a cooperation agreement or  updated/relevant basic cooperation agreement, there were challenges to their immunities and  privileges, specifically regarding the legal process, entry and residence for the execution of their  duties. In addition, their families might not receive visas to stay in the country or could be denied  access to services if not considered residents. Accounting for supplies   Medium  Given the scale and complexity of the deployment for the response, ECARO might have lacked  sufficient capacity and experience on specific aspects of financial accounting, leading to errors  and delays in accounting for supplies. Prevention of sexual exploitation and abuse   Medium  The   results   from   monitoring   related   to   PSEA   were   not   systematically   documented   and  communicated for action and follow-up. Further, partners’ PSEA assessments for subcontracted  CSOs were not enforced. As a result, offices may not have been able to determine whether the  risk of sexual exploitation and abuse was appropriately mitigated or whether all partners were well  equipped to prevent and respond to this risk, and therefore levels assumed might not be  commensurate with UNICEF’s zero tolerance for sexual exploitation and abuse.  The audit team reviewed the measures in place to enable the assessment of PSEA and the  implementation and monitoring of necessary mitigating actions in the communities served and  noted the following:  Sub-contracted partners risks:   UNICEF requires any sub-contracted CSO (or secondary  organization) in charge of an activity that involves contact with beneficiaries to be assessed by  the partner within six months, to cover the downstream risk. The audit noted that the responding  offices did not request PSEA assessment for CSOs subcontracted by municipalities, and found  that there were varying, and at times limited, levels of PSEA awareness by CSOs in direct contact  with the affected population, such as at Blue Dots, Spilno Spots and Education Hubs. The audit  team notes that UNICEF has not yet provided guidance to field offices on how to engage with the  government partners regarding their sub-contracted CSOs.  Risk monitoring and oversight:   The responding offices had established PSEA action plans that  included mitigation measures. The audit, however, observed a lack of systematic analysis and  monitoring of sexual exploitation and abuse risks. For example, there was inadequate follow-up  from programmatic or field visits on PSEA points such as training. In addition, the offices did not  have evidence that results of risk assessments, issues and concerns pertaining to PSEA  implementation were well documented and appropriately reported for action. At the time of the  audit, offices were taking steps towards strengthening PSEA monitoring by embedding PSEA  questions in programme monitoring templates.

Programme monitoring   Medium  The guidance on monitoring was issued by the regional office during the audit. Therefore, the  audit found that the preparation and implementation of a field monitoring plan was uneven and  inconsistent across the responding offices, and thus the approach to field monitoring was not risk-  based as some offices developed their plans based on the number of monitoring activities and  not the risks of the response. In addition, there were weaknesses in the monitoring of programme  supplies delivered to targeted communities.  Field monitoring:   Field monitoring takes place at community and service point levels to help  assure progress towards achieving planned outputs. The regional office expected the responding  offices to put in place monitoring plans and use those to inform the response. The audit found  that the seven responding offices relied primarily on programmatic visits in 2022 and only  developed field monitoring plans in 2023. The implementation of the monitoring plans, however,  was uneven. For instance, some offices were implementing their plans, while the remainder had  drafts or had not yet developed a plan. There were also cases in which the plan did not consider  the programmatic risk in determining the type, frequency and coverage of monitoring visits.  Further, the audit reviewed the quality of 62 sampled programmatic visits carried out with 42  implementing partners. It was noted that in 20 cases, the report did not make reference to the  activity or results achieved; as such, it was unclear what exactly was verified during the visit. The  lack of coordinated and risk-informed monitoring plans could result in challenges not being dealt  with   or   escalated   in   a   timely   manner   and   therefore   the   inability   to   adapt   the   response  appropriately. Although the offices provided the audit team with evidence of some  distribution reports and pictures of the distribution of UNICEF supplies, there was no structured  plan nor implementation of SEUM activities. The audit also noted that in some offices, field  monitoring, programmatic assurance, SEUM, AAP and PSEA were all considered in the plan.  Such comprehensive coverage, however, was not in place from the onset. The lack of structured  end user monitoring across the response meant that the regional office had no assurance that

Fraud risk management   Medium  A fraud and misconduct risk assessment would provide a necessary foundation for determining  risk tolerance levels and would help management identify the risks from fraud and misconduct,  identify gaps or weaknesses in controls and implement commensurate mitigants and other  appropriate preventive tools.  Oversight and leadership:   The refugee response, led by ECARO, had particularities – such as  the creation of new offices, lack of standard legal agreements for working with the different levels  of government, and unclear delegations of authority – that made it pertinent that the regional office  exercise closer oversight over key response risks, and in particular fraud risks. Although risk  exercises were conducted,   t he audit noted that leadership around fraud risk at the regional level  was slow in starting up and needed strengthening as fraud risk did not feature as an agenda item  in any management meetings, and the anti-fraud strategy that was drafted in May 2023 excluded  the programme countries, even though they had never managed emergencies at such a scale.  Failure by the regional office to oversee fraud risk management for all offices created a missed  opportunity to create, implement and enforce a coherent response for any identified high risks of  fraud across the region.  Fraud risk assessment:   The interviewed offices perceived the risk of fraud as low because they  were operating in high-income countries and had complied with UNICEF policies and procedures.  This perception, however, was not based on any actual assessment of contextual fraud risks or  checks of the effectiveness of controls to mitigate these risks. For example, the audit noted that  there was no specific risk assessment, including risk of fraud, for the HCT programme, despite  the fact that the programme was being implemented for the first time. Nor had there been any  assessment of the potential risks of using government systems. As there was no fraud risk  assessment, there were no appropriate context-specific mitigating actions or guidance put in  place or appropriate assurance activities undertaken that could provide reasonable assurance  that any mitigating measures taken to prevent fraud were adequate.  Potential conflicts of interest:   UNICEF staff members in key positions must disclose actual or  potential conflicts of interest to the head of office and the Ethics Office. The audit noted that staff  in the regional and responding offices were assigned key tasks in which potential conflicts of  interest might occur, but ECARO did not require them to file a financial disclosure statement.  Given the significant number of new hires and appointments and funds being handled in the  region, the risk to the organization resulting from a staff member’s potential conflict of interest was  higher and thus additional steps to identify and address potential conflicts of interest should have  been taken.  Communication and training:   Communication and training are major components of UNICEF’s  Anti-Fraud Strategy and the development of an anti-fraud culture. The audit noted that fraud  awareness among staff was low, as training on fraud risk commenced more than a year after the

20  start of the response. Additionally, there were no periodic communications to staff on fraud and  misconduct.  AGREED ACTION 13  ECARO agrees to:  i.   Put in place a regional risk management structure that will conduct regional fraud risk  assessments at appropriate intervals, including the assessment of all significant or new  programmes. The fraud risk assessments should inform the type and level of assurance  activities and be used to develop region-specific key fraud risk indicators and exceptions  that can be monitored.  ii.   Enforce all financial disclosure filing obligations of all staff in regional and all responding  offices. Lessons learned and knowledge management   Medium  There was insufficient evidence that lessons learned from previous UNICEF responses were  utilized, and there was an absence of a systematic process of documenting learning from  UNICEF’s response to the ongoing Ukraine refugee and migrant crises.  Knowledge management supports effective utilization of knowledge and lessons from the past in  delivering better results. ECARO informed the audit team that it utilized lessons learned from  UNICEF’s response to the 2015 migrant crises to inform the response to the current Ukraine  refugee and migrant crises. No documentation of the lessons learned from that response was  shared with the audit team, however. It was also not clear which lessons from that response were  utilized and how. In the current response, the audit team noted gaps that might have been avoided  if lessons from the 2015 response had been utilized. There were gaps in communications and  collaboration within ECARO and in the overall regional response planning. There were also gaps  in communication, coordination and collaboration with National Committees (NatComs)   –   non-  governmental organizations that UNICEF was already working through in the response countries  to mobilize resources for its global operations and advance children’s rights globally. From 2022  to 31 May 2023, approximately 60 per cent of the total funds that UNICEF allocated to its response  to the Ukraine refugee and migrant crises came through NatComs in countries where their  response was being   delivered.   While   there are existing   cooperation agreements between  NatComs and UNICEF, these are administered by Private Fundraising and Partnerships (PFP)  Division based in Geneva and do not specifically require UNICEF regional and local offices to  coordinate their activities with the NatComs.  The audit team also noted the absence of a systematic process of documenting learning from  UNICEF’s response to the ongoing Ukraine refugee and migrant crises. In fact, handover from  one staff member to another was done mainly via emails, with no centralized repository of such  emails to help future review of response activities. There were gaps in knowledge of the decision-  making process for important issues like staff recruitment, the deployment of HOPE and the  establishment of response offices. Overall, for the ongoing response, the audit found that there  was no systematic approach to collecting, analysing and documenting learning from the current

21  response. There was thus a risk that the knowledge and lessons from the current response might  again not be leveraged in the future.  AGREED ACTION 14  ECARO agrees to develop and implement knowledge management processes that guide the  effective capture, organization, analysis and sharing of the knowledge acquired from crises in  the region. The actions should ensure best practices and that lessons learned are preserved  and available for improved organizational performance in humanitarian actions for children. Further, using lessons learned from the Ukraine response,  PFP should develop guidelines to manage the dual presence of NatComs and humanitarian  emergency response offices. 23  APPENDIX  Definitions of Audit Observation Ratings  To assist management in prioritizing the actions arising from the audit, OIAI ascribes a rating to  each audit observation based on the potential consequence or residual risks to the audited entity,  area, activity or process, or to UNICEF as a whole. Individual observations are rated as follows:  Low  The observation concerns a potential opportunity for improvement in the  assessed governance, risk management or control processes. Low-priority  observations are reported to management during the audit but are not  included   in   the   audit   report.   Action   in   response   to the   observation   is  desirable.  Medium  The   observation relates to a weakness or   deficiency   in the assessed  governance, risk management or control processes that requires resolution  within a reasonable period of time to avoid adverse consequences for the  audited entity, area, activity or process.  High  The observation concerns a fundamental weakness or deficiency in the  assessed governance, risk management or control processes that requires  prompt/immediate resolution to avoid severe/major adverse consequences  for the audited entity, area, activity or process, or for UNICEF as a whole.  Definitions of Overall Audit Conclusions  The above ratings of audit observations are then used to support an overall audit conclusion for  the area under review, as follows:  Satisfactory   The assessed governance, risk management or control processes  were adequate and functioning well.  Partially  Satisfactory,  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management or control processes  were generally adequate and functioning but needed improvement.  The weaknesses or deficiencies identified were unlikely to have a  materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity,  area, activity   or   process.  Partially  Satisfactory,  Major  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management or control processes  needed   major   improvement.   The   weaknesses   or   deficiencies  identified could have a materially negative impact on the performance  of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  Unsatisfactory  The assessed governance, risk management or control processes  were   not   adequately   established   or   not   functioning   well.
