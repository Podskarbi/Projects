EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  3  The Office of Internal Audit and Investigations (OIAI) conducted an audit of the Kyrgyzstan Country Office (hereafter the Country Office), covering the period from January 2024 to September 2025. The audit was conducted from July 2025 to September 2025 in conformance with the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global Internal Audit Standards. The objective of the audit was to   assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the governance, risk management and control processes related to a selection of significant risk   areas   of the Kyrgyzstan Country Office.   The specific risks evaluated are set out in the Audit Objective, Scope and Approach section of this report.  The   Kyrgyzstan Country Office   works with government and civil society organizations (CSOs) to implement planned programme activities. During the audited period, the Country Office transferred approximately US$4.1 million in cash to implementing partners and procured US$2.1 million in programme-related supplies. There were several risks related to   the use of   partnerships, transferring cash and supplies to implementing partners, field monitoring and construction projects. The audit sought to determine whether and how the   Country Office   managed those risks.  Overall Conclusion  Based on the audit work performed, OIAI concluded that the assessed governance, risk management, or control processes were  Satisfactory   meaning that the assessed governance, risk management, and control processes were adequate and functioning well. Weaknesses or deficiencies were identified in only two areas. These were unlikely to have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process and actions to address them were already underway. C ONTEXT  6  Kyrgyzstan is a landlocked, mountainous, lower middle-income country with a multi-ethnic population and vibrant civil society. The country has an estimated population of 7.2 million, with children representing 37 per cent. In 2024, the country ranked 117 out of 193 countries on the Human Development Index and ranked 140 out of 180 on the Transparency International Corruption Index.  In the last decade there has been significant progress in the realization of child rights, especially in child survival, child justice and pre-school education. However, due to significant inequities between regions, urban and rural areas, challenges remain, especially for children affected by migration, children with disabilities, children from the poorest households, and girls.  The country is vulnerable to earthquakes, epidemics, civil unrest and border conflict, although the risk of armed border conflict was substantially reduced in March 2025, when Kyrgyzstan and Tajikistan signed a border demarcation pact, resolving a long-standing border dispute. In 2024, there was a spike in measles cases, leading to 12 deaths, primarily among children with disabilities and malnourished children.  UNICEF Country Pr ogramme  The Country Programme Document   2023-2027   comprises five programme components: (i) health, nutrition and environment; (ii) education and adolescent development; (iii) child protection; (iv) social policy; and (v) programme effectiveness. In all programme components, UNICEF aims to support system strengthening to ensure sustainability of progress through high-quality policy advocacy, technical expertise, improved public finance for children, digital transformation across sectors, and modelling and scaling integrated approaches to address the challenges faced by children in the realization of their rights. The country programme   aims to reduce disparities between urban and rural areas, and between boys and girls, with a special focus on disadvantaged groups.  The total budget for the Country Programme Document is US$42.3 million, with US$7.3 million from regular resources 1   and US$35 million from other resources 2   The Country Office reported total expenditure for the period 1 January 2024 to 31 August 2025 of US$13 million. AUDIT OBJECTIVES ,   SCOPE   AND APPROACH  8  The objective of the audit was to   assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the   governance, risk management and control processes   related to a selection of significant risk   areas   of the Kyrgyzstan Country Office.  The audit scope was determined during the audit planning process based on an assessment of the inherent 3   risks   of the Kyrgyzstan Country Office,   and included the following areas:  •   Governance  •   Risk management  •   Protection from sexual exploitation and abuse (PSEA)  •   Partnership management  •   Programme   monitoring  •   Cash transfers to implementing partners  •   Harmonized approach to cash transfers (HACT) assurance activities  •   Procurement of supplies and service contracts  •   Government procurement services and supply logistics  •   Construction  •   Travel management.  The audit fieldwork was conducted from 15 to 26 September 2025 in conformance with the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global Internal Audit Standards. For the purpose of audit testing, the audit covered the period from 1 January 2024 to 31 August 2025. The audit involved a combination of methods, tools, and techniques, including interviews, data analytics, document review, tests of transactions, site visits and validation of preliminary observations.  3   Inherent risk refers to the potential adverse event that could occur if management takes no actions, including internal control activities. The higher the likelihood of the event occurring and the more serious the impact would be should the adverse event occur, the stronger the need for adequate and effective risk management and control processes.

OBSERVATIONS AND MANAGEMENT ACTION PLAN  9  The key areas where actions are needed are summarized below.  1. Risk Management   Medium  By integrating the 2025 annual risk assessment and management planning exercises, the Country Office ensured that the Annual Management Plan was informed by a thorough, up-to-date view of key risks. While the risk register was subsequently updated to reflect the challenges associated with the global funding crisis, key risks related to the construction of a national vaccine warehouse were not included nor were they discussed in CMT meetings to ensure appropriate establishment and monitoring of mitigating actions.  Country offices should identify, assess, monitor and respond to key risks to the achievement of their objectives and ensure that the risk environment is regularly scanned for changes and mitigating actions are assessed for their ongoing effectiveness. A coordinated, comprehensive approach is essential to ensure sufficient management and oversight of risks and to allow timely, risk-informed decision-making and escalation of issues, as appropriate. The audit reviewed the adequacy of the Country Office’s approach to ensuring regular assessment and management of significant and emerging risks, and made the following observations:  Proactive risk management: In 2024, the Country Office was proactive in conducting a  programmatic re-alignment to mitigate risks related to the changing programmatic and  funding landscape. Following a Regional Office mission in January 2025, a consultant was  engaged to conduct a detailed affordability analysis to support the Country Office in  identifying a fit-for-purpose, sustainable structure to deliver on the updated priorities  identified by the programmatic re-alignment and to support the preparation of the PBR.  The affordability analysis captured the scale and nature of the expected funding gap, and  specific challenges were addressed in a revised resource mobilization strategy. These  exercises provided a strong foundation for the subsequent PBR, required by UNICEF  Headquarters as part of the wider global restructuring.  Integrated risk assessment and annual planning exercise: In 2025,   the Country Office held a one-day workshop, involving all staff, that combined the   annual risk assessment and Annual Management Plan (AMP) development exercise . The exercise involved grouping all staff into five   programme   and operation sections, each tasked with reviewing the previous risk assessment and updating the risk categories, risk areas, risk description and mitigation actions, based on the recent programmatic review and re-alignment and the current operating context. The results were consolidated into the country office risk register. This approach ensured that the development of the AMP was informed by a thorough, up-to-date view of the key Country Office risks. The risk register was revised again in August 2025 to reflect the challenges associated with the global funding crisis.

10  Risk   management   for   construction projects : Construction projects can involve significant expenditure and are often complex and high risk due to the number of actors involved, the need to coordinate multiple contracts and the potentially severe consequences of substandard work. Such risks require careful consideration as part of the planning process, allowing sufficient time to take adequate mitigating actions while ensuring completion of the work within the agreed timeframe and to the expected standard. Documenting the risk assessment ensures that accountabilities for agreed actions are clear, progress can be more easily monitored and any significant issues more easily escalated for management decision-making.  As part of the national immunization programme, in partnership with the Ministry of Health, the Country Office was responsible for reconstructing two regional vaccine warehouses at a cost of approximately US$178,000. At the time of the audit, one of the warehouses was complete and the procurement process for the other was in progress. In addition, the Country Office had signed an agreement for the construction of a national vaccine warehouse, estimated at US$1.3 million, which was pending the necessary land permit and finalization of the grant agreement with the donor.  The Country Office had taken practical steps to manage certain risks related to the two regional warehouse construction projects, such as hiring engineering consultants to support the design and technical oversight of the construction works. While the national vaccine warehouse project was still at the preparatory stage, there was no record of any risk assessment or plans to ensure effective mitigation of some significant risks that were noted by the audit. For example, there was a risk of ineffective planning and project oversight due to limited availability of technical expertise, and a risk of scope creep due to changing partner requirements. Also, there was uncertainty about the availability of donor funds to finance this significant construction project, despite the grant agreement being signed in 2022.  The audit noted that the Country Office risk register did not include any construction- related risks and there was no record of discussion or monitoring of these significant risks in CMT meetings. Following discussion with the audit team, the Country Office prepared a risk mitigation plan for the national vaccine warehouse.  Root cause(s): The Country Office had not previously undertaken significant construction work and did not have a practice of conducting a structured, documented risk assessment to ensure that adequate risk mitigation measures can be planned for and monitored until project completion. Programme monitoring   Medium  There was a need to ensure adequate supply end-user monitoring during programmatic visits, including for supply-only partners, to provide assurance on the distribution and use of supplies by the intended recipients. The Country Office did not utilize the eTools field monitoring dashboard data to monitor the results of field visits, to ensure that all necessary actions are recorded for follow-up.  According to the UNICEF Procedure on Monitoring,   programme   monitoring includes regular tracking of progress in key interventions and country   programme   outputs, covering implementation of workplan activities, programmatic risks and bottlenecks, the end-use of supplies, advocacy objectives, feedback from affected populations and the evolving context (including unintended effects of   programmes ). The audit assessed the adequacy and effectiveness of field monitoring and programmatic assurance and made the following observations:  Programmatic visits of supply-only partners: Where the value of supplies provided exceeds the cash transfer component of partnerships, country offices are required to base the minimum number of programmatic monitoring visits on the higher value and to include monitoring of end-use of supplies. Three government partners with minimal or no cash transfers received supplies from UNICEF worth US$737,000. In two cases, the Country Office did not conduct a programmatic visit. In the other, a programmatic visit was conducted in 2024 but did not cover supply end-user monitoring.  Root causes:   The Country Office was not aware of the requirement to consider the value of supplies in determining the minimum number of programmatic visits. In addition, the mimimum number of programmatic visits was generated in eTools based on the amount

12  of cash transfers and the partner risk rating, without factoring in the value of supplies. The value of supplies distributed to partners is only available in VISION and the Supply Chain Monitoring Dashboard.  Use of the eTools field monitoring dashboard: The eTools field monitoring dashboard shows an overview of monitoring visits in the Field Monitoring Module, including the coverage, summarized findings, the quality of the visits and any bottlenecks. However, the Country Office did not utilize this dashboard data to monitor the results of field visits. For example, in seven monitoring visits conducted in 2024 and 2025, the audit noted negative responses to two questions related to PSEA. In six of these, there was no action point, and in the remaining case, the action point was recorded in FMM, but closed without sufficient evidence of implementation by the partner.  Subsequent to this audit finding, the Deputy Representative sent an email to all programme staff emphasizing the need to record action points, to follow up any negative response to PSEA-related questions and to close the action point only after sufficient evidence of implementation has been obtained.  Root cause: Since 2024, the Country Office was focused on the transition from the Trip Management Module to Field Monitoring Module and had not yet had time to become fully familiar with the eTools field monitoring dashboard data.  Quality and coverage of programmatic visits: Programmatic visits provide assurance with respect to the status of programme implementation compared to the agreed workplan and the reliability of programme results reported by implementing partners.  The audit reviewed a sample of 14 programmatic visit reports from FMM and four programmatic visit reports from the Trip Management Module and noted the following:  •   In three out of five programme visits to partners that received substantial supplies, supply end-user monitoring was not covered in the reports.  •   In 2 out of 11 reports with findings, the action points were not recorded in eTools for tracking. However, in both cases, the staff did follow up with the partners.  •   In three out of six programmatic visits to CSO partners, PSEA was not covered. PSEA questions became mandatory for CSO partners as of the July 2025 CMT meeting. As noted above, an email reminder was sent by the Deputy Representative in September 2025.  Root cause(s):   Programme sections are responsible for planning and carrying out programmatic visits. However, the audit noted limited coordination with the supply section to ensure coverage of supply end-user monitoring in the planning and implementation of programmatic visits. This represents a missed opportunity to leverage the expertise of both sides to strengthen supply end-user monitoring and build the capacity of partners to effectively manage and distribute the supplies.

14  Definition s   of Audit   Observation   Ratings  To assist management in prioritizing the actions arising from the audit, OIAI ascribes a rating to each audit observation based on the potential consequence or residual risks to the audited entity, area, activity or process or to UNICEF as a whole. Individual observations are rated as follows:  Low  The observation concerns   a potential opportunity for improvement in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes. Low- priority observations are reported to management during the audit but are not included in the audit report. Action in response to the observation is desirable.  Medium  The observation relates to a weakness or deficiency in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes that requires resolution within a reasonable period to avoid adverse consequences for the audited entity, area, activity or process.  High  The observation concerns a fundamental weakness or deficiency in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes that requires prompt/immediate resolution to avoid severe/major adverse consequences for the audited entity, area, activity or process, or for UNICEF as a whole.  Definitions of Overall Audit Conclusions  The above ratings of audit observations are then used to support an overall audit conclusion for the area under review, as follows:  Satisfactory   The assessed governance, risk management, and control processes were adequate and functioning well.  Partially  Satisfactory,  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management, and control processes were generally adequate and functioning but needed improvement. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified were unlikely to have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  Partially  Satisfactory,  Major  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management, or control processes needed major improvement. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified could have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  Unsatisfactory  The assessed governance, risk management or control processes were not adequately established or did not function well. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified could have a severely negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  APPENDIX
