EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  3  The Office of Internal Audit and Investigations (OIAI) conducted an audit of the Sri Lanka Country Office, covering the period from January to early October 2025. The audit was conducted from late October 2025 to November 2025 in conformance with the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global Internal Audit Standards. The objective of the audit was to   assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the governance, risk management, and control processes related to a selection of significant risk   areas   of the Sri Lanka Country Office.  The specific risks evaluated are set out in the Audit Objective, Scope and Approach section of this report.  Sri Lanka Country Office (hereafter, the country office) works with government and civil society organizations (CSOs) to implement planned programme activities. During the audited period, the country office transferred approximately US$5.2 million in cash to implementing partners and procured US$6.6 million in programme-related supplies. There were several risks related to the use of partnerships, transferring cash and supplies to implementing partners, field monitoring and supply chain management. The audit sought to determine whether and how the country office managed those risks.  Overall Conclusion  Based   on   the   audit   work   performed,   OIAI concluded that the assessed governance, risk management,   or   control   processes   were Partially   Satisfactory,   Improvement Needed   meaning   that   the   assessed governance, risk management, and control processes   were   generally   adequate   and functioning   but   needed   improvement.   The weaknesses or deficiencies identified were unlikely to have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process. The audit team also made several   observations   related to the management of the key  risks evaluated. In particular, OIAI noted:  •   Risk management   and fraud risk assessments :   The audit identified gaps in the country office’s risk management framework, particularly in fraud risk assessment. Fraud risks were rated “very low” despite Sri Lanka’s high corruption perception index. Vendor due diligence was weak, with most vendors not registered in United Nations Global Marketplace (UNGM) and some purchase orders (POs) issued to vendors who were flagged in the earlier years’ supplier evaluations as “not recommended” or high financial or significant financial risk without adequate documented mitigation measures. Further, the two implementing partners interviewed by the audit team lacked awareness of mandatory fraud reporting requirements, as fraud topics were excluded from harmonized approach to cash transfers (HACT) training and no anti- fraud sensitization materials were provided. Additionally, the country management team (CMT) inconsistently reviewed risk management issues, limiting oversight and timely implementation of mitigation measures. These gaps increased the risk of undetected fraud, financial loss, and reputational damage.  •   Protection from sexual exploitation and abuse (PSEA) : At the time of the audit the country office was in the process of implementing various PSEA-related actions including community consultations to identify reporting barriers, development and display of sexual exploitation and abuse (SEA) reporting materials at   programme   sites,

5  PSEA sessions for hiring managers, and communication to CSOs on focal points and resources. There were delays in the implementation of some planned activities which affected the full rollout of UNICEF’s safeguarding standards. There was limited community awareness and safe reporting mechanisms, increasing the risk of SEA allegations going unreported. Additionally, gaps were noted in CSO PSEA capacity assessments, where positive scores were assigned without supporting documentation and capacity strengthening improvement plans for medium-capacity partners, which were overdue.  •   Programme   and field monitoring :   A cohesive monitoring plan did not exist beyond HACT minimum required programmatic visits that include other risk informed determinants such as PSEA and accountability to affected populations (AAP). Audit sample testing identified recurring quality gaps in programmatic assurance documentation.  •   Cash transfers to   i mplementing   p artners:   The audit observed fragmentation of cash transfers, where certain partners submitted numerous low-value funding authorization  and certificate of expenditure ( FACE)   forms, creating operational inefficiencies and reducing assurance coverage during spot checks. Additionally, the audit identified misclassification of approximately   US$1 million in HACT-related expenditure due to incorrect application of expense categories, which could distort expenditure reporting and limit data-driven decision-making.   As of the end of the audit fieldwork, the country office indicated that it was in the process of correcting the misclassified transactions  •   Implementing partnerships management :   The audit noted that CSO partnerships were primarily established through direct selection, with only three   programme  documents (PDs) created following an open selection process. Over-reliance on direct selection may limit opportunities to diversify partnerships.  •   HACT   f inancial   a ssurance activities :   The audit noted that the country office generally  complied with HACT requirements, completing all planned assurance activities for 2024 and most for 2025. However, the level of assurance derived from these activities was limited due to low testing coverage during spot checks - only 39 per cent of total reported expenditure was tested in 2025 and 27 per cent in 2024 - driven by a compliance-focused approach and fragmentation of cash transfers. Additionally, quality gaps were observed in spot check reports, with missing details such as budget  references and transaction approvers in several cases. Further, prioritization of findings was inconsistent, with some significant issues rated as “low” and not followed up,  contrary to UNICEF guidance requiring consideration of qualitative factors.  •   Supply   c hain   m anagement:   The audit noted weaknesses across supply planning, distribution visibility, customs clearance, warehousing oversight, procurement controls, and contract review committee (CRC) functioning. Supply plans were

CRC documentation gaps and inadequate mandatory training completion further weakened assurance over procurement decisions. Supply end user monitoring (SEUM) was not systematically implemented or documented. AUDIT OBJECTIVES ,   SCOPE   AND APPROACH  12  The objective of the audit was to   assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the governance, risk management, and control processes related to a selection of significant risk   areas   of the country office are adequate and functioning well.  The audit scope was determined during the audit planning process based on an assessment of the inherent 2   risks   of the country office,   and included the following areas:  •   Risk management, including fraud risk management  •   Protection from sexual exploitation and abuse (PSEA)  •   Programme and field monitoring  •   Implementing partnership management  •   Cash transfers to implementing partners  •   HACT financial assurance activities  •   Supply chain management  The audit fieldwork was conducted from late October 2025 to November 2025 in conformance with the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global Internal Audit Standards. For the purpose of audit testing, the audit covered the period from January 2024 to early October 2025. The audit involved a combination of methods, tools and techniques, including interviews, programme site visits, data analytics, document review, tests of transactions, evaluations and validation of preliminary observations.  2   Inherent risk refers to the potential adverse event that could occur if management takes no actions, including internal control activities. OBSERVATIONS AND MANAGEMENT ACTION PLAN  13  The key areas where actions are needed are summarized below.  1. Risk management and fraud risk assessments   Medium  The audit identified gaps in the   country o ffice’s risk management framework, particularly  in fraud risk assessment. Fraud risks were rated “very low” despite Sri Lanka’s high Corruption Perceptions Index. Vendor due diligence was weak, with most vendors not registered in UNGM and some POs issued to vendors who were flagged in the earlier years’ supplier evaluations as “not recommended”   or high financial risk or significant financial risk   without adequate documented mitigation measures. Further, the two implementing partners interviewed by the audit team lacked awareness of mandatory fraud reporting requirements, as fraud topics were excluded from HACT training and no anti-fraud sensitization materials were provided. Additionally, the CMT inconsistently reviewed risk management issues, limiting oversight and timely implementation of  mitigation measures. These gaps increased the risk of undetected fraud, financial loss,  and reputational damage.  The   country office   has integrated risk identification into its standard operating practices  with active participation from both programme and operations teams. Risk mitigation  measures are discussed collaboratively and assigned to relevant staff. However, the  internal audit   team identified certain areas for improvement to strengthen the risk  management process.  Fraud risk management:   The country office performs fraud risk assessment s as part of  the annual risk assessment. The risk register updated in October 2025 identified three  fraud-related risks linked to implementing partners, procurement process, and diversion of supplies. All three risks were assessed as “very low” despite the operating context, where there is a high perception of corruption – Sri Lanka is ranked 121 out of 180 3   (down  two positions from 2023). Additionally, the audit team identified gaps in the due diligence process of vendors. For example, the audit team observed that most of the country office  vendors were not registered in UNGM, which is the database for screening vendors against the ineligibility list. Further, the country   office entered contractual relationships  with some vendors that were “not recommended”   or high financial risk or significant financial risk   by the supply evaluation unit in the earlier years, without adequately documenting the risk mitigation measures.  UNICEF’s 2025 guidance on risk assessment requires offices, at a minimum, to assess risks  across six key risk areas, among them fraud, waste and misuse of resources. Fraud risk management was further reinforced by the UNICEF Policy on Anti-Fraud and Corruption,  which was effective from 11 April 2025. This policy requires country offices to conduct  3   https://www.transparency.org/en/countries/sri-lanka

14  fraud and corruption risk assessments annually. The audit noted that the country o ffice’s fraud risk assessment did not consider some “red flags” indicative of wastage of resources  and their impact on   country office   programmes and operations. For example, the audit  team noted inefficiencies delaying the customs clearance process (see the audit  observation on supply chain management); however, delays in programme implementation and non-receipt of supplies on a timely basis by the end users due to the delayed customs clearance were not considered in the fraud risk assessment.  The absence of a comprehensive fraud risk assessment limited the country o ffice’s ability to ensure establishment and monitoring of effective mitigating actions. The fraud risk  management framework lacked key elements such as a comprehensive, documented fraud risk assessment, and anti-fraud (risk mitigation) action plans to address the  identified risks.   The country office indicated that it plans to conduct its first annual fraud  and corruption risk assessment in 2026 as required by the new policy.  Root cause: This was mainly due to the methodology used by the country   office in  determining the severity of fraud risks. The methodology only focused on quantitative  data regarding the historical number of reported cases and the financial value. In the case  of Sri Lanka, no fraud cases were reported   by the country office , hence the "low" risk rating. However, this methodology failed to consider the qualitative aspects, such the Corruption Perceptions I ndex, and other fraud red flags.  Reporting suspicions of fraud: According to the UNICEF Policy on Anti-Fraud and Corruption, which applies to all s taff,   a ffiliate   personnel, implementing partners and vendors, it is mandatory to report reasonable fraud suspicions to UNICEF OIAI through the email hotline. The two implementing partners interviewed by the audit team indicated that they were not aware of the reporting requirements. The audit team reviewed the HACT training materials used for building the capacity of implementing partners and noted that they did not include fraud matters, including the reporting channels. The  UNICEF Regional Office for South Asia ( ROSA) peer review also noted that the country  office had not provided anti -fraud training to all implementing partners, and anti-fraud sensitization materials, such as posters, were not visible in UNICEF or implementing  partner’s offices. Limited identification   and inappropriate handling of allegations may result in fraud going undetected, potential   financial   loss due to an inability to recover any misappropriations and increased reputational risk with donors globally.  Root cause: This was due to lack of adequate awareness raising measures since the  country office’s implementing partners were inadequately trained   in   fraud identification  and reporting.  Regular monitoring of risks: The CMT terms of reference requires the consideration of risk management issues and provision of oversight on internal control. In addition, the  CMT is to regularly review identified risk areas and implementation of risk mitigation

15  measures. The audit team reviewed CMT meeting minutes from 2024 and 2025. None of  the reviewed CMT meeting minutes from 2024 displayed evidence of specific discussions  on risk management nor concerning implementing risk mitigation measures .  In 2025, management formed a CMT working group to review and monitor the risks and mitigation measures to be reported by   the CMT on a quarterly basis to address specific  risks. There was some improvement in the monitoring process through presentations to CMT of the key performance indicators (KPI), which include risk management as one of the performance areas. The audit team discussed the risk monitoring process and was informed that beginning September 2025, there were periodic updates to the CMT on risk management. This was evident from the October meeting where the risk register was circulated among CMT members soliciting their feedback, which led to an update in the risk register in October 2025.  Root cause: Gaps in regular monitoring of risks were due to competing priorities and lack of adequate risk management capacity in the country   office. Protection from Sexual Exploitation and Abuse   Medium  At the time of the audit,   the country office   was in the process of implementing various PSEA-related actions, including community consultations to identify reporting barriers, development and display of SEA reporting materials at programme sites, PSEA sessions for hiring managers, and communication to CSOs on focal points and resources. There were delays in the implementation of some planned activities which   affected the full rollout of  UNICEF’s safeguarding standards. There was limited community awareness on safe reporting mechanisms, increasing the risk of SEA allegations going unreported. Additionally, gaps were noted in CSO PSEA capacity assessments, where positive scores were assigned without supporting documentation and capacity strengthening improvement plans for medium-capacity partners which were overdue.  The country office had a comprehensive PSEA action plan in place.   It has recognized the importance of strengthening national systems to prevent and address child abuse and violence against children, as well as building accountable frameworks, as part of key programme priorities during 2025, and moving forward. The implementation of the PSEA action plan was overseen by cross-sectoral HACT focal points and a PSEA taskforce that was chaired by the UNICEF representative. PSEA was also a standing agenda in the CMT  meetings. The audit team reviewed SEA risk assessments in the country office, PSEA in  partnerships and availability of safe and accessible reporting. The following areas of  improvement were identified.  Implementation of the PSEA action plan: Most of the activities in the action plan were being implemented or ongoing, albeit with delays in implementation. Examples include:  •   Organizing consultations with communities (including men, women, children, children with disability) to explore possible barriers for reporting SEA cases and design appropriate recommendations for improving SEA reporting mechanisms.  •   Supporting partners in developing information materials for beneficiaries and staff of SEA reporting and ensure that all programme sites display them.  •   Conduct a session with hiring managers on PSEA and child safeguarding during recruitment.  •   Drafting a circular to inform CSOs on availability of materials and names, contact information and functions of UNICEF PSEA and child safeguarding focal persons.  The outstanding activities affected the country office’s core standard indicators which indicated that the office had not fully rolled out the PSEA system. In addition, it led to  some gaps in application of some parts of the UNICEF policy on safeguarding,   specifically  regarding the areas detailed below.

17  •   Supporting vendors to become aware of this policy, and to establish their own safeguarding-related policies and practices that are no less stringent than the provisions in this policy.  •   Informing the communities served and individuals visited and observing programme activities about UNICEF’s safeguarding standards and strengthening community awareness on how to report safeguarding concerns and incidents and provide a safe and accessible way to report such concerns. The audit was informed, during a field visit to a province, that the provincial government was seeking help from the country office to better identify violence against children. The country office confirmed that the government’s channels for identifying and responding to violence are indeed weak. Therefore, when SEA cases need to be referred to national child abuse mechanisms, the capacity of such mechanisms to adequately respond may be also limited. This has a risk that SEA allegations and/or incidents may not be noted and may go unaddressed. The country office indicated that several provinces and national governments are currently benefitting from UNICEF’s support to strengthen respective systems.  The above two areas were included in the PSEA action plan, and the most recent taskforce meeting recognized the need to expedite implementation of the outstanding activities. The PSEA taskforce met at the end of October 2025, reviewed the progress of implementation of the action plan, and assigned responsibilities and timelines for  implementation of the pending activities, with specific deadlines.  Root cause: The delays in implementation of the action plan activities were attributed to other competing priorities and a non-functional UN interagency PSEA network which hindered the implementation of the interagency activities.  PSEA in PD development process: Some areas of improvement in the PD development process related to integrating SEA mitigating actions in PDs signed with medium-capacity  partners, and identification of PDs with a procurement component. Per UNICEF’s  programme implementation handbook, the strategy section of the PD addresses, among other things, any associated risks including SEA risks. In a sample of six PDs signed with medium-capacity CSOs, the audit noted that SEA risks and mitigating measures were only included in three. This inconsistent application may result in unmitigated SEA risks that could bring harm to children and bring reputational damage due to unmet expectations.  Root cause: This was caused by lack of awareness of PSEA standards relating to integrating SEA mitigating risks into the PDs of medium-capacity partners.  CSO capacity assessments: A capacity assessment on PSEA is conducted for all prospective civil society partners before the partnership is established through a partnership agreement and signed PD. The audit team reviewed the quality of PSEA  assessments conducted during the audit period. In five of seven CSO assessments

18  reviewed, positive scores were assigned without attaching the necessary documentation to support the score, indicating quality concerns in the PSEA assessment . The country  office has been progressively trying to enhance the rigor with which the PSEA assessors  are undertaking the assessments. In addition, the audit noted that capacity strengthening improvement plans for seven medium-capacity partners were overdue, i.e. exceeded the seven-month threshold. Under the HACT   framework, UNICEF country offices must conduct assurance activities,  such as programme visits, spot checks, and audits, to ensure implementing partners' proper use of cash transfers. The framework also mandates timely, complete, and accurate follow-up on   financial findings from these assurance activities. The OIAI team assessed the country office’s process of managing HACT financial assurance activities and identified the following areas of  improvement:  Testing coverage in spot checks and assurance Level: The HACT framework and UNICEF’s spot check guidance emphasize spot checks as one of the essential pillars of UNICEF’s partnership risk management. The number of spot checks conducted annually for each implementing partner is determined automatically and populated into eTools for all partners that have liquidated expenditures equivalent to US$50,000 per year (October  of previous year to September of current year). However, country offices can conduct  additional follow-up spot checks beyond the minimum requirements, prompted by the country context and risk factors. The low level of testing relative to total expenditure limits the level of assurance that can be derived from spot checks, potentially allowing material misstatements, misuse, or unintentional errors to go undetected. It also weakens the country o ffice’s ability to  demonstrate adequate stewardship of resources.  Root cause: The low testing coverage is due to a focus on meeting the minimum requirements as per UNICEF guidelines rather than ensuring coverage of total expenditures, creating a compliance mindset as opposed to a risk-based approach. Further, the fragmentation of cash transfers to some implementing partners through low- value FACE forms contributed to this issue.

34  Definition s   of Audit   Observation   Ratings  To assist management in prioritizing the actions arising from the audit, OIAI ascribes a rating to each audit observation based on the potential consequence or residual risks to the audited entity, area, activity or process or to UNICEF as a whole. Individual observations are rated as follows:  Low  The observation concerns   a potential opportunity for improvement in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes. Low- priority observations are reported to management during the audit but are not included in the audit report. Action in response to the observation is desirable.  Medium  The observation relates to a weakness or deficiency in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes that requires resolution within a reasonable period to avoid adverse consequences for the audited entity, area, activity or process.  High  The observation concerns a fundamental weakness or deficiency in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes that requires prompt/immediate resolution to avoid severe/major adverse consequences for the audited entity, area, activity or process, or for UNICEF as a whole.  Definitions of Overall Audit Conclusions  The above ratings of audit observations are then used to support an overall audit conclusion for the area under review, as follows:  Satisfactory   The assessed governance, risk management, and control processes were adequate and functioning well.  Partially  Satisfactory,  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management, and control processes were generally adequate and functioning but needed improvement. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified were unlikely to have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  Partially  Satisfactory,  Major  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management, or control processes needed major improvement. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified could have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  Unsatisfactory  The assessed governance, risk management or control processes were not adequately established or did not function well. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified could have a severely negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  APPENDIX
