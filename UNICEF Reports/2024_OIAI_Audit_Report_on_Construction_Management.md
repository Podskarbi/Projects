EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  3  The Office of Internal Audit   and Investigations (OIAI)   conducted an audit   of   c onstruction  m anagement   process es   in   eight   UNICEF country offices   during   the period from   September 2022  to   Ju ne   2023 .   Each   audit   was conducted in   conformance   with   the Code of Ethics and   the  International Standards for the Professional Practice of Internal Auditing.   The overarching  objective of the audit s   was to   assess the adequacy and effectiveness   of the governance, risk  management ,   and control processes   across   a selection of risk areas   related to   UNICEF’s  construction   activities .   The descriptions of the specific risks evaluated are provided in the Audit  Objective s , Scope ,   and Approach Section   of this report .   This report summarizes the most  significant results f rom the audit s   of   construction management ,   highlights   trends and/or systemic  issues   identified   from a review of the results of those   audits   and recommends actions to improve  UNICE F’s   management   of construction activities   organization - wide .  In recent years,   there has been   a   significant increase   in construction activit y .   According to the  available data, the total value of construction   projects   implemented directly 1   by UNICEF   grew  from US$ 1 18.3   million in 2018 to   almost   US $ 3 00   million in 2022.   Construction projects   are often  inherently risky, due to   the   high   cost ,   complexity,   and   involvement of multiple actors.   S trong  coordinat ion   and   contract   manage ment   is required   to mitigate the risk of   substandard work   and  potentially severe   reputational   consequences for UNICEF .   The audit s   focused on   the risks related  to   the   capacity of   country offices   to manage construction   work s ;   the   selection   of   implementation  modalities and   management of   contractual   arrangements ;   project   monitoring and oversight ;  financial and legal   indemnification ;   and long - term sustainability of construction outputs .  Overall Conclusion  Based   on   the   audit   work   performed,   OIAI  concluded that the assessed governance, risk  management,   or   control   processes   were  Partially Satisfactory, Improvement Needed ,  meaning   they   were   generally   adequate   and  functioning, but needed some improvement. The  weaknesses   or   deficiencies   identified   were  unlikely to have a materially negative impact on  the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process. C ONTEXT  7  C onstruction   activity at UNICEF  Construction projects in UNICEF are   undertaken   to support the achievement   of   programmatic  objectives .   In   UNICEF’s   context,   construction   includes   all   activities   associated   with   the  construction ;   reconstruction ;   retrofitting ;   demolition ;   repair or renovation   of buildings or structures ;  and   activities such as site preparation, foundation, erection, building, installation of materials and  finishing.   Construction   o utputs   typically include schools, latrines, hand - washing facilities, health  facilities, as well as offices,   warehouses,   and guesthouses .   E mergency construction work  compris es   temporary and semi - permanent structures.  While construction at UNICEF is primarily a lo cal activity   undertaken by c ountry offices ,   the  Construction Unit within   UNICEF   Supply Division   provides   guidance and   support on planning,  procurement and execution of construction and rehabilitation works through development of  procedures,   guidelines,   and   tools. It also performs   consultations, process es   local procurement  authorizations, conduct s   field missions and provid es   training and advice   to country offices .  Regional offices also provide support and oversight, mainly in relation to   procurement.  Construction management   procedures and   guidance  In July 2022,   UNICEF   Supply Division issued a new   Procedure on Construction   Projects , which  requires that   engage ment   in construction projects   only   advance   whe n   the following conditions are  met : (i)   programme   goals are clearly established with the   h ost   g overnment, or construction is  required to establish or expand UNICEF operations; (ii) UNICEF has access to the nece ssary  funding; (iii) UNICEF has allocated appropriate in - house capacity to support technical and project  planning, procurement, implementation, and oversight of the project; and (iv) the level of risk is  acceptable.   This procedure , which   applies to   country ,   regional ,   and   headquarter   offices,   outlines  the   requirements   for   planning   construction   projects   and   managing   the   related   contract ual  arrangements .   While   the   procurement   of   construction   projects   follows   UNICEF’s   general  procurement procedures   a nd guidance on supply, specific planning is needed to   mitigate the  particular   risks related to construction projects, which can involve significant expenditure and  multiple   internal   and   third - party   actors.   This   requires   sufficient   capacity   for   construction ,  supervision,   and oversight, to   be able to   coordinate multiple stakeholders and select the  appropriate implementation modality and contractual arrangement.  UNICEF   construction projects   generally   involve   collaborati on between the   country office, host  country   government   stakeholders ,   implementing   partners   such   as   non - governmental  organizations   ( NGOs ) , contractors including engineering and quality assurance firms, and local  communities.   UNICEF   uses   a three - party approach   a s follows:  •   C ountry offices are responsible for planning, securing funding, procurement of contractors  or selection of implementing partners (C ivil   S ociety   O rganization s   (CSOs) , government  entities or U nited Nations or ganizations), managing contracts and agreements, payments,  coordination, stakeholder management and   oversight.  •   C ontractors   or   implementing   partner s   are   responsible   for   execution   of   works   in  accordance with the signed contract or agreement .  •   The entity provi ding engineering services (engineering firm or a government entity) is  responsible for the assigned engineering services such as site assessment, design, and  site supervision, in accordance with the signed contract or agreement.

Planning of construction activities   Medium  At the   country   office   level,   there was a need for   enhanced guidance   on the   development of a  robust business case for each construction project ,   to mitigate the risk of   undertaking construction  activities that do not   materially   contribute to programme goals.   A t the corpor ate level,   there was  a   need for the   establishment of clear accountabilities for oversight of   the management of   strategic  and   programmatic risk s   related to construction activities .  1.1   Business case and planning for country office construction projects  The significant growth in the number and value of construction projects in recent years increases  the need to ensure that local construction activities are   adequately   planned, resourced an d  managed to   maximize their contribution to   the   achievement of   the   organization’s   strategic  objectives within clearly defined risk tolerance limits. UNICEF’s Procedure on Construction  Projects requires, inter alia, that offices only engage in construction   projects if   programme goals  are clearly established with the host government, or construction is required to   establish or  expand UNICEF   operations . However,   the   audit   found that,   in some country offices ,   decisions to  undertake   construction projects w ere   sometimes driven by the availability of donor funding or  government   requirements, rather than by specific programmatic needs.   This may lead to  misalignment between construction projects and programmatic goals, as well as operational  challenges due to a lac k of forward   planning and longer - term capacity   building.  While providing basic criteria for co untry office engagement in construction, existing guidance  focuses on the technical aspects of construction management. It is not sufficient to support  country   offices in developing a comprehensive business case for construction projects and  detailed planning, including a thorough risk assessment, clear identification   of resource needs,  and a realistic timeline that factors in the potential hurdles of constructio n management. There is  therefore an increased risk that country office management may not fully grasp the specificities,  risks, and complexities of construction projects. As highlighted by several country office audit  findings, this may result in project d elays and gaps in monitoring, leading to potential cost  overruns, sub - optimal construction quality and structures that do not align with or support long -  term community needs.  1.2   Strategic positioning of UNICEF’s construction activities  UNICEF is highly dece ntralized,   allowing   significant autonomy   f o r   country offices in   determining  and executing   their   programmatic activities, including construction projects. At   the h eadquarter  level ,   the   Supply Division is the lead for UNICEF’s construction - related procuremen t, developing  technical procedures, guidance and tools for project management and risk mitigation. However,  OIAI was unable to identify an equivalent lead or broader framework that set out UNICEF’s overall  approach to managing the strategic and programmati c risks and opportunities associated with  construction projects. OIAI noted that there was minimal involvement from other organizational  actors in areas which may be important to construction management, such as strategic planning  and risk assessment, dono r relations, fundraising, capacity building, and policy development.  Some issues identified in country office audits   –   including capacity constraints, delays in  implementation, and unclear sustainability strategies   –   can be at least partially attributed to   the  lack of clear strategic and programmatic direction.  In the   absence of a clearly communicated global strategy or   framework   for construction, there is  a risk that construction activities at the country office level may not be aligned with the

12  organization’s risk appetite or long - term goals. Furthermore, without appropriate supporting  structures and resources at   the country and   regional levels, and adequate oversight at the  headquarters level, risks and challenges at country office level are less likely to be identified and  managed, despite the existence of technical tools and guidance, exposing UNICEF to reputational  risk and r educed levels of trust and credibility among stakeholders and beneficiaries. Risks associated with procurement and contracting in construction projects are often inherently  high, due to the significant cos ts involved and operational complexities. Typical risks include  collusion between bidders to fix prices, submission of inaccurate or misleading bid information,  and post - award additions or alterations to contract terms. Direct implementation of construction projects follows UNICEF ’s   supply   procedures and guidance  more generally , with spec ific   contract templates d epending on the value of the construction works.  Due to the high risk involved in construction projects, bank guarantees   or other forms of   insurance  are   required ,   for example,   performance security, advance payment guarantee, insura nce against  all risks, and retention money security during the defects liability period.  Five of the   eight   country office   audits identified issues   related to   construction procurement and  contracting. In   one   country office , the audit identified   several weaknesses in contract ing ,   rang ing  from mismatches between technical and financial analyses to the bid not being awarded to the  best bidder ,   due to criteria   being added to   the approved   terms of reference   after selecting the  bidder . The   country   offi ce frequently used limited tendering for speed, lacked formal bid opening  records, and did   not   inform   all   vendors of tender results, raising   concerns about   transparency.   In  addition , anomalies were found in   purchase orders , including incorrect defect   liability periods and  inaccurate amounts.  A nother   country office   audit   noted   failure   to assess the capacity of partners   for   significant   indirect  implementation   projects and   to   review   partners’   vendor selection processes.   I n   a   third   country  office ,   the   aud it   found   that   the   government   requested   significant   adjustments   to   project  specifications   after   contract issuance ,   leading to increased costs and delays. S ustainability   of construct ed facilities   Medium  The   country office   audits   highlighted   risks   related to   the   maintenance   and   sustainability   of  construction   outputs   once handed over to   the   local authorities and/or   beneficiaries. To address  these challenges, UNICEF should enhance its policies by focusing on comprehensive project  sus tainability and community engagement, including robust long - term maintenance planning and  effective communication strategies   to mitigate risks to the organization's reputation, resource  allocation ,   and community relationships .  UNICE F’s P rocedure   on Constr uction Projects and related   guidance highlight the   importance of  project completion and handover. Works are deemed substantially complete when ready for  operation,   at   which   point   the   contractor   and   the   country   office   should   provid e   essential  maintenance in formation. A defect liability period follows, typically lasting 6 - 12 months, during  which the contractor   is expected to   address any defects or substandard work. For contract  closure, a sustainability plan is   required,   to detail maintenance and upkeep requirements. If the  end - user is a community or government entity, they are encouraged to draft a maintenance plan,  securing   funding   and   appointing   maintenance   personnel.   A   completion   report   capturing  achievements and less ons learned is also recommended, ensuring the project's longevity and  effectiveness.  In three of the audited country offices,   significant   issues   were   identified. In   one   case ,   substandard  construction projects   appeared to have been   left   unused ,   due to   conc erns raised by   the  beneficiary community   about   the   quality and security of the structures. In   two   country   offices , local  authorities, communities, and beneficiaries expressed concerns   about   the maintenance and long -  term sustainability of the constructed facilities, citing a lack of allocated funding for upkeep.   I n   one  of those offices , beneficiaries seemed unaware of the mechanisms for   reporting   issues with  recently handed - over buildings, an d   the audit team noted   significant maintenance needs in  structures handed over four years ago, with local authorities acknowledging the absence of  funding for maintenance.  The issues highlighted by the audits carry broad - ranging risks and consequences for   UNICEF.  S ub standard   construction   works may   pose a   safety   risk to   beneficiaries   and , by extension,   to  UNICEF’s   reputation.   U nused   structures   also re present   a waste of resources, undermining the  organization's credibility   amongst donors , communities   and other key stakeholders   and er oding  trust and   support for future initiatives.   T he absence of allocated funds for maintenance in certain  locations   creates   the   risk   of   premature   deterioration   of   infrastructure   and   may   lead   to  dissatisfaction among the af fected communities.  OIAI r ecognize s   that ,   upon handover of constructions to local authorities, there is a shift in direct  control over the buildings. Nevertheless,   the audits indicated that there may be   deficiencies in  capacity   building and communication   strategies aimed at equipping local stakeholders with the  necessary skills and knowledge to manage and maintain the   constructions . Furthermore, there  appeared   to be   limited   consideration and communication   in the audited country offices about   long -  term main tenance and sustainability during the planning and development phases of the projects .
