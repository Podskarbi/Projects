EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  3  The Office of Internal Audit and Investigations (OIAI) conducted an audit of the Chad Country Office, covering the period from January 2024 to September 2025. The audit was conducted from 15 to 26 September 2025 in conformance with the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global Internal Audit Standards. The objective of the audit was to assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the governance, risk management and control processes related to a selection of significant risk areas of the Chad Country Office, including the control environment, programme implementation and monitoring, and financial assurance. The specific risks evaluated are set out in the Audit Objectives, Scope and Approach section of this report.  Between January 2024 and June 2025, supplies represented the largest cost category for the Chad Country Office, accounting for 26.3 per cent (US$42 million) of total utilization. Within this, nutrition-related supplies made up 41.5 per cent (US$17.5 million) and education related supplies accounted for 14.3 per cent (US$6 million).  The Chad Country Office transferred a total of US$38.4 million to implementing partners. Of this amount, US$23.6 million (61.45 per cent) was allocated to government partners, while US$14.8 million (38.55 per cent) went to civil society organizations (CSOs). The majority of these transfers – 94.6 per cent – were made through direct cash transfer (DCT) advance payments, with 4.9 per cent processed via reimbursement and only 0.5 per cent through direct payments. More than US$19.7 million (51 per cent of funds transferred) was transferred to partners classified as high risk. AUDIT OBJECTIVES ,   SCOPE   AND APPROACH  9  Chad is a landlocked country in Central Africa. Approximately 76 per cent of the population resides in rural areas, which presents significant challenges for accessing basic services and infrastructure. Chad ranks 189 out of 191 on the Human Development Index, with 44.8 per cent of its population living below the national poverty line. The country continues to face significant challenges in achieving progress towards the Sustainable Development Goals, with major gaps remaining across most indicators.  The country faces recurring multidimensional and complex rapid-onset and protracted humanitarian crises, including food insecurity, health emergencies and natural disasters; the presence of non-state armed groups in the Lake Chad Basin; and the refugee crisis in the Eastern Chad provinces. Climate change has exacerbated these issues, with frequent floods and droughts impacting livelihoods and infrastructure. Chad is ranked second globally in terms of climate change impact on children.  Chad is the fifth-largest refugee-hosting country in the world, with over 1.5 million refugees and 394,317 returnees from Sudan, which has further strained resources. Security remains a major concern, particularly in the Lake Chad Basin, where non-state armed groups operate. Despite its socio-economic challenges, Chad plays a stabilizing role in the region due to its relative stability compared to neighbouring countries.  The 2024–2026 Country Programme Document (CPD) is aligned with Chad’s National Health Development Plan (2024–2026), focusing on child survival, nutrition, community health and child-sensitive budgeting. It has a total budget of US$216.2 million, of which US$58.5 million is for regular resources and US$157.7 million for other resources (OR). This shows a strong reliance on OR funding.  The review of the programme outcomes/outputs and budget information in the CPD and the 2024/ 2025 Chad Humanitarian Appeal highlight the key programmatic focus. OBSERVATIONS AND MANAGEMENT ACTION PLAN  12  The key areas where actions are needed are summarized below.  1.   Oversight and accountability   Medium  The Chad Country Office has faced vacancies in critical leadership positions, which were addressed through interim appointments of officers in charge. These gaps could have an impact on programme delivery.  The Country Office has established governance structures and annual management plans; however, the following were noted in terms of arrangements in place:  -   Extended vacancies – such as chief of education (21 months) – combined with rest and recuperation cycles and interim arrangements could result in ad hoc decisions on higher-risk matters without proper documentation or strategic review.  -   Accountability gaps were evident in annual management plans, which included delegation tables but did not define responsibilities or escalation thresholds for interim staff. As a result, critical issues such as lease termination and teacher compensation were handled without structured oversight, leaving legal and financial risks unrecorded in the risk register, increasing the risk of exposure to unauthorized transactions and reputational damage.  -   Governance bodies functioned but lacked integration. The Country Management Team (CMT) met regularly, and reviewed budgets and performance yet did not systematically oversee risk management or statutory committees. Significant risks – estimated at over US$2.2 million – were neither escalated nor documented, limiting the CMT’s ability to provide timely guidance. Similarly, the Contract Review Committee and Property Survey Board operated without adequate follow-up or reporting to the CMT. Mandatory procurement training was incomplete for Contract Review Committee members, and expired vehicle loan agreements (23 vehicles, some dating back to 2010) remained unresolved, as there was no documentation on steps taken to recover or donate those assets, exposing the office to potential asset loss.  Root causes: These issues are largely due to unclear interim authority, lack of a comprehensive understanding of risk management and documentation of all underlying risks, as well as lack of clarity due to the pending operationalization of UNICEF’s risk framework.

Fraud risk and risk management   High  Governance, risk and compliance processes in the Chad Country Office require significant strengthening to address vulnerabilities in fraud prevention, risk management and internal controls. These improvements are essential to ensure effective decision-making, accountability and beneficiary safety and highlight the need for stronger governance and integrated risk management across all operational areas.  The Country Office operates in a complex environment with heightened risks related to the operational context. UNICEF applies a zero-tolerance policy on fraud, misconduct and corruption. Policies require robust governance and risk management frameworks. The audit noted the following:  Fraud risk management: Despite technical support from the West and Central Africa Regional Office in October 2024, including training for suppliers and partners and a fraud vulnerability assessment, the office’s anti-fraud strategy (January 2025) did not incorporate the Regional Office’s recommendations and was not referenced in the 2025 Annual Management Plan (AMP). Fraud prevention activities were absent from AMP priorities and indicators. A fraud register was not established.  Controls over high-risk functions, such as asset management, cash handling and RUTF distribution, were insufficient. Leakage of RUTF persisted, and detection and reporting

14  mechanisms were limited. These gaps expose the office to undetected fraud, resource diversion and reputational harm, undermining UNICEF’s zero-tolerance policy and accountability standards.  Risk management: UNICEF’s risk management framework requires a comprehensive, regularly updated risk register integrated into the AMP and reviewed by the Country Management Team. While a risk register was prepared, it lacked key components such as risk factors, mitigation measures, effectiveness ratings and action owners. Emerging risks were not consistently updated, and separate monitoring of RUTF supply chain risks resulted in fragmented oversight. Incomplete documentation limits management’s ability to identify, monitor and mitigate risks effectively, increasing exposure to operational disruptions, financial loss and reputational damage.  Internal controls: The 2024 annual internal control self-assessment was incomplete. Planned administrative self-assessment for Q2 2024 was not conducted. According to the 2024 internal control questionnaire submitted to the comptroller, the office had established a task force to examine ineligible expenditures; this was confirmed by the audit. Additionally, high-risk functions such as assets, fuel management and airtime allocation lacked formalized risk and control procedures. IPSAS compliance was compromised by using job orders instead of recording commitments in VISION. In the absence of structured risk-control assessments and related standard procedures, the office cannot systematically identify or mitigate risks, increasing the likelihood of inconsistent practices, resource misuse or losses to fraud and reduced accountability.  Root causes: Fraud and risk management frameworks were not fully implemented. In addition, there is a lack of systematic oversight by the former CO management and monitoring mechanisms.

This strategy is implemented through the Country Programme Management Plan, which established four field offices – in Abéché, Bol, Mongo and Moundou – to serve distinct regions and populations. These offices aim to reduce regional disparities, strengthen local governance and improve access to integrated services in both emergency and development contexts.  To support this structure, the Country Office developed an accountability framework in February 2024 to clarify roles and responsibilities between central and field office staff. A subnational retreat was held to coordinate field and emergency operations. However, the following issues were noted:

Strategic funding and structural alignment   Medium  Uncertainties in funding and data inconsistencies may weaken the reliability of staffing plans for the 2026 programme cycle. While efforts were made to align staffing with projected resources, reliance on high-probability income and a few major donors increases financial risk.  In anticipation of the 2026 programme cycle, the Country Office conducted a comprehensive affordability analysis in May 2025, which informed the June Programme Budget Review submission under UNICEF’s Future Focus Initiative. This analysis aimed to align staffing costs with reliable funding sources to ensure sustainability and minimize operational disruptions.  The audit noted a projected 11 per cent reduction in overall salary costs compared to 2025, reflecting a strong effort to align staffing with available resources. However, the 2026 funding pipeline includes US$49.1 million in confirmed income and US$45.2 million in high-probability income, introducing uncertainty in salary affordability, particularly for OR-funded positions. In addition, the 2026 proforma staff costs total US$19.9million, with

19  OR and OR emergency contributing US$9.4 million and US$1.2 million, respectively. Meanwhile, regular resources-funded staff costs are projected to decrease from US$9.4 million in 2025 to US$7.2 million in 2026, a shortfall of US$2.2 million in regular resources allocation. This shift in funding sources increases dependency and may result in funding gaps and affect staff continuity if expected income is not secured.  The June 2025 PBR approved several structural changes, including post abolitions and multi-country roles, scheduled for implementation in January 2026. However, the new Country Programme Document taking effect in 2027 is not yet developed, which could lead to a misalignment between the proposed staffing structure and future CPD priorities and may result in resource inefficiencies or delays in restructuring, especially if the proposed structure does not support future CPD priorities.  The audit identified inconsistencies between Integrated Budget Costing System data and PBR organigrams, including misclassified or missing positions.  Root Cause(s): These discrepancies stem from the absence of a standardized process for cross-verifying human resources data with planning and budgeting submissions.  Such inconsistencies could undermine the reliability of staffing and budget analysis, increasing the risk of misinformed decisions and compliance issues. With the support of the Regional Office, establish a formal transitional planning  process to align structural decisions with evolving strategic frameworks.  iii.   Implement a cross-verification protocol across human resources, planning and  budgeting systems, seeking assistance from the Regional Office. Root causes: The challenges affecting programme monitoring and financial assurance in the Country Office stemmed from lack of managerial oversight to ensure processes and procedures are followed, limited staff capacity and training, delays and incomplete execution of financial assurance activities, poor planning and documentation of programmatic visits – including AAP and PSEA assessments – and inadequate end-user monitoring of supplies. Plan programmatic visits to align with activity timelines, include mandatory AAP  and PSEA assessments, and record all visits in eTools with supporting evidence.  iii. 37  Definition s   of Audit   Observation   Ratings  To assist management in prioritizing the actions arising from the audit, OIAI ascribes a rating to each audit observation based on the potential consequence or residual risks to the audited entity, area, activity, or process or to UNICEF as a whole. Individual observations are rated as follows:  Low  The observation concerns a potential opportunity for improvement in the assessed governance, risk management, or control processes. Low-priority observations are reported to management during the audit but are not included in the audit report. Action in response to the observation is desirable.  Medium  The observation relates to a weakness or deficiency in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes that requires resolution within a reasonable period to avoid adverse consequences for the audited entity, area, activity, or process.  High  The observation concerns a fundamental weakness or deficiency in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes that requires prompt/immediate resolution to avoid severe/major adverse consequences for the audited entity, area, activity, or process, or for UNICEF as a whole.  Definitions of Overall Audit Conclusions  The above ratings of audit observations are then used to support an overall audit conclusion for the area under review, as follows:  Satisfactory   The assessed governance, risk management, and control processes were adequate and functioning well.  Partially  Satisfactory,  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management, and control processes were generally adequate and functioning but needed improvement. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified were unlikely to have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity, or process.  Partially  Satisfactory,  Major  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management, or control processes needed major improvement. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified could have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  Unsatisfactory  The assessed governance, risk management or control processes were not adequately established or did not function well. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified could have a severely negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  APPENDIX
