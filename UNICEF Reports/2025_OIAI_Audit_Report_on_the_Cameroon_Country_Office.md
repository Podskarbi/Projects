EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  3  The Office of Internal Audit and Investigations (OIAI) conducted an audit of the UNICEF Cameroon Country Office (CCO), covering the period from January 2023 to June 2025. The audit was conducted from May 2025 to June 2025 in conformance with the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global Internal Audit Standards. The objective of the audit was to   assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the governance, risk management, and control processes related to a selection of significant risk   areas   of the CCO.   The specific risks evaluated are set out in the Audit Objective, Scope and Approach section of this report.  The CCO works with government and civil society organizations (CSOs) to implement planned   programme   activities.   During   the   audited   period,   the   CCO   transferred approximately US$40 million in cash and US$19 million in supplies to implementing partners, representing 58 per cent of expenditure. There were several risks related to partnerships and cash transfers to implementing partners. The audit sought to determine whether and how the CCO managed those risks.  Overall Conclusion  Based   on   the   audit   work   performed,   OIAI concluded that the assessed governance, risk management,   or   control   processes   were partially   satisfactory,   major   improvement needed,   meaning   that   the   assessed governance,   risk   management,   or   control processes needed major improvement. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified   could have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  Summary of Observations and Agreed Actions  Since the previous OIAI audit in 2022, the CCO demonstrated progress in several key areas. By developing resource mobilization strategies for both institutional and private sector funding, the CCO increased mobilization of funds through the retention of existing donors and the addition of new donors to the donor base. Steps were also taken to strengthen harmonized approach to cash transfers (HACT) assurance, including establishing a HACT Committee to oversee the quality assurance of all HACT assurance activities, provide guidance on HACT-related oversight, and monitor the performance of staff and service providers, as well as compliance with targets. In January 2025, the eTools field monitoring module was adopted by the CCO and is expected to further improve the reporting and quality   assurance   of   programme   monitoring,   supporting   decision-making   and accountability to   programme   stakeholders. 4  During   the   audit,   OIAI   observed   certain   operational   challenges   related   to   internal communication and collaboration, and other people-related issues. These posed risks to the overall office performance, if not adequately managed. The CCO acknowledged these issues and, at the time of the audit, was actively working to address them, in partnership with the UNICEF West and Central Africa Regional Office. As mitigation efforts are ongoing, these matters are not detailed in the current report; however, staff performance management and related topics may be considered as potential areas for review by OIAI in 2026.  The audit team made several   observations   related to the management of the key risks  evaluated. In particular, OIAI noted:  •   Resource   m obilization:   While the CCO had a comprehensive fundraising strategy and some activity tracking tools, there was a need for a comprehensive, accessible system that included information on planned initiatives, results of completed initiatives, and continuous evaluation of fundraising activities, to ensure that the CCO focuses its resource mobilization efforts on those opportunities most likely to generate positive funding outcomes.  •   Partnerships   m anagement : The CCO lacked an overarching partnership strategy that was aligned with its internal capacity to fulfil assurance, monitoring, and partner capacity building requirements. This resulted in a large, diverse partner base, that created internal coordination and capacity challenges, and contributed to weaknesses in cash transfer, HACT assurance, and   programme   monitoring activities. There was also a need to improve staff and partner use of UNICEF’s partner selection and management tools, and to strengthen planning and oversight activities, to ensure the timely, accurate completion of   programme   documents (PDs)/simplified   programme   documents (SPDs) and monitoring of partner progress.  •   Programme   m onitoring:   The lack of an office-wide monitoring plan, coupled with inadequate coordination and oversight of monitoring activities, resulted in insufficient programmatic monitoring coverage during the audited period. In addition, the CCO did not have a robust quality assurance mechanism for programmatic monitoring and reporting, which led to inconsistent coverage, for example, of protection from sexual exploitation and abuse (PSEA), supply end user monitoring (SEUM), and suboptimal reporting, hampering efforts to improve service delivery. In January 2025, the CCO fully adopted the eTools field monitoring module, which was expected to strengthen the reporting and quality assurance of   programme   monitoring.  •   Cash   t ransfers to   i mplementing   p artners :   The limited availability of partners to conduct work for specific outcomes and in certain locations, combined with outdated partner risk profiles, led to the use of the direct cash transfer (DCT) modality to transfer cash to high-risk partners. Several DCTs were outstanding for over six months, and cash transfer processing was delayed due to late submission of supporting documentation

5  by partners, inadequate planning by various sections, and the protracted completion of  PDs .  •   HACT   a ssurance :   Since the previous OIAI audit, the CCO had taken steps to strengthen HACT   assurance,   with   a   focus   on   internal   capacity   and   oversight.   While   many improvements were noted, full implementation of all necessary measures was impeded by resource constraints. The recent programme and budget review (PBR) included plans to build capacity to accelerate implementation of sustainable improvements in the oversight of funds disbursed to partners and timely resolution of identified issues.  •   Supply   p lanning,   d istribution and   w arehousing :   The CCO had not conducted logistics capacity assessments for key implementing partners that had received significant volumes of supplies, resulting in a lack of assurance of the partners’ capacity to receive, store, and distribute UNICEF supplies. Additionally, there were gaps in SEUM, which meant   that   the   CCO   could   not   determine   whether   the   supplies   provided   to implementing partners had reached the intended beneficiaries and were of suitable quality and benefit to the end user.  •   Construction :   The CCO did not obtain performance guarantees for construction works and did not exercise sufficient oversight of construction works conducted through CSOs. Some CSO capacity assessments were completed after signing the PDs/SPDs, and there were no risk mitigation actions for CSOs with low or medium construction capacity. In some cases, the value of construction work with CSOs exceeded the maximum allowable amount without exceptional approval. This increased the risk of financial loss, delays and suboptimal construction works.  •   Service   c ontracts :   Contracts were not always promptly closed upon expiry due to delays in pre-closure actions, such as contractor performance evaluations. Several contracts were issued after the contract start date and commencement of services, creating contractual and reputational risks for UNICEF. Other contracts were issued shortly before grant expiry, which may contravene donor agreements and negatively impact donor relations. C ONTEXT  8  Cameroon is a republic in Central Africa. It has an estimated population of 28.4 million, of which children represent 49 per cent. In 2024, the country ranked 155 out of 193 countries on the Human Development Index, which measures social and economic development over time. Cameroon ranked 140 out of 180 on the 2024 Transparency International Corruption Index 2   has a high risk of sexual exploitation and abuse (SEA) according to the 2025 SEARO Global Index 3   .  Cameroon faced persistent humanitarian crises, driven by multiple factors, including conflict in some parts of the country, intercommunal violence, disease outbreaks, and climate shocks, such as severe flooding. These have affected millions, particularly children, and led to widespread displacement and limited access to essential services. Food insecurity and malnutrition are critical concerns, with health, education, protection, and water, hygiene and sanitation (WASH) services significantly disrupted across several regions, particularly in the North-West, South-West, Far North, East, and Adamawa.  Country   programme   document: The Cameroon Country   Programme   Document (CPD) 2022-2026 covers six programmatic areas: Health and human immunodeficiency virus (HIV)/acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS); improving maternal and child nutrition; every child learns; every child lives in a safe and clean environment; every child is protected from violence and exploitation; and every child has access to social protection and lives free from poverty.  UNICEF and its partners provided multisectoral humanitarian response in several parts of the country especially in the North-West and South-West regions and the Far North, including essential health, WASH, nutrition and protection to those affected by protracted crises and also sudden onset emergencies such as the 2024 exceptional floods in the Far North.   Programmatic   activities   included   the   provision   of   lifesaving   severe   acute malnutrition treatment for children in UNICEF-supported health facilities; community-based mental health and psychosocial support; access to formal or non-formal education for children affected by crisis; and safe access to water and sanitation to help mitigate preventable diseases.  The CPD included a proposed aggregate indicative budget of US$58 million from regular resources and US$146 million in other resources. During the period covered by this audit, CPD expenditure totalled US$103 million.  The CCO’s expenditure by   programme   outcome during the audited period is shown below ( see Figure 1). AUDIT OBJECTIVES ,   SCOPE   AND APPROACH  11  The objective of the audit was to   assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the governance, risk management, and control processes related to a selection of significant risk   areas   of the CCO.  The audit scope was determined during the audit planning process based on an assessment of the inherent risks 4   of the CCO,   and included the following areas:  •   Governance  •   Risk management including fraud risk management  •   Resource mobilization  •   PSEA  •   Partnership management  •   Programme monitoring  •   Cash transfers to implementing partners and HACT assurance  •   Cash assistance to beneficiaries  •   Supply planning, distribution and warehousing  •   Construction  •   Service contracts  •   Travel management  The audit fieldwork was conducted in June 2025 in conformance with the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global Internal Audit Standards. For the purposes of audit testing, the audit covered the period from January 2023 to June 2025. The audit involved a combination of methods, tools and techniques, including interviews, data analytics, document review, tests of transactions, evaluations and validation of preliminary observations.  4   Inherent risk refers to the potential adverse event that could occur if management takes no actions, including internal control activities. The higher the likelihood of the event occurring and the more serious the impact would be should the adverse event occur, the stronger the need for adequate and effective risk management and control processes.

OBSERVATIONS AND MANAGEMENT ACTION PLAN  12  The key areas where actions are needed   are summarized   below.  1 .   Resource   mobili z ation   Medium  While the CCO had a comprehensive fundraising strategy and some activity tracking tools, there was a need for a comprehensive, accessible system that included information on planned   initiatives,   results   of   completed   initiatives,   and   continuous   evaluation   of fundraising activities, to ensure that the CCO focuses its resource mobilization efforts on those opportunities most likely to generate positive funding outcomes.  The global reduction in UNICEF funding, combined with underfunded Humanitarian Action for Children (HAC) appeals 5 , required the CCO to put a strong focus on resource mobilization to sustain its activities. The audit assessed the adequacy and effectiveness of the control processes in place to ensure effective and efficient fundraising efforts commensurate with the CCO’s funding needs.  Since the previous OIAI audit in 2022, the CCO had developed resource mobilization strategies for both institutional and private sector funding and undertaken capacity building initiatives. These efforts resulted in increased mobilization of funds, through retention of existing donors and addition of new donors to the donor base.  While   fundraising   depends   on   informed,   well-managed   risk-taking,   a   structured, documented, coordinated approach is required to mitigate the risk of inefficiency and missed funding opportunities. Resource mobilization activities were tracked at a high level in a Microsoft Excel pipeline, but it lacked clear steps, assigned responsibilities, accessibility for all relevant staff, and regular updates on potential opportunities. In addition, the CCO’s approach   to   decision-making   for   resource   mobilization   was   based   on   informal communication rather than a structured evaluation after each activity. A more structured, transparent tracking and monitoring system would enhance strategic decision-making and minimize the risk that resource mobilization goals are not achieved due to delayed or poorly managed pursuit of funding opportunities.  Root cause: Despite having access to the corporate resource mobilization tool, UNISON, the CCO had insufficient licences to be able to leverage its full potential. The above issues were due primarily to the weakness of the alternative tracking mechanisms adopted by the CCO.  5   In 2023, only 15 per cent of the HAC funding target was raised and in 2024, only 35 per cent of the funding target was raised.

Partnership management   Medium  The CCO lacked an overarching partnership strategy that was aligned with its internal capacity to fulfil assurance, monitoring, and partner capacity building requirements. This resulted in a large, diverse partner base, that created internal coordination and capacity challenges,   and   contributed   to   weaknesses   in   cash   transfer,   HACT   assurance   and  programme   monitoring activities.   There was also a need to improve staff and partner use of UNICEF’s partner selection and management tools, and to strengthen planning and oversight activities to ensure the timely, accurate completion of   PDs /SPDs and monitoring of partner progress.  From January 2023 until May 2025, the CCO collaborated with 130 implementing partners who received cash transfers and/or supplies amounting to US$59 million. This included 88 government partners (who received 84 per cent of cash transfers and supplies) and 42 CSO partners (who received 16 per cent of cash transfers and supplies).  The audit assessed whether partner selection processes were guided by clearly defined criteria,   resulting   in   selection   of   the   most   suitable,   competitive   CSOs   to   support programmatic goals. It also reviewed the mechanisms in place to identify, assess and mitigate key partnership risks, and to monitor partner progress against expected results. The following observations were made:  Partnership strategy: The CCO lacked a defined, overarching partnership strategy that was aligned with its internal capacity to fulfil assurance, monitoring, and partner capacity building requirements. The large, diverse partner base, combined with limited internal coordination and a single implementing partnerships officer to oversee all arrangements, contributed to weaknesses in   programme   monitoring (Observation 3), cash transfers (Observation 4), and HACT assurance (Observation 5).  UNICEF implements   programmes   under the CPD 2022-2006 agreed with the Government, under   which   interventions   are   co-developed   and   rolling   workplans   signed   with   the

14  respective line ministries. For CSOs, the most suitable partners were identified by open selection, influenced by factors such as proximity to target populations and scope of reach. For instance, 95 per cent of health and HIV/AIDS funding - the largest outcome in the CPD - was allocated to government partners, while 75 per cent of WASH funding went to CSOs based on their expertise and access to communities. The CCO has invested significantly in the localization agenda, even leading the United Nations localization group, and prioritizing implementation with national and local CSOs. However, this approach brought challenges, including limited financial management and governance capacity among partners and high partner staff turnover, leading to a continuous need for capacity building. Many partners were engaged by the CCO despite known internal control weaknesses.  These operational limitations indicate a mismatch between the number and capacity of implementing partners and the CCO’s ability to oversee and support them effectively. Without a structured approach to partner selection and internal capacity assessment, the effectiveness   of   assurance   activities,   risk   mitigation,   and   long-term   programme  sustainability may be compromised.  Root   cause: There was a need for improved strategic partnership management and capacity planning that the CCO also acknowledged. The CCO was using the opportunity of the ongoing PBR process to establish a   Programme   Support Hub, with seven staff, including an implementing partnerships officer, to address these challenges.  Oversight of training for partners and vendors: Country offices are responsible for providing capacity building training to implementing partners and vendors on critical topics such as fraud prevention, SEA, cash transfers, and HACT assurance. The audit noted that the CCO prepared relevant training materials and invited partners and vendors to training sessions, however participation was not systematically recorded. This created challenges for tracking attendance, making it difficult to ensure that all implementing partners and vendors received essential training, exposing the CCO to increased risk in these key areas.  Root cause: Training activities were not sufficiently integrated into broader compliance and assurance activities to ensure accurate attendance data and adequate monitoring and follow-up, as required.  Partnership   opportunities:   For   open   partner   selection,   UNICEF   posts   partnership opportunities on the United Nations Partner Portal (UNPP) and invites all interested partners to submit electronic concept notes. For a sample of eight such partnership opportunities, the audit reviewed the CSO selection process, finalization of PDs and SPDs and adherence to reporting requirements. Four partnership opportunities were still open in UNPP more than one year after the application closure, and in one instance, an opportunity was progressed and completed without clear approval from the Partnership Review Committee. Management indicated that the opportunities were concluded, and PDs signed. However, it was unclear how this was done outside the UNPP system, and whether

15  the required control checks, such as due diligence, were completed. Critical partner selection review and assessment steps may be missed if UNPP is not utilized as required. Furthermore, the absence of a system trail in UNPP limited the CCO’s ability to ensure appropriate accountability for and ownership of the selection process.  Root cause: These issues were due to difficulties with the use of UNPP by CCO and implementing partner staff, the latter struggling with steady internet access and navigation of the system, which caused delays in the completion and closure of opportunities.  PDs and SPDs: The audit noted that 23 out of 41 PDs and 41 out of 109 SPDs did not meet the target of 45 days and 15 days, respectively, from submission to signature as required by the Programme Implementation Handbook. These were delayed by an average of 16 and 4 days respectively. Ten of the late 23 PDs were delayed due to amendments; 53 of all 150 PDs/SPDs signed during the audited period were amendments to the original document. The CCO explained that amendments generally result from delays in finalizing the original PD or in the implementation of agreed activities, which results in no-cost extensions to project durations. Any delay in completion of PDs may postpone the commencement of activities implementation and necessitate multiple amendments to the signed documents.  The CCO informed the audit team of plans to ensure timely processing of PDs through improved coordination of the preparation of PDs/SPDs and finalization of discussions with targeted implementing partners. This will be facilitated by the proposed Programme Support Hub. In addition, the planning, monitoring and research (PM&R) section was developing a Power BI-based tracker to reinforce accountability of staff involved in the PD/SPD processes. Further measures have been proposed by the CCO for consideration by the regional office, including signature of PDs/SPDs at the field office level.  Root causes: The above issues were due to insufficient capacity and high staff turnover among partners, and suboptimal use of UNPP and eTools by partner and CCO staff. Inadequate planning during partner onboarding also contributed to PD/SPD amendments, delays and process extensions.  Partner   quarterly   progress   reports   (QPRs)   and   humanitarian   reports:   QPRs   are prepared by partners to allow assessment of progress towards planned results for any PD. Partners are also required to submit humanitarian reports for SPDs and in an emergency context. To improve monitoring, the CCO set up a system for partners to submit situation reports to report on the status of high-frequency humanitarian indicators. Country offices are expected to upload all reports to eTools to allow complete, timely monitoring and follow-up of any issues. From its review of eTools, the audit noted that all the required reports (87 QPRs and 40 humanitarian reports) were overdue at the time of field work in June 2025. While the CCO indicated that some of these reports had been submitted to different sections but not uploaded to e-Tools, the audit was unable to substantiate this. Programme monitoring   High  The lack of an office-wide monitoring plan, coupled with inadequate coordination and oversight   of   monitoring   activities,   resulted   in   insufficient   programmatic   monitoring coverage during the audited period. In addition, the CCO did not have a robust quality assurance   mechanism   for   programmatic   monitoring   and   reporting,   which   led   to inconsistent   coverage,   for   example,   of   PSEA   and   SEUM,   and   suboptimal   reporting, hampering efforts to improve service delivery. 17  Good programme monitoring supports better decision-making by programme managers, adaptive   management,   organizational   learning   and   accountability   to   programme stakeholders. According to the UNICEF procedure on monitoring, country offices are expected to maintain a structured, coordinated office-wide monitoring and data system to ensure that monitoring activities are planned and implemented and that results are used to support effective programmatic delivery. The audit assessed the adequacy of the CCO’s monitoring activities and made the following observations:  Planning for programme monitoring: The UNICEF procedure on monitoring states that office-wide planning for programme monitoring links each programme indicator or marker of progress with specific monitoring activities within the unit and field office workplans. In the case of Cameroon, an office-wide plan for programme monitoring was not prepared. Instead, a decentralized approach was adopted whereby monitoring plans were prepared and implemented   by field offices and sections. The PM&R section communicated the number of programmatic visits to be conducted by each section or field office, based on the programmatic   assurance   key   performance   indicators,   and   reviewed   the   quality   of monitoring reports on a sample basis. As a result of this fragmented approach to planning and insufficient monitoring, the total number of programmatic visits performed during the period from January 2023 to December 2024 was 58 less than the required minimum target of 320, a shortfall of 18 per cent. Still, the total level of programmatic visits was 394 as the CCO had conducted visits in excess of those required for some partners. The absence of a coordinated, office-wide monitoring plan may lead to inconsistent or inadequate coverage, reduced visibility of the monitoring frequency and data, and suboptimal quality.  Root   cause:   The   above   issues   were   due   to   inadequate   coordination   between   the programme sections and with the PM&R section in ensuring the appropriate number of visits were conducted for each partner.  Programmatic visit reports: Programmatic assurance through field monitoring should be documented   in   eTools,   including   information   on   what   was   verified,   the   method   of verification, the findings of the verification, the conclusion, and any follow-up actions required. The audit reviewed a sample of 25 programmatic visit reports and noted that for 17 reports, follow-up actions were inconsistently recorded in eTools, and verification of the use of resources received by partners was not captured. SEUM was not covered in six reports, and PSEA coverage was absent from nine reports on CSO partners. The quality review of the reports was inadequate, as evidenced by the absence of supervisory sign-off, inconsistent referencing of output and activity numbers, and unclear verification of results. In three cases, critical observations regarding PSEA awareness, reporting and escalation that warranted CCO attention were not translated into action points in either the reports or eTools. Cash transfers to implementing partners   High  The limited availability of partners to conduct work for specific outcomes and in certain locations, combined with outdated partner risk profiles, led to the use of the DCT modality to transfer cash to high-risk partners. Several DCTs were outstanding for over six months, and   cash   transfer   processing   was   delayed   due   to   late   submission   of   supporting documentation by partners, inadequate planning by various sections, and the protracted completion of PDs.  Between January 2023 and May 2025, the CCO transferred US$59 million in cash transfers and supplies to implementing partners. Sixty-three per cent of cash transfers were to medium- and high-risk partners. The audit assessed the adequacy of controls to ensure the timely and accurate disbursement and reporting of cash transfers to implementing partners for   planned   activities.   It   also   assessed   whether   financial   assurance   activities   were appropriately planned, resourced, and executed to ensure that cash transfers were used

19  for their intended purposes and whether corrective measures were taken to recover any ineligible expenditure. The following observations were made:  Use of the DCT modality for high-risk partners: The UNICEF Programme Implementation Handbook requires that for significant and high-risk partners, country offices use direct payment   for   large   purchases   of   goods   or   services,   reimbursement,   or   a   blend   of reimbursements, direct payments and DCTs. In the period January 2023 to December 2024, 95 per cent of all cash transfers to non-United Nations implementing partners were nonetheless through DCT modality, and 33 per cent of these were to high-risk partners. Use of the DCT modality for high-risk partners exposes funds to a higher risk of misuse or fraud. The CCO explained that plans were underway to conduct micro-assessments for all high- risk assumed partners in order to establish the correct risk profiles for all partners and plan capacity building for those that require it. In addition, as part of the strategy to strengthen oversight of the cash transfer process, a review of the ratings of previously high-risk rated partners was planned, with a view to adjusting the rating for any partners whose financial management capabilities had improved since the previous assessment.  Root causes:   The CCO is limited in the scope of partners it can work with for certain outcomes, such as health and HIV/AIDS, which must be implemented through government systems. The limited financial capacity of some partners restricts the possibility to use reimbursement as a payment modality. The insecurity in the North-West and South-West regions also limits the number of partners in operation in those areas and the government exercises particular scrutiny with respect to authorizing non-governmental organizations to operate in these areas.  Processing of FACE forms: The UNICEF Programme Implementation Handbook states that cash should be disbursed no later than 10 working days after receipt of a properly completed FACE form from the partner. The audit found that 14 out of a sample of 25 FACE forms were processed after the 10 working day key performance indicator. Ten took between 15 to 25 days to process, and the remaining four took 35, 48, 77 and 407 days, respectively. Such delays in cash disbursement and liquidation affect financial performance and cash flow and may limit the effectiveness of fund usage in achieving planned results. The audit also noted that there was no evidence of prior approval of activities conducted by two government partners and one CSO   partner for which a total amount of US$240,485 was reimbursed by the CCO. UNICEF requires that partners do not incur expenditure for which they will seek direct payment or reimbursement, unless UNICEF has first agreed to the request, to mitigate the risk that partners might implement and seek reimbursement for unauthorized activities.  Root causes: Turnover of trained and knowledgeable partner staff has impacted partner capacity and led to the submission of FACE forms requiring clarification and correction. Reimbursements resulted from inadequate planning by the CCO prior to signing a PD to ensure sufficient resources for agreed activities. Some requests were made by partners with

20  unliquidated overdue cash transfers which required clearance in the system prior to processing the new requests.  DCTs monitoring and oversight: The audit noted that DCTs amounting to US$473,479 were outstanding for over six months at the time of audit fieldwork in June 2025, contrary to the three-month requirement 6 . Delays in liquidating DCTs may also delay issuance of additional transfers, with potential implications for programme delivery.  Cash transfers made during the period from January 2024 to June 2025, amounting to US$1,859,576, were issued with less than 30 days to grant expiry, and as of June 2025, 61 per cent of these transfers were yet to be liquidated. The use of funds outside the grant period   creates   a   financial   risk   to   UNICEF   with   the   possibility   of   donors   requiring reimbursements.  Root   causes:   The above issues were due to partner delays in submitting complete supporting documents and the CCO’s failure to check the completeness of submitted support documents before processing them in the system. Inadequate planning by the different sections and their implementing partners led to delays in the completion of PDs and subsequently impacted the timeliness of cash transfers. Implement the planned actions to update/correct partner risk profiles, plan  partner capacity building as required, and assess the possibility of establishing a  mechanism to minimize the use of the DCT modality for significant and high-risk  partners.  ii.   Strengthen the planning, coordination and oversight of cash transfer processes to  ensure adherence to all UNICEF requirements, including timely, accurate FACE  form registration, processing and issuance. Construction   Medium  The CCO did not obtain performance guarantees for construction works and did not exercise sufficient oversight of construction works conducted through CSOs. Some CSO capacity assessments were completed after signing PDs/SPDs, and there were no risk mitigation actions for CSOs with low or medium construction capacity. In some cases, the value of construction work with CSOs exceeded the maximum allowable amount without exceptional approval. This increased the risk of financial loss, delays and suboptimal construction works.  The audit assessed whether the CCO had adequate justification, appropriate internal structures and capacity to plan, manage and oversee construction projects. It also evaluated the effectiveness of controls - such as monitoring, oversight and supervision - to ensure that construction   activities   were   completed   as   planned,   meeting   timelines   and   quality specifications.   In   addition,   the   audit   reviewed   the   transparency   and   fairness   of   the processes used to source construction vendors, with a focus on ensuring best value for money. The audit noted the following:  Performance guarantees: Due to the high risks involved and the level of financial and reputational   exposure,   the   UNICEF   procedure   on   construction   projects   requires performance guarantees for construction projects. A performance guarantee is issued by an insurance company or a bank to provide financial compensation in the event that a contractor fails to fulfil its contractual obligations.

25  The audit noted that, in all six sampled construction projects amounting to US$1.3 million, the CCO did not obtain performance guarantees from the contractors. In three of the six projects, the contract did not include the requirement for a performance guarantee. In two of the three cases, this was due to a technical misunderstanding, as the projects were not classified   as   ‘construction’   despite   including   both   borehole   drilling   and   construction elements. This was clarified by UNICEF Supply Division during the audit fieldwork. Without a performance guarantee, the CCO would not be compensated in the event of the contractor's failure to perform the agreed services.  Root   causes: The CCO faced several challenges associated with the requirement for construction firms to submit performance guarantees, including reluctance by local banks to issue performance guarantees, delays in the release of funds after project completion, and a significant impact on the firms’ cashflow, which may discourage small- to medium- sized construction firms from bidding for UNICEF construction contracts. The   CCO   signed   SPDs with   construction   components with seven   CSOs in   2024   and conducted capacity assessment for all. 29  Definition s   of Audit   Observation   Ratings  To assist management in prioritizing the actions arising from the audit, OIAI ascribes a rating to each audit observation based on the potential consequence or residual risks to the audited entity, area, activity or process or to UNICEF as a whole. Individual observations are rated as follows:  Low  The observation concerns   a potential opportunity for improvement in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes. Low- priority observations are reported to management during the audit but are   not   included   in   the   audit   report.   Action   in   response   to   the observation is desirable.  Medium  The observation relates to a weakness or deficiency in the assessed governance,   risk   management   or   control   processes   that   requires resolution within a reasonable period to avoid adverse consequences for the audited entity, area, activity or process.  High  The observation concerns a fundamental weakness or deficiency in the assessed   governance,   risk   management   or   control   processes   that requires prompt/immediate resolution to avoid severe/major adverse consequences for the audited entity, area, activity or process, or for UNICEF as a whole.  Definitions of Overall Audit Conclusions  The above ratings of audit observations are then used to support an overall audit conclusion for the area under review, as follows:  Satisfactory   The assessed governance, risk management, and control processes were adequate and functioning well.  Partially  Satisfactory,  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management, and control processes were generally adequate and functioning but needed improvement. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified were unlikely to have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  Partially  Satisfactory,  Major  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management, or control processes needed major improvement. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified could have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  Unsatisfactory  The assessed governance, risk management or control processes were not adequately established or did not function well.
