EXECUTIVE   SUMMARY  3  The Office of Internal Audit and Investigations (OIAI) conducted an audit of the   Libya  Country Office , covering the period from   January 2024   to   March 2025 .   The audit was  conducted   in   March 2025   in   conformance   with   the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global  Interna l Audit   Standards.   The objective of the audit was to   assess the adequacy and  effectiveness of the   governance ,   risk management   and   control processes   related to   a  selection of significant risk   ar eas   of the   Libya Country Office.   The   specific   risks evaluated  are   set out   in the Audit Objective, Scope and Approach   s ection of this report .  The   Libya   Country Office   supports the Government of   Libya   in the implementation of the  country programme   through partnerships with the Government and with civil society  o rganizations (CSOs).   During the audit period,   the Country Office contributed to  humanitarian   response efforts   resulting from the 2023 floods and the continuing  Sudanese migrant   situation ,   o perating   in challenging conditions with   limited access to  beneficiaries in some areas .   The Country Office   contributed cash of   US$ 8. 1 million   and  programme supplies of   US $7.9 million   to   partners, representing approximately   22   and 21  per cent of total expenditure   in 2024 .   S ervice contracts ,   including   for   construction  activities ,   amounted to   26   per cent   of t otal   expenditure .   In planning for this audit, the  audit team identified several risks related to the   transfer of cash and supplies, as well as  the management of service contracts .   In addition, t he   Country O ffice   had   reduced   its  workforce by   20 per   cent in 2024 and   anticipated further   reduction s   in 2025   due   to  funding cuts .   The audit sought to determine whether and how the Country Office  managed those risks.  Overall Conclusion  Based on the audit work performed,   OIAI  concluded that the assessed governance, risk  management, or control processes were  Partially Satisfactory, Improvement Needed,  meaning that the   assessed governance, risk  management or control processes were  generally adequate and functioning but  needed improvement. The weaknesses or  deficiencies   identified were unlikely to have a materially negative impact on the  performance of the audited   entity, area, activity or process. C ONTEXT  8  Country context  Libya is an upper - middle income country with   a   population of 6.8 million , of which   children  and adolescents represent 34   per cent .   In 2023,   severe floods r esulting from   Storm Daniel  increased   the number of people   requiring   some form of humanitarian assistance .   The  Sudanese crisis has also impacted Libya since April 2023 ,   with the remote southeast ern  region   of   Libya   receiving the highest numbers of Sudanese migrants .   The   operationally  challenging context   has led to   high levels of humanitarian need.  Country   O ffice affordability   and   programming review  Due to   a decrease in   funding ,   the   Country O ffice performed a   Programme Budget   R eview  (PBR)   in   late   2024   which resulted in   a   significant   reduc tion   in   payroll costs .   I n February  2025 ,   a review of the Country Office’s strategic direction ,   programm ing priorities   and  implementation modalities   was conducted   to enhance programme effectiveness for the  remaining period of the 2023 - 2026 Country Programme Document (CPD) period .   Within  the context of   continuing funding cuts , the Country Office conducted a n   affordability  analysis in March 2025, with a view to achieving further cost reductions and increasing  fundraising efforts.   These efforts to ensure that   UNICEF Libya remains fit for purpose ,  amid financial constraints ,   were   ongoing at the time of the audit fieldwork.  Prior to the affordability and programming review s , t he country programme 2023 - 2025  (extended to 2026)   was structured around   the following   outcome areas:  •   Health and Nutrition: Enhance access to quality health and nutrition services with  improved health system capacity and vaccination coverage.  •   Education: Ensure uninterrupted, inclusive, and quality learning through policy  support, teacher training, and infrastructure upgrades.  •   Child Protection: Strengthen child protection systems to reduce violence, abuse,  and exploitation, while raising awareness and improving services.  •   Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene (WASH): Expand access to sustainable, climate -  resilient WASH services through legislative support and infrastructure improvements.  •   Social Policy: Develop inclusive social protection to reduce child poverty and  vulnerabilities through evidence - based policies and capacity building.  •   Programme Effectiveness: Ensure efficient programme management, monitoring,  and partnerships to maximize outcomes.  Country Office location and workforce  The   Libya   C ountry   O ffice   is located in Tripoli and has a zon e   office   and warehouse   in  Benghazi . AUDIT OBJECTIVES ,   SCOPE   AND APPROACH  10  The overarching objective of this risk - based audit was to assess the adequacy and  effectiveness of the governance, risk management and control processes over a selection  of significant risk areas of the   Libya   Country Office.  The audit scope was determined during the audit planning process based on an  a ssessment of the highest inherent risks 1   to the achievement of programme objectives  and included the following areas:  •   Risk management including   f raud risk management  •   Partnership   m anagement   and   c ash transfers  •   Harmonized approach to cash transfer assurance activities  •   Field m onitoring, including   monitoring of   Protection from   S exual   E xploitation and  A buse  •   Management of   c onstruction   a ctivities  •   Supply distribution,   i nventory and warehouse management, and   s upply   e nd - u ser  m onitoring  •   V endor management and procurement of goods and services .  Due to the   reviews of the Country Office affordability and programme strategy that were  in progress at the time of the audit,   risk   areas related   t o fundin g , programme planning  and other programme activities ( except for   those referred to above ) were   excluded from  the audit scope.  The audit   field work   was conducted through remote preparatory interviews with Country  Office management and an on - site visit from   2   to 2 1   March 202 5 ,   i n conformance with the  Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global Internal Audit Standards.   For the purpose of audit  testing, the audit covered the period from J anuary   202 4   to   March   202 5 . The audit   involved  a combination of methods, tools and techniques, including interviews, data analytics,  document s   review, tests of transactions   and validation of preliminary observations.  1 Inherent risk refers to the potential adverse event that could occur if management takes no actions, including internal  control activities. 13  The audit team assessed the adequacy and effectiveness of controls over the selection ,  management   and capacity - building   of implementing partners,   to ensure   the timely,  accurate transfer of resources   and their use for intended purposes by the partners .  The auditors noted the following:  Partner contributions   to programme costs :   According to the UNICEF Programme  Handbook, country offices should determine the minimum contribution to be made by  each partner   to its programme document cost. Any deviations from the agreed  amount   are   documented in a note for the record, approved by the Country Office  Representative. The Libya Country Office established a minimum partner contribution  of 10 per cent.   For Efficiency & Effectiveness (E&E) outcome areas in simplified  (humanitarian) programme documents, the C ountry   O ffice’s   contribution should not  exceed 18 per cent.  The audit team noted that, in 9 out of the Country Office’s   1 5   programme documents  and simplified programme documents,   the partner contribution   was less than 10 per  cent ;   in   seven   cases, it was zero.   The auditors did not find any documented rationale  for these exceptions. I n three   cases , the   Country Office contribution to   partner s for  E&E   either exceeded or came close to the   18   per   cent ceiling . This indicated   a need to  monitor and reassess the level of expected contributions , and a potential opportunity  to increase the share of costs borne by partners, to continue delivering results for  children in Libya in an increasingly challenging funding environment.  Root Cause:   Country Office management   stated that , due to   the Darna flood  emergency   response and other urgent matters,   the programme documents   had to be  finalized   quickly. This meant that attention was not paid to   reviewing the level of  partnership contributions and/or addressing   or documenting deviations from the  minimum contributions.  Implementing partner b udgeting process :   According to   the   UNICEF Programme  Implementation Handbook , implementing partners should provide itemized cost  estimates (ICE) as the basis for obtaining   periodic ( quarterly )   cash payments from  UNICEF   and for justifying expenditure for   agreed programme interventions. The  partners submit a quarterly ICE, obtain cash transfer advances and then are expected  to liquidate the advances in alignment with the ICE . This is especially important in the  Libya context where short - term grants are common, to minimize the Country Office’s  exposure to cashflow challenges .  The audit team reviewed all notes for the record in 2024 to assess the management of  any exceptions to UNICEF policies and procedures and noted ten instances (related to  four partners) where spending by the partner exceeded the quarterly   tranche  disbursed by   UNICEF, including re curring overspending   over consecutive quarterly  periods . For example, one partner exceeded the approved quarterly amount in four  consecutive quarters, while another did so in three quarters.

14  Root cause :   These weaknesses were due to   i nadequate   capacity building by   the  Country Office   to develop   the   implementing partners’   financial management  capa bilities.  Disbursement and liquidation of c ash   transfers to implementing partners:  Ac cording to the UNICEF Programme   Implementation Handbook   and to   ensure timely  implementation of programme activities, country offices   must disburse cash to  implementing partners no   more   than 14 calendar days   after   receipt of adequately  completed Funding Authorization and Certificate Expenditure (FACE) forms   and  liquidate the advances within a reasonable timeframe, not exceeding the grant expiry.  The Country Office transferred about   US $8.1 million to   18   implementing partners in  2024   and   issu ed   124   FACE forms.   The auditors reviewed a sample of 12 FACE forms  amounting to about US$1.3 million ,   to assess the timeliness and accuracy of cash  disbursement and liquidation.   In   one   case, cash was disbursed only five days before  the relevant grant’s expiry   and in another,   after the grant had   expired . The   audit team’s  analysis showed that a pproximately US$1 million   or 17 per cent of total cash   transfers  w as   made   less than 3 0 days before grant expiry . The audit team noted d elays in  processing   six of the 12 sampled   FACE form s ,   ranging from 18 to 59 days.   The lack of  timely cash disbursement and liquidation   negatively   affects financial performance   and  cash flow .  Root Cause s :   The   Country Office   attributed   these delays   to   late submission of FACE  forms by partners (some due   to a technical issue with the UNICEF system that has been  escalated for resolution by UNICEF Headquarters) and to delays in updating  programme document budgets   to reflect changes in planned activities. The Country  Office management explained that cash was sometimes disbursed close to the grant  expiry date because many   grant s in the Libya country context have short   lifespans .  Coverage of assurance activities:   Under the Harmonized Approach to Cash Transfers  (HACT) framework, UNICEF country offices must carry out assurance activities  ( including   programmatic visits and   spot checks )   to   verify that   proper use   is made   of  cash transfers by implementing partners. The framework also requires   that any  significant weaknesses in implementing partners’ internal control and programme  activities identified   by assurance activities are followed up by country offices on a  timely   basis ,   to ensure that the weaknesses are addressed ,   and partner capacity can be  strengthened .   The audit   team   noted the following :  •   The Country Office had not based its planned HACT programmatic visits on the  higher of   cash or supply contributions, as required by UNICEF’s Procedure on  Monitoring ,   but was using cash only as the determinant.   This meant, f or example,  that the number of programmatic   visits   conducted for the   Ministry of Education  and   the Ministry of Environment   was insufficient to provide the expected level of  assurance, as   supply contributions   of US$1 million and US$0.6 million respectively  were not taken into account.

15  Root Cause:   The Country Office was not aware that the new, mandatory UNICEF  Procedure on Monitoring was effective 1 April 2024.  •   UNICEF’s HACT Procedures require spot checks of partners   liquidating   US$50,000   or  more to verify whether the cash transferred by UNICEF has been used for intended  purposes. Three   out   of 12   CSO partners were not spot checked in 2024 although  they received cash transfers exceeding   US $480,000,   US $195,000 and   US $191,000  respectively.  Root Cause:   The Country Office stated that the HACT dashboard did not require these  spot checks; however, in OIAI’s view, amounts which significantly exceed the  US $50,000 threshold should be considered for financial assurance coverage.  F ailure to conduct sufficient risk - based HACT assurance activities reduces the level of  assurance that resources transferred to partners are used for intended programmatic  purposes.  Follow - up of assurance activities:   I n a sample of five spot check   reports   reviewed by  the audit team:  •   N o exit meetings were held   with the partners to ensure that partners were  committed to implementing the spot check   recommendations.  •   I n four   out   of five   cases ,   there was no documentation to indicate that the   partners  accept ed   the high priority   findings. Therefore, it was not clear whether the  recommendations would be implemented and the partners’ capacity improved.  •   T he   same observation ‘O riginal receipts were not stamped paid by UNICEF’  appeared in all reports   and there was no evidence that this issue has been resolved  by the Country Office . This could lead to re submission   of original receipts and  potential   overpayment to partners.  As at 13 January 2025, 36 out of 418 or 9 per cent of financial findings from HACT  assurance activities completed in 2022 to 2024 were overdue, including seven rated  high risk. In a sample of five recommendation s   (action points) closed by the   Country  O ffice, the audit team was not able to find sufficient evidence that the partners had  implemented the spot check recommendations. The Country Office had not adopted  the practice of systematic follow - up and documentation evidencing the partners’  implementation of recommended actions.  Inadequate monitoring of the quality of financial assurance activities and follow - up of  recommendations for partner capacity development may lead to unresolved control  weaknesses and gaps in delivery of results to children.  Root Cause:   The Country Office stated that these shortcomings resulted from a single  staff member having to manage both partnership and HACT assurance matters .   At the  end of the audit fieldwork, the   Country O ffice   was   establishing a   HACT Taskforce to  monitor   HACT assurance activities and review   the allocation of tasks for financial  assurance and partnership management .

25  Definition s   of Audit   Observation   Ratings  To assist management in prioritizing the actions arising from the audit, OIAI ascribes a  rating to each audit observation based on the potential consequence or residual risks  to the audited entity, area, activity or   process   or   to   UNICEF as a whole. Individual  observations are rated as follows:  Low  The observation concerns   a   potential opportunity for improvement in  the assessed governance, risk management or control processes . Low -  priority observations   are reported to management during the audit  but   are   not included in the audit report. Action   in response to the  observation   is desirable.  Medium  The observation relates to   a   weakness or deficiency in the assessed  governance, risk management or control processes   that   requires  resolution within a reasonable   period   to avoid adverse consequences  for the audited entity, area, activity or process.  High  The observation concerns a   fundamental weakness or deficiency in the  assessed governance, risk management or control processes   that  requires prompt/immediate resolution to avoid severe/major adverse  consequences for the audited entity, area, activity or process ,   or for  UNICEF as a whole.  Definition s   of Overall Audit Conclusions  The above   ratings of audit observations are then used to support an overall audit  conclusion for the area under review, as follows:  Satisfactory   The assessed governance, risk management ,   and   control  processes were adequate and functioning well. The w eaknesses or deficiencies identified   were  unlikely   to   have a materially negative impact on   the  performance   of the audited entity, area, activity   or   process.  Partially  Satisfactory,  M ajor  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management ,   or control  processes   needed major improvement .   The w eaknesses or  deficiencies identified could   have a materially negative impact  on   the   performance of the   audited entity, area, activity or  process.  Unsatisfactory  The assessed governance, risk management or control  processes   were not adequately established or   did not function  well .
