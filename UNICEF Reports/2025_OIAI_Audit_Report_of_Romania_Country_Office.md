EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  3  The Office of Internal Audit and Investigations (OIAI) conducted an audit of the   Romania  Country   O ffice , covering the period from   January 2024   to   December 2024 .   The audit was  conducted   in   May 2025   in   conformance   with   the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global  Interna l Audit   Standards.   The objective of the audit was to   assess the adequacy and  effectiveness of the governance, risk management   and   control processes   related to   a  selection of significant risk areas   of the   Romania Country Office.   The   specific   risks  evaluated are   set out   in the Audit Objective, Scope and Approach   s ection of this report .  UNICEF   supports the Government of Romania in the implementation of the country  programme through partnerships with the Government and with civil   society  organizations (CSOs).   In addition to   supporting national and local   efforts   to reduce child  poverty and social exclusion ,   since 2022   the Country Office   has   provided humanitarian  assistance to refugees transiting   through   or settling in Romania , consequent to the war in  Ukraine.   During the audited period, the Country Office transferred approximately US$7. 7  million to implementing partners, which represents approximately 45 per cent of total  expenditure in 2024 .   Due to Romania’s ‘high - income’ status ,   the Country Office was  planning its transition to operate without regular resources as of 2026 and to be self -  funding by 2028 .   In planning for this audit, the audit team identified   several risks related  to   cash transfers to implementing partners , resource mobilization   and   the management  of risks related to the   Country Office’s   sustainability .   The audit sought to determine  whether and how the   Country O ffice managed those risks.  At the time of the   audit ,   the   Country Office   was undergoing a   Programme   and   Budget  Review   (PBR) to realign its structure, staffing and programmatic priorities   following  decisions linked to the Future Focus Initiative launched by UNICEF H eadquarters.   While no  decision had yet been taken   by UNICEF H eadquarters   with regard to   the future business  model for   th is and other high - income   c ountry   o ffice s, it   was anticipated that   a   major  restructuring   and oth er cost control measures   would be required .   The audit   team   was  mindful that   this   might create   additional strategic and operational risks ,   with potential  implications   for the Country Office’s governance, risk management   and control processes  and took this into account in   its audit approach   and in   developing the proposed actions   to  address the identified issues. AUDIT OBJECTIVE ,   SCOPE   AND APPROACH  9  The   objective of the audit was to assess the adequacy and effectiveness   of the  governance, risk management and control processes   related to a selection of significant  risk areas of the   Romania Country Office.  The audit scope   was determined during the audit planning process based on an  assessment of the   inherent 3   risks   of the   Romania Country Office ,   and included the  following areas:  •   Governance and Accountability  •   Risk Management including Fraud Risk Management  •   Resource Mobilization  •   Pr otection from   Sexual Exploitation and Abuse (PSEA)  •   Management of Implementing Partners  •   Cash Transfers   to Implementing Partners  •   Harmonized Approach to Cash Transfers (HACT) Assurance  •   Programme Field Monitoring .  The audit   fieldwork   was conducted   in   May 2025   in   conformance   with   the   Institute of  Internal Auditors’ Global   Internal Audit Standards.   For the purpose of audit testing, the  audit covered the period from   January 2024   to   December 2024 .   The audit   involved   a  combination of methods, tools and techniques,   including interviews,   data analytics,  document review, tests of transactions and validation of preliminary   observations .  3   Inherent risk refers to the potential adverse event that could occur if management takes no actions, including  internal control activities. The higher the likelihood of the event occurring and the more serious the impact would be  should the adverse event   occur, the stronger the need for adequate and effective risk management and control  processes.

Managed transition to   high - income   status:   In line with UNICEF’s Executive Board  decision 2008/15, once a country has attained high - income status and maintained that  status for two consecutive years, the UNICEF office in that country is no longer eligible for  allocations from UNICEF’s regular reso urces to support its core activities and  infrastructure. As Romania was classified as a high - income country   in   2023, the Country  Office had been proactively preparing a structured transition from   upper middle   to hig h -  income country status in its goal to   operate without   r egular   r esources as of 2026 .  The transition strategy was based around four pillars:  •   Identifying and addressing programmatic challenges and bottlenecks related to  national commitments:   An extended (mid - term) programme review was in progress  and the results of a Country Programme evaluation   to be undertaken   during 2025,  w ould   inform the finetuning of programmatic priorities   for the r e m a in der   of the  C ountry   P rogramme   D ocument   2023 - 2027 and shape   the future   of   UNICEF  programme work   in a   high - income country   from 2028 onwards.  •   Fundraising and leveraging resources for children:   Having met its targets for the  last two years, the Country Office anticipated further growth in local fundraising  potential in a dynamic private sector context, while leveraging national resources and  exploring new innovative financing solutions.  •   Operational efficiencies:   Romania was one of four Country Offices in the Europe and  Central Asia Region (ECAR) that volunteered to participate in an office simplification  and modernisation review led by UNICEF’s Global Shared Service Centre. While this  was in its early stages and   put on hold in March 2025, the Country Office was  committed to exploring options to streamline and tailor processes and internal  controls to fit high - income country office contexts.

Risk   management   activities :   Country offices should identify, assess, monitor and  respond to key risks to achieve ment of   their objectives and ensure that the risk  environment is regularly scanned for changes and   that   mitigating actions are assessed for  their ongoing effectiveness. The audit team reviewed the adequacy of the Country Office’s  approach to ensuring regular assessment and management of significant and emerging  risks,   including   those   related to the global funding crisis ,   and made the following  observations:  •   Relevance and usefulness of the risk register:   In 2024, t he   Country Office   developed  its   risk register using a   ‘ bottom - up ’   approach that involved   providing staff with  guidance on how to   develop a risk register and then   soliciting i nput   from all staff on  risks to be included in the risk register . The consolidated risk register was presented to  the Country Management Team (CMT)   for endorsement   every six months .   T he   risk

12  register included   some significant risks ,   f or example,   related to   the   capacity to   respond  to a   regional   crisis   and   the   adequacy of   local fundraising .   However,   it   did not   directly  address   the most critical   risks   specific   to the   global   funding situation   and   its impact on  the Country Office’s sustainability ,   thus limiting the usefulness of the risk register   as a  tool to support structured   analysis   and review   of   all key existing and emerging risks .  The audit team   acknowledged th e   pressure on   t he   Country Management Team   at the  time of the audit   to manage the fast - changing situation while continuing to deliver   on  country programme priorities . However ,   the Country Office should   endeavour to   use  the risk register   to prompt   and record   the regular review and   discussion   of   ongoing  and emerging risks,   including   the adequacy of existing   mitiga ting actions   and the need  for   additional measures .   Used in this way, the risk register will   serve its intended  purpose as a management tool to   support   effective , transparent   Country Office  governance ,   and agreement of   risk tolerance levels   at   the   country ,   regional   and  headquarters   office level . Root cause:   G uidance on risk management from   UNICEF   H eadquarters   was   in the  process of being   updated .  •   Inclusion of key institutional risks:   T he risk register did not include an assessment of  risks related to sexual exploitation and abuse/sexual harassment (SEA/SH), as required  by the UNICEF Guidance on Risk Assessment. Risks related to cybersecurity and  business continuity management were also   not recorded, as required, although risks  specific to earthquakes were assessed and rated as low. Inclusion of these critical risk  areas is essential not only to ensure effective risk management at the local level but  also for regional office and headquart ers oversight and, in the case of SEA, for global  reporting purposes. Root causes:   The Country Office considered that,   since it had no local servers and  all data was stored in the cloud, it was not necessary to include business  continuity and   cybersecurity risks in the register as these   risks had been  transferred to the Information and Communication Technology Division (ICTD). No  SEA/SH risks were included in the risk register as staff did not raise any risks related to  these areas in risk discussions.   T he Country Office had not received   any feedback   from  the Regional Office or relevant Headquarters   Offices   on this matter.  Risks and opportunities:   T he audit team noted the following risks and opportunities  associated with the   global funding   situation, for consideration by the Country Office:  •   Lessons learned from the Ukraine crisis:   The auditors   acknowledged the agility  demonstrated by the Country Office in emerging from a period of significant change  following the Ukraine crisis with a generally strong control environment and well -  functioning internal control processes. Valuable lessons can be l earned by UNICEF  from this experience at a local, regional and potentially global level.

13  •   Process simplification and digitization:   The Country Office demonstrated  commitment to streamlining and simplifying processes and shared with   the audit team  several opportunities to increase efficiency (at Country Office and Headquarters level)  through greater automation, better knowledge - sharing and more risk - based internal  controls (aligned with Country Office risk profile and size). Any reduction of staff   is  likely to accelerate the drive to simplify and automate business processes across  UNICEF. In a lean office structure, the effects of unplanned  staffing gaps may be more significant. Therefore, it is important for the Country Office  to en sure that measures are taken to identify and seek to retain ‘at risk’ key staff.  •   Employee morale:   Staff morale may decrease due to uncertainty and restructuring  decisions, and staff wellbeing and health may suffer, which could reduce productivity.  The Country Office should continue the practice of being open and transparent with  staff, involving them   in PBR processes as appropriate, and availing them of support  services e.g. counselling.  •   Knowledge management:   Maintaining up - to - date procedural documentation and  ensuring adequate cover for staff absences e.g., through occasional job ‘shadowing’ or  training becomes more critical in the event of unplanned staff departures or a major  downsizing, to ensure knowledge   retention and/or transfer. Provision of handover  notes/document should be a pre - condition for ‘offboarding’ and sufficient time allowed  for handover, where necessary.  •   Internal controls:   Reducing staff numbers may disrupt workflows and compromise  key control processes e.g., segregation of duties. The risk of fraud is also heightened  during such periods of uncertainty, restructuring etc. The Country Office should  carefully consider the imp act of any change on key internal controls in the future  business structure and any transitional period with a particular focus on fraud risk  management and segregation of duties.
