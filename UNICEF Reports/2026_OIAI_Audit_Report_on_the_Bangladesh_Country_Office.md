EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  3  The Office of Internal Audit and Investigations (OIAI) conducted an audit of the Bangladesh Country Office (BCO), covering the period from January 2024 to August 2025. The audit was conducted from September 2025 to November 2025 in conformance with the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global Internal Audit Standards. The objective of the audit was to assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the governance, risk management and control processes related to a selection of significant risk areas of the Bangladesh Country Office. The specific risks evaluated are set out in the Audit Objectives, Scope and Approach section of this report.  The Bangladesh Country Office works with government partners and civil society organizations to implement planned programme activities. During the audited period, the Country Office transferred approximately US$103 million to implementing partners, representing 42 per cent of expenditure. There were several risks related to partnerships and use of cash transfers to implementing partners. In addition, the country context indicates a high-risk environment for sexual exploitation and abuse of children and women. The audit sought to determine whether and how the Country Office managed those risks.  Overall conclusion  Based   on   the   audit   work   performed,   OIAI concluded that the assessed governance, risk management   or   control   processes   were Partially   Satisfactory,   Improvement Needed,   meaning   that   the   assessed governance,   risk   management   and   control processes   were   generally   adequate   and functioning   but   needed   improvement.   The weaknesses or deficiencies identified were unlikely to have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  Summary of Observations and Agreed Actions  OIAI noted the following practice that was innovative or exceeded expected levels of control:  •   W ater, sanitation and hygiene   (WASH) inventory management system :   The WASH team had implemented an inventory management system for implementing partners that did not have their own systems. This allowed the WASH team to obtain accurate information on the supplies managed by implementing partners and helped it determine when to send more supplies to implementing partners and when to request movements of these supplies to other locations as needed. C ONTEXT  6  Prior to 2024, Bangladesh experienced robust economic growth. In April 2024, the World Bank projected Bangladesh's gross domestic product growth to be 5.6 per cent for 2024. Following civil unrest and ensuing economic volatility, however, there was a downward revision of this forecast for 2025 to 4 per cent.  Inflation was a critical concern in 2024. Political instability exacerbated existing economic vulnerabilities, leading to a sharp rise in prices, hitting a peak in October, with general inflation at 10.87 per cent and food inflation at 12.66 per cent. The local currency devalued against the US dollar, and while foreign exchange reserves increased, slightly supported by higher remittances and a positive trade balance, they remained precariously low at around three months’ worth of imports. Budgetary expenditure was pressured by high inflation, a weakened economy and low tax revenue. The office faced an extended period of operation without government counterparts, and under challenging civil circumstances.  The year 2024 saw unprecedented floods and cyclones impacting children. Cyclone Remal and subsequent floods in the Haor Region, Jamuna Basin and Chattogram affected over 18.4 million people, 38.64 per cent of whom were children, with 6.4 million needing humanitarian aid. Cyclone Remal alone impacted 4.6 million people in eight coastal districts, leaving 1.3 million in need of aid. The northeastern floods in June affected 3.7 million people, followed by riverine floods in the Jamuna Basin in July impacting 5.1 million, and the worst floods in three decades in the East in August affecting 5 million. Overall, 5 million people required humanitarian support due to the 2024 floods. These disasters threatened to reverse progress in child outcomes and service deliveries in Bangladesh and illustrate Bangladesh’s extreme vulnerability to climate change.  In health, data from the 2022 Socio-Economic and Demographic Survey and the 2023 Bangladesh Sample Vital Statistics Report revealed a persistent neonatal mortality rate of 20 deaths per 1,000 live births and an under-five mortality rate of 33 deaths per 1,000 live births. Rural areas showed the poorest outcomes, with under-five mortality at 35.96 per 1,000 live births, and rural males experienced higher mortality rates (40.40) than females (31.26). The maternal mortality ratio was 130.58 per 100,000 live births nationally and was higher in rural areas (148.51). These disparities underscored the need for targeted interventions to improve maternal and child health services in rural settings. In education, over one fifth of young people in Bangladesh were not in education, employment or training, with insufficient vocational training available. Informal employment was prevalent (84.9 per cent), often with low pay and poor conditions. For those young people who achieved higher education and graduated from university, there was often a mismatch between their skills and employer demands, leading to high graduate unemployment (27.8 per cent in 2022). The labour market did not provide enough opportunities for more educated young people, with young female graduates especially affected.

AUDIT OBJECTIVES ,   SCOPE   AND APPROACH  9  The objective of the audit was to assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the governance, risk management and control processes related to a selection of significant risk areas of the Bangladesh Country Office.  The audit scope was determined during the audit planning process based on an assessment of the inherent risks of the Country Office and included the following areas:  •   Governance and accountability  •   Risk management including fraud risk management  •   Resource mobilization  •   Protection from sexual exploitation and abuse (PSEA)  •   Humanitarian cash transfers  •   Programme monitoring  •   Management of implementing partners  •   Cash transfers to implementing partners  •   HACT financial assurance  •   Service contracts  •   Supplies and logistics  The audit fieldwork was conducted from September 2025 to November 2025 in conformance with the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global Internal Audit Standards. For the purpose of audit testing, the audit covered the period from January 2024 to August 2025 and involved a combination of methods, tools and techniques, including interviews, data analytics, document review, transactions tests, evaluations and validation of preliminary observations.

OBSERVATIONS AND MANAGEMENT ACTION PLAN  10  The key areas where actions are needed are summarized below.  1. Humanitarian cash transfers   Medium  Advance disbursement to the financial service provider exceeding defined thresholds was made without required approvals, driven by the perception that the approval process was too slow. Delays in follow-up and closure of redressal tickets from payment cycles, caused by staffing constraints, may undermine accountability to the affected population.  UNICEF’s Procedure on Cash Transfers to Beneficiaries requires country offices to establish monitoring and assurance mechanisms to provide reasonable assurance over beneficiary list eligibility, existence, accuracy and validity; whether eligible beneficiaries received accurate cash assistance amounts; and whether they have a channel to voice concerns. The audit team assessed the adequacy of controls over beneficiary targeting and beneficiary data, due diligence over the financial service provider, and whether there was reasonable assurance that intended beneficiaries received the correct cash transfer amount on time. OIAI noted the following:  Split HCT payments  The audit noted that the Country Office made advance payments of more than US$70,000 without the specific approval of the comptroller as required under Financial Regulation rule 112.6 and the 2015 Division of Financial and Administrative Management (DFAM) policy on prepayments and advance. All six remittances through the financial service provider, totalling $934,481.98, exceeded the defined threshold and required comptroller approval. The office noted that it split high-value payments into smaller tranches when beneficiary numbers pushed totals above the threshold. The process is inefficient and is not compliant with financial regulations. The Country Office explained that transfers from UNICEF’s bank account to eWallet, a mobile banking platform, are invoiced by the financial service provider based on the payment plan, with funds deposited into eWallet for subsequent disbursement to beneficiaries. This accounting treatment is incorrect, however, as the eWallet functions as a temporary repository of the funds rather than an expense account, and any unused balance remaining after beneficiary payments must be returned to UNICEF.  Root cause: The DFAM approval process is perceived as lengthy, and HCT implementation is largely driven by the programme section, which cited inadequate guidance on payment procedures as a contributing factor to the gaps identified.  Monitoring of cash assistance interventions  This monitoring generally includes real-time process monitoring and post-distribution monitoring confirming that payments are made in full, on time, and to the correct

11  beneficiaries. To ensure objectivity, monitoring should be carried out by entities that are not involved in beneficiary identification or payment processing. Based on the scope of work defined in the programme documents for one of the three partners supporting HCT programme implementation, the audit noted that the partners’ responsibilities include beneficiary identification and registration, beneficiary database and grievance redressal, and post-distribution monitoring and analysis. Involvement across multiple stages of the delivery chain can compromise the objectivity of the monitoring function. Independent oversight is essential to provide an unbiased assessment of operations and to identify issues requiring corrective action. The Country Office indicated that, beginning in 2026, it plans to engage a third-party service provider to conduct post-distribution monitoring activities; therefore, no recommendation is made in this area.  Grievance and redressal mechanism (GRM)  The GRM is embedded within the Humanitarian Cash Operations and Programme Ecosystem (HOPE), but it was rolled out in stages. At the outset of the response, the civil society organization partners set up field-level feedback and complaint mechanisms. Following 2025 training, however, the GRM embedded in HOPE was rolled out. The post- distribution report on the Remal response highlighted gaps in knowledge of the complaints process, with 69 per cent of respondents indicating they were unaware of how to lodge a complaint. This, however, improved in 2025, as the European Union’s  Directorate-General for European Civil Protection and Humanitarian Operations   flood response noted that 65 per cent of respondents were aware of how to provide feedback on the programme. During a system walkthrough, the audit team observed delays of up to five months in the follow-up and closure of 2,184 out of 5,869 system-generated tickets in the GRM module related to HCT payment cycles. The office had not prioritized the tickets that arose from the payment cycles. Further review of a sample of tickets showed that many relate to potential duplicate beneficiary entries, which must be investigated to prevent the exclusion of eligible beneficiaries. Unresolved tickets may undermine accountability to affected populations by exclusion of eligible beneficiaries.  Root cause: Inadequate staffing capacity led to gaps in follow-up and timely closure of tickets from HCT payment cycles.  AGREED ACTIONS  The Bangladesh Country Office agrees to:  i.   Strengthen compliance with advance payment requirements for humanitarian  cash transfer programmes by providing adequate training to the programme  section and establishing appropriate oversight by the finance section.  ii.   Ensure follow-up and timely closure of tickets raised in the grievance and  redressal mechanism module by addressing the capacity constraints.

Programme monitoring   Medium  The Country Office did not utilize the eTools field monitoring dashboard data to monitor the results of field visits in order to ensure that all necessary actions were recorded for follow-up. Quality assurance over programmatic visit reports needs strengthening. There were gaps in the quality of programmatic visits in the Field Monitoring Module based on the audit’s review of samples and the office’s internal quality review in 2025.  According to the UNICEF Procedure on Monitoring, programme monitoring includes regular tracking of progress in key interventions and country programme outputs, covering implementation of work plan activities, programmatic risks and bottlenecks, the end-use of supplies, feedback from affected populations and the evolving context (including unintended effects of programmes). The audit assessed the adequacy and effectiveness of field monitoring and programmatic assurance and made the following observations:  Use of the eTools field monitoring dashboard  The eTools field monitoring dashboard shows an overview of monitoring visits in FMM, including the coverage, summarized findings, the quality of the visits and any bottlenecks. However, the Country Office did not utilize this dashboard data to monitor the results of field visits.  The audit selected 21 sample programmatic visit reports with 23 significant negative responses to key monitoring questions related to PSEA, supplies, activity implementation and community feedback in the dashboard and noted the following:  •   In 2 out of 23 cases, the action points were closed without sufficient update or supporting evidence of implementation by the partners.  •   In 6 out of 23 cases, there was no action point recorded to address the gap identified in the visit for follow-up.  •   In 8 out of 23 cases, the monitoring report did not document as audit trail on why an action point was not needed to address the gap identified during the visits.  •   In 6 out of 23 cases, the staff incorrectly selected the negative response to the questions.  Root cause: Since 2024, the Country Office was focused on the transition from the Trip Management Module to FMM and had not become fully familiar with the eTools field monitoring dashboard data.

13  Quality and coverage of programmatic visits  Programmatic visits provide assurance with respect to the status of programme implementation compared to the agreed work plan and the reliability of programme results reported by implementing partners.  The audit reviewed a sample of 25 programmatic visit reports from FMM and noted the following:  •   The details section: In 2 out of 25 cases, there was no reference to the Programme Documents. In another case, the team member and report reviewer were the same person, which is not in line with the segregation of duties principle.  •   The summary report section: In 2 out of 25 cases, the general observations were too brief, or vague and unhelpful. In another case, there was no general observation and many key questions in the report were not answered. In six cases, not all key questions on PSEA were answered, and in one case the supplies questions were not answered.  •   Action Point section: In 12 out of 25 cases, there was no action point recorded in FMM to address the gaps identified in the visit, or the report did not document an audit trail on why an action point was not needed. In four cases, the action points in FMM had been closed without clear update and/or without sufficient evidence of implementation.  The Country Office conducted a quality review of sample programme visit reports in the first half of 2025, following a 2022 audit recommendation to establish a structured quality assurance mechanism. Many of the findings from the quality review were similar to the findings above from audit samples. The findings and recommendations in the quality review were shared in the Programme Coordination Meeting attended by 22 key programme and Programme Monitoring and Reporting (PMR) staff in July 2025. However, six out of seven recommendations and actions discussed in the Programme Coordination Meeting were not recorded in the office action points matrix for tracking.  In September 2025, the PMR section organized two online webinars on “HACT Programmatic Visit and Field Monitoring Module (FMM) of eTools”, including the quality review findings, to reinforce best practices and improve consistency in reporting.  Root causes: The Country Office started using FMM in 2024 and only transitioned fully from the Trip Management Module to FMM in 2025. Thus, there was a learning curve for programme staff to become fully familiar with the new functionalities (e.g., digitized data collection, site-specific question checklists and custom questions and templates) and requirements in FMM. Some gaps remained, as shown by the office’s own quality review exercise.

14  AGREED ACTIONS  The Bangladesh Country Office agrees to:  i.   Strengthen the quality assurance process of programmatic visits, including the  use of a quality assurance checklist for reviewers.  ii.   Utilize the eTools field monitoring dashboard to enhance the quality of  monitoring and reporting and to ensure relevant action points are recorded to  address significant gaps observed during the visits. Cash transfers to implementing partners   Medium  Some implementing partners submitted reimbursement authorization forms after the start of the implementation period. The delay in authorization requests by partners may increase the risk of programme implementation by partners before authorization by UNICEF.  The Bangladesh Country Office worked with 270 implementing partners who received cash transfers totalling US$110.1 million between January 2024 and October 2025. Of the 270 implementing partners, 46 were civil society organization partners that received resources amounting to US$72.2 million, or approximately 66 per cent of the total cash resources transferred during the period.  The audit team assessed whether the Country Office had established adequate and effective controls to ensure timely and accurate transfer of resources for planned activities and prompt reporting of utilization by implementing partners.  The auditors noted that there were delays in partners’ submission of reimbursement authorization forms. Under the reimbursement modality, the partners are required to submit a request for authorization to incur expenditures in support of programme activities in advance. In four out of nine reimbursement samples, the partners submitted the request for authorization between 9 and 49 days after the start of the implementation period. This practice may increase the risk of programme implementation by partners before the authorization by UNICEF.  Root   causes: There was insufficient oversight and monitoring to ensure all partners submitted their request for authorization to incur expenditure in support of programme activities before the start of the implementation period.

15  AGRE ED ACTION  The Bangladesh Country Office agrees to reinforce the requirement for partners to  submit their request for reimbursement authorization before the programme  implementation period. HACT financial assurance   Medium  The Country Office exercised oversight of the completion of required financial assurance activities. However, some high-priority action points from spot checks were not recorded in eTools or closed with sufficient evidence of implementation by partners, which may result in delays in corrective actions, recurring findings in subsequent assurance activities and missed opportunities for improvement of partner capacity.  According to UNICEF’s HACT procedure, assurance activities must be conducted during programme implementation to verify that results are achieved as planned and funds used for their intended purposes, in line with the work plan or Programme Document. These activities support the management of fiduciary risks associated with cash transfers. The audit assessed whether the Country Office’s HACT assurance activities were adequately planned, executed and followed up to provide sufficient, timely assurance with respect to the use of UNICEF resources and noted the following:  Follow-up of high priority observations  Timely, complete and accurate follow-up of financial findings 1   is key to ensuring that the funds transferred to implementing partners are used for their intended purpose to achieve results for children. The follow-up process entails prioritization of findings (high/low priority), action plan development and implementation of required follow-up actions. For example, 10 sample spot checks conducted by audit firms had high-priority action points. In four spot checks, the office did not record all high-priority recommendations in eTools for follow-up, mostly related to the lack of bank reconciliation. In two spot checks, high-priority action points were closed in eTools without sufficient supporting evidence of implementation. Service contracts   Medium  The audit noted that 15 out of 18 members of the Contract Review Committee had not completed the training required before appointment to the committee, which meant that they were ineligible for appointment to the CRC.  During the audit period, the Country Office issued service contracts with a total value of approximately US$48 million. Most of the contracts were selected through open competition, and approximately half of the procurements were conducted through long- term agreements that had been established with vendors.  The audit selected a sample of 20 contracts with a total value of $6.4 million dollars, or approximately 13 per cent of the expenditure on service contracts during the period audited and tested the procurement and contract management processes to assess whether they were in line with the relevant policies and procedures governing the procurement and management of the contracts. In general, the audit found that the office complied in all material respects with the relevant policies and procedures as to how the need for services was identified, how terms of reference of assignment bids were developed and approved, how bids were advertised and how bids submitted by vendors were evaluated to ensure value for money for UNICEF; that where necessary the selections were reviewed and endorsed by the CRC; that single source procurements were

17  adequately justified; and that vendors were evaluated at the end of their contracts. However, there are some opportunities for improvement:  Contract Review Committee  The CRC is an advisory body assembled to support risk management and facilitate independent review of the procurement process for higher-value goods and services exceeding predetermined limits.  The audit verified that the CRC had produced an annual report for 2024 as required by the UNICEF Procedure on the Contract Review Committee and that it had adequately reviewed the   high-value   procurements   from   the   sample   of   service   contracts   referred   to   in Observation 8 below.  The UNICEF Procedure on the Contract Review Committee states that individuals nominated to serve on this committee must have completed the Procurement at UNICEF course. This training is necessary to ensure that committee members are familiar with CRC processes and   are   adequately   prepared   to   take   on   their   accountabilities   as   members   of   the committee.  The audit noted that 15 out of the 18 CRC members at the Bangladesh Country Office had not completed the Procurement at UNICEF course prior to joining the committee. When informed of this issue by the audit team, country office management took immediate action and ensured that the 15 CRC members who had not completed the training did so prior to the conclusion of the audit fieldwork.  Root cause: The Country Office had not checked that all staff nominated to serve as members of the CRC had completed the required training.  AGREED ACTION  The Bangladesh Country Office agrees to ensure that in future all staff nominated to  serve as members of the CRC have completed the required training. Supplies and logistics   Medium  Month-on-month inventory valued at $500K–$1M remain in partner warehouses, including obsolete items dating back to 2021, resulting in an aged stockpile and risk of

The stock reports obtained from partners monthly offered limited insights into inventory management, with no evidence of detailed analysis and reporting by the supply and programme sections. Inadequate warehouse conditions due to limited partner capacity and government requirements for transitionary facilities expose supplies to safety hazards.  From January 2024 to October 2025, the Country Office distributed US$16.6 million worth of supplies in addition to $8.8 million worth of supplies procured through programme documents with implementing partners. The audit sought to obtain assurance that the partners properly handled the supplies delivered to them.  At least three partners in Cox’s Bazaar hold inventories valued at $500K–$1M, as per implementing partner monthly stock reports. The reports offered limited insights into stock management, as neither the supply nor programme sections evidenced reporting or detailed analysis of the stock reports. This was largely attributable to the manual nature of the process and capacity constraints within the team. Based on review of the stock reports and visits to partner warehouses, the audit observed that some supplies distributed between 2021 and 2024 remained in implementing partner warehouses, creating an aged stockpile and posing a risk of obsolescence. The audit team observed that obsolete supplies dating back to a 2021 curriculum change still occupied scarce warehouse space at partner sites, pointing to gaps in disposal of obsolete inventory. The stockpile reflects a shift towards prepositioning, contrary to the intended role of partner warehouses as staging points for distribution to beneficiaries. Losses through obsolescence or theft of supplies at partner warehouses pose accountability risks for UNICEF with donors since distributions to partner warehouses are recorded as expenditure.  Root causes: Supply demand estimation is undermined by the constant influx of displaced persons and changes in government requirements.  The audit team observed weaknesses in the storage conditions of the two partner warehouses during the audit, including the absence of smoke detectors and fire alarms, bamboo cutting surrounding one warehouse, the use of bamboo reeds for temperature regulation in another, and a staircase without a handrail. Lack of appropriate safeguards in a context where fires are an imminent threat may impact the Country Office’s ability to respond and or prevent stock losses in the event of an incident.  Root causes: Partner warehousing capacity is insufficient, and government mandates for transitionary facilities discourage investment in existing structures.  The audit noted that the supply section has been providing warehousing and logistics capacity-building sessions to partners.

19  Supplies   performance   monitoring  The country office management team monitors supply key performance indicators (KPIs), including goods in transit. During the review, the audit team supplies recorded as GIT amounted to US$700,000 although they had already been distributed to beneficiaries. The programme section engages vendors to distribute supplies to beneficiaries and submit invoices based on actual consumption by beneficiaries. However, the audit team noted that these supplies continued to be recorded as GIT from January through October 2025, raising concerns about the accuracy of supply reporting and reconciliation. Despite reminders from the supply section and reporting KPIs in Country Management Team meetings, the section responsible acknowledged a lapse in expensing supplies distributed to beneficiaries, which were addressed during the audit. Inadequate management of GIT may result in misreporting and create accountability and monitoring gaps.  Root causes: This issue results from inadequate follow-up and resolution of supply performance monitoring flags.  AGREED ACTIONS  The Bangladesh Country Office agrees to:  i.   Analyse partner inventory stock reports to derive insights and report on  inventory management including stockholding position, redistribution and  disposal as needed. In collaboration with the Supply Division, expedite roll-out of  the system to improve visibility of UNICEF supplies at partner warehouses.  ii.   Establish internal protocols requiring written prepositioning plans and  contingency triggers for redistribution of supplies at partner warehouses. These  steps should be reviewed and approved by the Country Management Team and  aligned with emergency preparedness standard operating procedures.  iii.   Finalize partner logistic capacity assessments and establish secure and  appropriate storage facilities, including periodic inspection and maintenance of  safeguards in place.  iv. 20  Definition s   of Audit   Observation   Ratings  To assist management in prioritizing the actions arising from the audit, OIAI ascribes a rating to each audit observation based on the potential consequence or residual risks to the audited entity, area, activity or process or to UNICEF as a whole. Individual observations are rated as follows:  Low  The observation concerns a potential opportunity for improvement in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes. Low- priority observations are reported to management during the audit but are not included in the audit report. Action in response to the observation is desirable.  Medium  The observation relates to a weakness or deficiency in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes that requires resolution within a reasonable period to avoid adverse consequences for the audited entity, area, activity or process.  High  The observation concerns a fundamental weakness or deficiency in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes that requires prompt/immediate resolution to avoid severe/major adverse consequences for the audited entity, area, activity or process, or for UNICEF as a whole.  Definitions of Overall Audit Conclusions  The above ratings of audit observations are then used to support an overall audit conclusion for the area under review, as follows:  Satisfactory   The assessed governance, risk management, and control processes were adequate and functioning well.  Partially  Satisfactory,  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management, and control processes were generally adequate and functioning but needed improvement. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified were unlikely to have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  Partially  Satisfactory,  Major  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management, or control processes needed major improvement. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified could have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  Unsatisfactory  The assessed governance, risk management or control processes were not adequately established or did not function well. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified could have a severely negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  A PPENDIX
