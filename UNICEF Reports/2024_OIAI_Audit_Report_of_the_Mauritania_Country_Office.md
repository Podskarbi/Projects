EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  3  The Office of Internal Audit and Investigations (OIAI) conducted an audit of the   Mauritania Country  Office , covering the period from   1 January 2023   to   30   August 2024 .   The audit   team’s site visit  was conducted   from   19   to   30 August 2024   in accordance with   the Code of Ethics and   the  International Standards for the Professional Practice of Internal Auditing.   The overarching  objective of the audit was to   assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the governance, risk  management   and   control processes over a selection of significant risk   areas   of the   Mauritania  Country Office,   including   supply planning, inventory management and supply end - user monitoring  (SEUM),   c ontracts for   s ervices ,   social cash transfers,   and   safety and security .   The   specific risks  evaluated are described   in the Audit Objective ,   Scope   and Approach   Section of this report.  The   Mauritania   Country Office operates in a   complex   environment   with   a   high poverty rate ,  increasing   climate   change   challenges   and a refugee crisis   that negatively impact s   poor and  vulnerable households. The   Mauritania Country Programme   is delivered through   69   implementing  partners, mainly government partners. During the audited period, the Country Office   transferred  approximately US$ 16 . 3   million to implementing partners,   representing   41   per   cent   of total  expenditure.   The Country Office   spent   approximately US$ 5 . 4   million on   services   and US$ 2 . 4  million on   programme supplies ,   representing   14   per   cent and   6   per   cent of total expenditure s ,  respectively.   There were several risks related to   transferring   cash to implementing partners   for  distribution to beneficiaries   and   the   procurement , warehousing   and distribution of programme  supplies. The audit   therefore   sought to determine   whether and how   the Country Office managed  those risks. C ONTEXT  6  Mauritania is a lower - middle - income country with a gross domestic product per capita of  US$ 2 , 149 . In 202 3 , 1   Mauritania   had an estimated population of   4.9   million, of which   52   per   cent  were children   under   1 9 .  While   initiatives   have been   undertaken  in the country   to create enough jobs,  especially   for   youth,   and   to   ensure  redistributive   growth   and   equitable  access to public services , more efforts  are needed .   The poverty rate remains  high (28.2 per   cent in 2024), especially  in rural areas (41.2 per   cent, compared  with   14.4   per   cent   in   urban   areas).  Nearly 56.9 per   cent of the population  lives   below   the   multidimensional  poverty line. Children are the poorest  age group, with a 31 per   cent rate of  monetary   poverty   and   an   estimated  61.9 per   cent rate of multidimensional  poverty.   Thirty - six   per   cent   of   14 –  19year   olds and 44 per   cent of 20 –  24year   olds   are   not   in   education,  employment   or   training.   Significant  disparities   exist   between   urban   and  rural areas, rich and poor areas, and  regions .  Mauritania   is   experiencing   an   accelerated   influx   of   refugees,   coupled   with   the   return   of  Mauritanian refugees from Mali due to the deteriorating security situation there.   In 2023, nearly  1.1 million people, including 411,300 children, needed humanitarian assistance .  The UNICEF Country Office is based in Nouakchott, and there are field offices in Bassikounou  and Kiffa. There are also outposts in Selibaby and Nema that were   established to implement the  multi - sectoral programmatic approaches promoted by the CPD in the regions targeted by the  programme.   At the time of this audit, t he Country Office   had   7 2   approved   staff positions , with   55  in   Nouakchott,   and 14   in Bassikounou   (9)   and   Kiffa   (5) . There   was   also   an   outpost   in   Selibaby  with two staff positions and another in   Ne ma   with one .   T here were 1 9   vacant posts ,   including four  posts at the P3 level or above.  The   2018 - 20 22   C ountry   P rogramme   D ocument (CPD)   was extended in   early   2023   to enable the  Country Office to ensure that the   new 2024 - 2027   CPD   was   finalized following a consultative and  participative   process   driven   by   the   United   Nations   Sustainable   Development   Cooperation  Framework (UNSDCF). AUDIT OBJECTIVES ,   SCOPE   AND APPROACH  8  The overarching objective of the audit was to   assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the  governance, risk management   and   control processes over a selection of significant risk   areas   of  the   Mauritania Country Office ,   including   supply planning, inventory management and   supply end -  user monitoring ( SEUM ) , contracts for services, social cash transfers, and safety and security .  The audit scope   was determined   during the audit planning process based on an assessment of  inherent risks 2   and included the following areas:  •   Protection from Sexual Exploitation and Abuse (PSEA)  •   Accountability to Affected Populations (AAP)  •   Safety and Security  •   C ontracts for Services  •   Social Cash Transfers  •   Resource Mobilization  •   HACT Assurance Activities  The audit was conducted through remote preparatory interviews with Country Office management  and   representatives of   the West and Central Africa Regional Office and an on - site visit from 19 to  30 August   202 4. It   was conducted in   conformance   with   the Code of Ethics and   the International  Standards for the Professional Practice of Internal Auditing. For   testing, the audit covered the  period from   1 Janu ary 2023   to   30 August 2024 .   It involved   a combination of methods, tools and  techniques,   including   interviews,   d ata   analytics,   document   review,   tests   of   transactions,  evaluations and validation of preliminary observations.  2   Inherent risk refers to the potential adverse event that could occur if management takes no actions, including internal  control activities. The higher the likelihood of the event occurring and the more serious the impact would be should the  adverse event o ccur, the stronger the need for adequate and effective risk management and control processes.

OBSERVATIONS AND MANAGEMENT ACTION PLAN  9  1.   Fraud   Risk management   Medium  T he Country Office conducted   an   annual risk assessment , including fraud risk assessment ,   and  developed an anti - fraud strategy.   It   needs to provide fraud training to implementing partners and  service providers to increase their fraud risk awareness and reinforce UNICEF’s zero - tolerance  policy on fraud .  The Country Office operates in an increasingly challenging environment, with economic, social,  and security - related factors contributing to heightened risk s .   In this context, the audit evaluated  the   adequacy of the Country Office’s approach to assessing and managing risks, including fraud  risk ,   ensuring adequate controls and   effective   mitigation measures were in place .   The Country  Office   conducted its   2024   annual risk and   fraud risk assessment   collaboratively ,   with input from  all programme sections   and   field offices and   support from the Regional Office   to   assist in  developing   its anti - fraud strategy .   The   audit team   reviewed the Country Office’s assessment and  agre ed that   that   risk s and mi tigation measures   identified   were relevant   and   i n place.   T he 2024  risks   were approved   by the   C ountry   M anagement   T eam   at   its   September   meeting.   In addition to  the risk assessments, the   Country Office   developed an anti - fraud strategy that set out the roles  and responsibilities of management and staff members and the   critical   fraud prevention, detection  and reporting measures. The strategy was aligned with   UNICEF’s   global corporate anti - fraud  strategy   and its long - standing   zero - tolerance   position .  Fraud   training for implementing partners and service providers:   The   audit team noted that  no fraud training was provided to   implementing partners   and service providers ,   an important  control   for   fraud   detection and prevention.   This was confirmed during   visits to   four   implementing  partners . The Country Office   told   the   auditors it   planned   to   conduct   the training in September  2024.   The lack of or delay in providing fraud training increased the Country Office’s exposure to  potential   fraud ,   which   could   result   in   finan cial   losses,   reputational   damage,   and   non -  implementation of planned activities.  Internal controls effectiveness:   During the period examined, the audit team noted that the  Country Management Team   regularly   monitored   the   effectiveness of   its   internal   controls at its  monthly meetings ,   during which key performance indicators were reviewed .   The   Country Office  predominantly   relied on HACT assurance activities to ensure that programme activities were  implemented as intended.   However, the   audit team identified   weaknesses   in   the Country Office’ s  implementation and monitoring of HACT assurance activities.   The   Country Office’s   reliance on  HACT assurance activities to monitor the effectiveness of its internal controls   expos es   it to  additional risk , including delays in identifying   and responding to   issues .  In May 2024, t he Country Office created an efficiency   task force   to strengthen   its   efficiency and  effectiveness by   focusing on risk management, the transfer of funds implemented according to  the HACT procedure, the follow - up of audit recommendations ,   and the proper use of   it s resources.  The task   force   only met once ,   in July 2024. The audit team   therefore   could not assess its  effectiveness. See recommendations in   the   HACT assurance activities section.  AGREED   ACTION  The Country Office   should   conduct   fraud training for implementing partners and service  providers to   increase risk awareness and reinforce UNICEF’s zero - tolerance   policy on fraud .

Supply planning, inventory management and   s upply  end - user monitoring   (SEUM)   Medium  Challenges   in   logistics and warehouse management reduced management’s oversight of the  storage and movement of goods and the degree of assurance that supplies were being received  on a timely basis by the intended beneficiaries.  In the period under review, the Country Office procured   supplie s worth US$ 2 . 4   million   ( six   per  cent of total expenditure ) .   At the time of the audit,   the Country Office had   two   warehouses ,   in  Nouakchott   and Bassikounou , with   a   total   inventory balance of   approximately   US$0.5 million .   The  audit evaluated the controls related to supply and logistics planning, warehousing and inventory  management ,   and distribution of supplies to partners and beneficiaries.   The audit team visited  the warehouse in Nouakchott.  The audit team found that supply planning was   comprehensive and   timely ,   and there was good  collaboration   with   programme   sections. The Country Office also was   implementing a SEUM plan .  OAIA also noted the following:  Nouakchott warehouse management : The   audit team noted   that the Nouakchott warehouse  was managed by   the Country Office but   was owned by the Government.   The   warehouse   was  used free of charge .   N o agreement   or   documentation   was prepared to outline the   roles and  responsibilities of the   parties   involved   or   the conditions   of use . The lack of documentation  increased the Country Office’s exposure to liability in the event of a dispute, which could result in  loss of resources and increased reputational risk.  Delays in   delivery   to partners :   During the audit period , supplies related to 16 release orders ,  totaling US$0.4 million   ( 22 per   cent of the supplies shipped from the UNICEF warehouse to  partners ) ,   were shipped 3 1   to   138   days later than expected.   D uring the same period,   supplies  totaling   US$0.2 million   (   44 per   cent of the total direct deliveries to partners from local vendors )  were also shipped between 31 and   186   days later than expected.   The   Country O ffice   stated   that  these delays were due to unrealistic delivery dates set by sections ,   delays by the Country Office’s  programme sections in recording the handover to partners in the corporate system, VISION, and  lack of readiness of government partners to receive the supplies at their premises. Inadequate  logistics planning led to delays in   t he   delivery of supplies to partners.  AGREED   ACTION S  The Country Office should:  i.   Ensure that a signed agreement is established with the Government of Mauritania  concerning   it s use of the Nouakchott warehouse ,   in which the roles and responsibilities  of all parties   are   documented.  ii.   Strengthen   its   logistics   planning,   including   improved   communication   with   critical  stakeholders, to   increase   the efficiency and timeliness of delivery   of supplies to  partners . Safety and Security   High  Weaknesses in   the implementation of the programme criticality plan   and in the office’s security  assessments   exposed the   Country O ffice’s staff and programme activities to significant security  risks given the   country's   context.  In 2023, there was an accelerated influx of refugees, coupled with the return of Mauritanian  refugees from Mali due to the deteriorating security situation in that country. Consequently, the  Country Office implemented   humanitarian   programmes   on the country’s southern border with  Mali, regarded as an area of   high insecurity.   The   Country Office   was   expecting the   number of  refugees to increase   throughout   2024.  Programme   Criticality :   The   Programme   Criticality   exercise   is a systematic evaluation used by  the United Nations to determine the importance of   programmatic   activities, especially in high - risk  areas   where the   security situation   is volatile .   The   most senior United Nations representative in -  country leads the assessment, and most UN   C ountry   T eam (UNCT)   member organizations are  involved .   In November 2023,   the   UNCT developed and approved a programme criticality  document, which included an implementation plan   covering the period from December 2023 to  November 2024 . HACT Assurance   A ctivities   High  The implementation and oversight   of   HACT assurance activities   were not adequate.   Quality  assurance   over   programmatic   visit   report s   need ed   str e ngthening .   R ecommendations   from  assurance activities   were not systematically recorded to facilitate tracking   and follow - up of action  points, increasing   the risk that control weaknesses persist and potentially   impact the delivery of  results.  The Harmonized Approach to Cash Transfers (HACT) framework requires UNICEF country  offices to conduct assurance activities ,   such as programme monitoring, spot checks and audits,  to ensure proper use of cash transfers by implementing partners. The HACT framework also  requires timely, complete and accurate follow - up of financial findings.   During the audited period,  the Country Office   prepared a cash transfer assurance plan that was periodically updated. It  included spot checks, programmatic visits, and scheduled audits .   The audit team   reviewed the  assurance activities and noted the following areas for improvement.  Spot checks :   A minimum of one spot check is required for all implementing partners reporting  annual   expenditures   of more than US$50,000 from funds   provided by UNICEF.   In 2023, the HACT  assurance plan indicated that   34   spot checks were required   for 58 partners . The Country Office  completed   33 .   However, fewer   spot - check visits were completed than were required for four  partners with combined expenditures of US$0.4 million.   This limited the Country Office’s ability to  verify how resources were managed, confirm   that   funds were used as intended, and take timely  corrective actions.  Programmatic visits (PVs):   Programmatic visits   ensure   the status of programme implementation  compared   to   the   agreed   work   plan   and   the   reliability   of   programme   results   reported   by  implementing partners.   Six partners   receiving   a total of   US$0.6 million had   fewer   programmatic

19  visits than the minimum required.   The audit   team   reviewed a sample of 13 PV reports,   5   reports  on CSO partners and   8   for government partners. The audit team noted   that   7   of   the   13   PV   reports  were   not   actually   programmatic visit reports but rather notes   from   meetings with   implementing  partners .   S ome   recommendations from the programmatic reports were closed in eTools without  adequate   evidence or   explanation.   The actions   recommended for follow - up were general and had  no deadlines assigned for their implementation.   PSEA and AAP were not monitored in four of   the  five CSO reports   reviewed .  Follow - up of action points:   UNICEF requires country offices to use eTools to facilitate more  efficient, transparent tracking and follow - up of assurance activities and related   recommendations.  After   a   review of a sample of programmatic visits, spot checks, audits and micro - assessments,  the audit team noted deficiencies in   creating and recording action points   in eTools .   T here were  instances where   no deadlines   were   set for implementation ,   and no action   was   taken by the partner  to address the findings.   N one of the high - risk findings   for either micro - assessments or   audits   were  recorded in eTools. Two audit reports noted that the recommendations from previous micro -  assessments, which   included some high - risk recommendations, were not implemented.   A   review  of the sample of spot check reports noted instances where the deadlines to address the action  points in the report were different   from   those recorded on eTools,   and no justification was  provided.   In other reports,   the follow - up action to close the high - priority finding was   insufficient .  T here were 30 overdue action points, of which   7   were high priority.  The issues   noted   highlight a lack of adequate oversight in monitoring action points, which is  e ssential for ensuring that corrective actions are taken to ensure that programme implementation  activities achieve results for children as planned.  AGREED   ACTION S  The Country Office   should :  i.   Strengthen   implementation and oversight of HACT assurance activities to ensure that  the assurance plan maximizes coverage of higher - risk partners and activities and  complies with relevant corporate policies.  ii.   Identify   and   implement   appropriate   actions   to   improve   quality   and   reporting   for  programme monitoring.  iii.   E nsure   high - priority   actions are   correctly   recorded, reviewed, implemented, and closed  in eTools. 20  Definition s   of Audit   Observation   Ratings  To assist management in prioritizing the actions arising from the audit, OIAI ascribes a rating to  each audit observation based on the potential consequence or residual risks to the audited entity,  area, activity or   process,   or to UNICEF as a whole. Individual observations are rated as follows:  Low  The observation concerns   a   potential opportunity for improvement in the  assessed governance, risk management or control processes . Low - priority  observations   are reported to management during the audit but   are   not  included   in   the audit   report.   Action   in response   to the   observation   is  desirable.  Medium  The observation relates to   a   weakness or deficiency in the assessed  governance, risk management or control processes   that   requires resolution  within a reasonable period of time to avoid adverse consequences for the  audited entity, area, activity or process.  High  The observation concerns a   fundamental weakness or deficiency in the  assessed governance, risk management or control processes   that   requires  prompt/immediate resolution to avoid severe/major adverse consequences  for the audited entity, area, activity or process ,   or for UNICEF as a whole.
