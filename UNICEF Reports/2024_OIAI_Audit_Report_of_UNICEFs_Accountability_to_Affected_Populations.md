EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  2  The   Office   of   Internal   Audit   and   Investigations   (OIAI)   conducted   an   audit   of   UNICEF’s  accountability to affected population s   (AAP)   covering the period from   January 2022   to   December  2023 .   The audit was conducted   from   March 2023   to   December   2023   in accordance with   the Code  of Ethics and   the International Standards for the Professional Practice of Internal Auditing.   The  objective of the audit was   to   assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the governance, risk  management ,   and control processes   across   a selection of significant risk   areas   of UNICEF ’ s   AAP  approach ,   including the oversight and coordination of UNICEF headquarter divisions,   AAP  strategies and accountabilities, integration into country office s’   plans, and community - based  feedback mechanisms .   D escriptions of the specific risks evaluated are provided in the Audit  Objective s , Scope and Approach   s ection   of this report .  UNICEF subscribes to   the   Inter - A gency   Steering Committee (IASC) and Core Humanitarian  Standards   AAP   P rinciples , which   define AAP as   a   commitment by humanitarians to use power  responsibly   to   take   account   of,   give   account   to ,   and   be   held   to   account   by   the   people  humanitarians seek to assist.   According to   the   UNICEF AAP   S trategy   2022 - 2025 ,   the goal   of AAP  is t o ensure that affected children and families participate in the decisions that affect their lives,  are properly informed and consulted, and have their views acted upon.   To achieve th is goal, one  of   UNICEF’s four strategic objectives for AAP requires that all country offices integrate AAP into  their plans, with appropriate tech nical and financial support in place to support this effort. Feedback mechanisms   Medium  Due to the lack of evidenced - based processes for   obtaining feedback   from   beneficiaries,   it was  unclear how UNICEF’s requirement that all country programmes are informed by the views,  participation and feedback from communities was being met.  Clarity on who m   to   obtain   feedback from :   The   IASC emphasizes the need for aid organizations  to be “held accountable by the people we seek to assist” . In UNICEF, all country offices in  emergency situations develop a Humanitarian Appeals for Children (HAC) that state s   the number  of people expected to receive UNICEF assistance   -   be it cash, supplies, etc. Given that the   IASC  emphasizes the need for aid organizations to be “held accountable by the people we seek to  assist” , the audit team expected that AAP   targ ets   would be the same as the number of people  expected to receive UNIC E F assistance. However, this was not   the   case ,   as the AAP targets in  the same HAC were often higher because they include cross - sectorial targets for all interventions  including humanitarian cash transfers, social behaviour change (SBC), etc. For example, in one  of the audited country offices, the AAP   targets were the same as the targets for social behaviour  change . This   approach creates risks to the effectiveness of   the   feedback process a s the tools  use d   to obtain feedback related to SBC may not work for beneficiaries of other types of  interventions like humanitarian cash transfers. The audit team noted that whil e   feedback can be  obtained from   SBC targets through mass communication, the same cannot be said for targe t s of  social cash transfers who may lack mass communication tools like radios and mobile phones as  well as the wherewithal to use such tools.  Establishing feedback and complaint mechanisms:   Adequate planning of the feedback  process can result in the identification of the   appropriate   tool   and   method ology   and ultimately  increase the success of the feedback process.   C ountry offices had in place various feedback -  gathering tools   such as   call centres, surveys, television ,   radio,   and   social media. However, the  audit   team   did not see evidence of adequate planning of feedback   processes. It was therefore  un clear whether unique characteristics like culture,   language,   and   education level of the target  population were adequately   consider ed   when choosing the   appropriate tools .   The a udit of   three  country offices   identified   gaps in   the   use   of tools   among   partners and target communities .  Moreover, some of the   tools   used were one - way   communication and   did not allow for feedback  from beneficiaries.  Seeking feedback from beneficiaries :   Whil e   there was evidence of consultations with partners  including   government ministries at national level and/or with provincial/state authorities at a  decentrali z ed level , there was insufficient evidence to show that the views of the affected

9  population were directly   sought or taken into consideration in the initial identification, selection  and prioritization of interventions.   Due to the lack of guidance on who to consult at each stage  (e.g. design, planning and execution), a plausible explanation of the lack of direct consultation  with end - beneficiaries could not be established.   The audit   team   also noted that offices required  clearly defined   evidenced - based processes for the consultation and coll ection   of beneficiaries’  views, as well as how   t hey   should be   u sed   in the selection and prioritization of interventions. In tegrating   AAP   in to   country plans   and interventions   Medium  Due to the lack of   evidence - based processes for the consultation, collection and analys i s of  beneficiaries’ views and the use of such views to adjust programming, it was unclear how  UNICEF’s requirement that all country programmes are informed by the views, participation and  feedback from communities was being met.  The   IASC’s   AAP   F ramework   2023   O utcome 5   requires that   response   implementation   be  coordinated   and   driven   by   informed   community   participation   and   feedback   systems   and  monitored and adjusted accordingly. The IASC refers to adaptation of systems based on collected  feedback and the   subsequent   modification of programmes as “ c losing the loop”. UNICEF’s  second strategic objective for AAP dovetails the outcome 5 requirements. Specifically, one of the  UNICEF’s four strategic objectives for AAP requires that all country programmes are informed by  the views, participation and feedba ck from communities and provide timely information to affected  communities. Another   strategic objective for AAP requires that all country offices integrate AAP  into their plans, with appropriate technical and financial support in place to support this effo rt.   The  UNICEF   AAP   h andbook   (2020)   emphasizes integrating AAP principles into humanitarian work  and the programme cycle   to   ensure that the communit y’s   voice   is   heard, needs   are   met ,   and  rights   are   respected.   The   audit therefore assessed whether adequate mechanisms were in place  to collect, analyse and take corrective action based on the feedback.   The audit   team   noted:  Resources for AAP in country offices :   During the audit of 12 country offices, it was observed  that the representatives and deputy representatives were aware of their   accountability to  implement AAP, and in all 12 countries, staff members   were   assigned AAP responsibilities.   Due  to the lack of clear criteria and an appropriate resourcing model, the   country   offices found it  challenging to ensure   that   adequate resources   were in   the right places.   T he audit   team   noted that  the level s and   reporting lines of   AAP   staff   differed   from one country office to another.  Mechanisms   for   integrat ing   AAP in   country office   plans :   For   m ost   of the   12 country   offices  audited , the re   was no evidence   that   AAP   was discussed   in   relevant senior management meetings  such as   within   country management team s , emer gency management team s   or   programme review

10  committee s   where important decisions about programming were made . Mechanisms for integrating AAP in   activities assigned to partners :   The UNICEF   AAP  Strategy   identified   the   in tegration   of   AAP   into   planning   and   monitoring   systems   and   the  articulation of what is expected from partners (local,   national,   or international)   who are   the primary  interface with communities as   two of five   strategic priorities . Due to the lack of clear guidance and criteria, it was  un clear whether there were plausible reasons for   the   country   offices   not embedding AAP - related  requirements in   some   programme documents. Furthermore, t he audit   team   found no requirement  for   any g overnment implementing partner to implement AAP activities.   This may be due   to   the  lack of   guidance   to help country office s   handle potential political sensitives around requir ing  government implementing partners to implement AAP activities.  Analysing and using   beneficiaries’   feedback   in programming :   OIAI noted that the 12 country  offices audit ed   were indeed delivering   lifesaving   intervention services   –   e.g. ,   they were distributing  supplies and cash, paying for various social services, building, and rehabilitating schools and  health facilities for the benefit of affected children and families. For many of these responses and  interventions such as the unconditi onal social cash transfer programme,   country   offices   were  collecting feedback and comp laints from beneficiaries. However, there was no concrete evidence  that t his was   systematically analysed and used to adjust plans, programmes, and interventions.  Self - r eport ed   progress   towards   integration :   The   2023   insight   report   on   core   standard  indicators   (CSIs) 8   reported a general increase   in the progr ess against   global   AAP   indicators .   This  self - reported progress   may be overly   optimistic . Several   audits in 2023   found   that   the integration  of AAP was   still   in   the   early stage s , a n observation   that   was confirmed by the   country   offices  themselves . 13  APPENDIX  Definition s   of Audit   Observation   Ratings  To assist management in prioritizing the actions arising from the audit, OIAI ascribes a rating to  each audit observation based on the potential consequence or residual risks to the audited entity,  area,   activity,   or   process,   or to UNICEF as a whole. Individual observations are rated as follows:  Low  The observation concerns a   potential opportunity for improvement in the  assessed governance, risk management or control processes . Low - priority  observations   are reported to management during the audit but   are   not  included   in   the audit   report.   Action   in response   to the   observation   is  desirable.  Medium  The observation relates to   a   weakness or deficiency in the assessed  governance, risk management or control processes   that   requires resolution  within a reasonable period of time   to avoid adverse consequences for the  audited entity, area, activity or process.  High  The observation concerns a   fundamental weakness or deficiency in the  assessed governance, risk management or control processes   that   requires  prompt/immediate resolution to avoid severe/major adverse consequences  for the audited entity, area, activity or process ,   or for UNICEF as a whole.  Definition s   of Overall Audit Conclusions  The above   ratings of audit observations are then used to support an overall audit   conclusion for  the area under review, as follows:  Satisfactory   The assessed governance, risk management or control processes  were adequate and functioning well.  Partially  Satisfactory,  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management or control processes  were   generally   adequate and functioning   but needed improvement .  The w eaknesses or deficiencies identified   were   unlikely   to have a  materially negative impact on   the performance   of the audited entity,  area, activity   or   process.  Partially  Satisfactory,  M ajor  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management or control processes  needed   major   improvement .   The   w eaknesses   or   deficiencies  identified could   have a materially negative impact on   the   performance  of the   audited entity, area, activity or process.  Unsatisfactory  The assessed governance, risk management or control processes  were   not   adequately   established   or   not   functioning   well .   The  w eaknesses or deficiencies identified could   have a severely negative  impact on   the   performance of the   audited entity, area, activity or  process.
