EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  3  The Office of Internal Audit and Investigations (OIAI) conducted an audit of the Armenia Country Office (ACO), covering the period from January 2024 to November 2025. The audit was conducted in November 2025 in conformance with the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global Internal Audit Standards. The objective of the audit was to   assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the governance, risk management and control processes related to a selection of significant risk   areas   of the ACO.   The specific risks evaluated are set out in the Audit Objective, Scope and Approach section of this report.  The Armenia Country Office (hereafter the Country Office) works with government and civil society organizations (CSOs) to implement planned programme activities. During the audited period, the Country Office transferred approximately US$11.1 million in cash and US$0.8 million in supplies to implementing partners from January 2024 until November 2025, representing 55 per cent of expenditure. There were several risks related to using partnerships and supply and cash transfers to implementing partners. Furthermore, the country experienced challenges resulting from an influx of approximately 115,000 Armenian refugees from late 2022 until September 2023, straining social services and infrastructure. The audit, therefore, sought to determine whether and how the office managed those risks.  Overall Conclusion  Based on the audit work performed, OIAI concluded that the assessed governance, risk management, or control processes were partly satisfactory, meaning that the assessed governance, risk management, and control processes were generally adequate and functioning but needed improvement. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified were unlikely to have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process. C ONTEXT  6  Armenia, with a population of 2.8 million, faced the arrival of over 115,000 ethnic Armenian refugees from late 2022 to September 2023, in addition to 26,700 people displaced since the 2020 escalation of the Nagorno-Karabakh conflict. Refugee integration became a national priority, with an impact on social services and infrastructure. Assessments by UNICEF and the Ministry of Labour and Social Affairs highlighted disparities in living conditions, low preschool attendance among refugee children, dropout risks for adolescents, and widespread but underreported mental health concerns.  Domestically, new social benefit laws were adopted, and a draft Youth Policy Law was reviewed. The UN noted progress on child rights but urged greater investment in protection, justice, and anti-discrimination measures.  Despite the refugee crisis, Armenia’s economy continued to grow, outpacing the regional average. Labour market trends were mixed, with surveys showing higher unemployment but official data pointing to more formal jobs. Youth unemployment remained elevated, alongside persistent gender gaps. Poverty indicators improved overall, though some measures worsened. Inflation stayed low, but rising global food prices posed risks.  Social spending increased in absolute terms in education and social protection, while health expenditure declined. Education showed progress with preschool enrolment rising to 68.6 per cent. Deinstitutionalisation of children with disabilities advanced through foster care, resource centres, and personal assistant services, though disability-related costs continued to exceed benefits. Health reforms focused on universal insurance, though rollout was delayed. Neonatal mortality remained high, vaccination coverage improved, and adolescent overweight and poor nutrition persisted. Justice sector reforms emphasised independence, anti-corruption, and accessibility, while expanding forensic psychology capacity for child investigations.  These dynamics shaped UNICEF Armenia’s operating environment, requiring a balance between emergency response and long-term support to national reform processes in education, child and social protection, justice, youth and health. Rising demands, resource constraints, and evolving political frameworks challenged programme implementation. Despite these pressures, UNICEF Armenia remained committed to advancing child rights and ensuring that Armenia’s most vulnerable children—particularly refugees, those with disabilities, and those at risk of exclusion—are not left behind.  The Armenia Country Office has 40 staff (2 IPs, 22 NOs and 16 GSs) and three vacancies. The main office is located in the capital, Yerevan (38 staff), with a field office in Goris (2 staff). The Representative, Christine Weigand, started her role on 22 September 2021. The Deputy Representative, Silvia Mestroni, has held her position since 16 April 2021.

AUDIT OBJECTIVES ,   SCOPE   AND APPROACH  8  The objective of the audit was to   assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the governance, risk management and control processes related to a selection of significant risk   areas   of the ACO.  The audit scope was determined during the audit planning process based on an assessment of the inherent 1   risks   of the ACO,   and included the following areas:  •   Governance  •   Risk Management  •   Partnerships, programme management and monitoring  •   Resource mobilization  •   Partner evaluation and reporting  •   Cash transfers to implementing partners and HACT assurance  •   Supply chain, including warehousing  •   Construction  •   Procurement  •   Cash assistance to beneficiaries  •   PSEA  •   Emergency response  The audit fieldwork was conducted in November 2025 in conformance with the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global Internal Audit Standards. For the purpose of audit testing, the audit covered the period from January 2024 to November 2025. The audit involved a combination of methods, tools and techniques, including interviews, data analytics, document review, tests of transactions, evaluations and validation of preliminary observations.  1   Inherent risk refers to the potential adverse event that could occur if management takes no actions, including internal control activities. OBSERVATIONS AND MANAGEMENT ACTION PLAN  9  The key areas where actions are needed are summarized below.  1. Risk management   Medium  The Country Office demonstrated commendable practices, including integrating risk management into planning and procurement processes and maintaining proactive discussions at the management level. However, gaps remain that could undermine governance and accountability. Conflict of interest declarations were not completed by all staff with decision-making abilities, and training oversight lacked systematic reconciliation of requirements against participation. Furthermore, the inclusion of mandatory special conditions for indirect construction projects was inconsistent, exposing the office to reputational and operational risks.  All UNICEF offices are required to implement a comprehensive risk management process that identifies, assesses, responds to, and monitors risks within their operating environment. Risk consideration is fundamental to effective management and begins during strategy formulation and continues throughout the programme cycle. A robust risk management approach safeguards UNICEF’s reputation, supports the achievement of strategic objectives, and ensures operational continuity and safety.  With ongoing updates to UNICEF’s risk management framework, there is an opportunity to reinforce strategic alignment and embed forward-looking risk practices across Country Offices. Risks should be systematically identified, documented, and integrated into operational planning and decision-making. Mitigation measures must be clearly defined, assigned to responsible parties, and regularly monitored for effectiveness.  The audit reviewed the Armenia Country Office’s Risk Register to assess its completeness and relevance, with a focus on the identification and evaluation of mitigation measures. It examined whether mitigation strategies were documented and aligned with identified risks, and whether active monitoring was in place. Additionally, the audit evaluated whether risk management results were consistently discussed in Country Management Team (CMT) meetings, reflecting a culture of proactive oversight and governance.  The Country Office has integrated risk identification into its planning processes, including its annual risk assessment and management plan. Risk management considerations are embedded in procurement processes, and risk-related discussions are a regular feature of CMT meetings. The office demonstrated agility by documenting ongoing changes to the risk environment in CMT meeting minutes, together with updated mitigation actions, and tracking implementation progress.

10  Despite the integration of risk identification, the audit identified some gaps in the Country Office’s risk management approach:  Conflict of interest: Declaring one’s conflicts of interest is crucial to reducing fraud risk and upholding UNICEF's values of integrity and accountability. The UNICEF Financial Disclosure Policy 2   list staff members who are required to complete annual conflict of interest declarations. The requirement applies to staff members in leading roles, participants in procurement processes, and other staff with decision-making authority. During the audit, the audit team noted that annual conflict of interest declarations had been submitted only by the Representative, the Deputy Representative, and the Operations Manager. Failing to declare a potential or actual conflict of interest diminishes trust.  Root cause: The Country Office referred to historic communications from the Ethics Office to reduce the number of conflict-of-interest declarations, given the extensive use of these statements, thereby limiting the Ethics Office's capacity. Following this communication, the Country Office interpreted the communication too strictly, reducing the number of declarations below the requirements in the Financial Disclosure Policy.  Training oversight: UNICEF has a clear institutional responsibility to ensure that all partners and associated entities are adequately trained in key areas, including PSEA and anti-fraud. The audit reviewed the adequacy of the Country Office’s training programme for PSEA, and anti-fraud. The team also examined its monitoring of the completion of this training. The Armenian refugee situation creates a context where SEA risk can emerge, aligned with the UN SEARO’s (Sexual Exploitation and Abuse Risk Overview) risk dimensions, elevating the risk of SEA. Moreover, the country is ranked 63 out of 180 countries on Transparency International’s corruption index, indicating a medium fraud risk.  The Country Office provided examples of training materials on PSEA and Anti-Fraud to implementing partners. The training materials were well-structured and adapted to the local context. The team found that the training sessions were also provided at regular intervals. However, the office did not reconcile training requirements against training participation. This leaves a potential for implementing partners not attending training sessions without the Country Office’s knowledge, hindering the Country Office’s ability to identify training gaps and systematically implement corrective actions. Moreover, they present a reputational risk to UNICEF.  Root cause(s): The Country Office could not verify whether all relevant service providers and partners had received required mandatory training due to a lack of oversight.  2   The UNICEF Financial Disclosure Policy as set out in Executive Directive CF/EXD/2012-003

11  Management of risk related to indirect construction : To protect country offices against the additional risk involved in indirect construction projects (construction by implementing partners), the UNICEF procedure for construction projects demands inclusion of special conditions in Programme Documents (PDs) or Simplified Programme Documents (SPDs) for Civil Service Organizations.  The audit team noted that the Country Office did not include these special conditions in four out of seven programmes with CSOs during the audit period. Whilst staff explained to the audit team that they were aware of the obligations for special conditions, they informed the audit team that, when creating PDs/SPs, a checkbox needed to be ticked at a particular stage in the process. As such, if this box was not checked, all the progress required to restart as it was not possible to check this box later in the process. The staff could further document that for two of the four projects without special conditions included, these conditions had been communicated to the partners as a mitigating measure. Still, these emails did not bind the partners to the stated conditions. By not including mandatory special conditions for indirect construction in PDs/SPDs, staff exposed the Country Office to an increased risk of loss and reputational damage.  Root Causes: Country Office staff have chosen not to amend or recreate PDs/SPDs when the requirement for special conditions for construction is identified late in the process to avoid any delays. Combined with insufficient oversight, these issues have not been detected and rectified by management.  AGREED ACTIONS  The Country Office should:  i.   Request all staff with decision-making abilities to complete the annual conflict of  interest declaration.  ii.   Monitor partner compliance with training requirements and establish a routine  to follow up on training gaps.  iii.   Strengthen oversight to ensure adherence with indirect construction  requirements for special conditions in PDs/SPDs. HACT assurance activities   Medium  The audit team identified weaknesses in HACT assurance practices. Gaps in programmatic visit reports included inadequate rationales to support conclusions, self-review, and a lack of mandatory PSEA monitoring. Further, differences between the UNICEF spot check

12  guidance and national accounting practices in interpreting expense recognition led to several spot-check findings that increased the workload for implementing partners and UNICEF, thereby reducing operational efficiency.  Under the Harmonized Approach to Cash Transfers (HACT) framework, UNICEF country offices must carry out assurance activities, including programme visits, spot checks and audits, to ensure implementing partners' proper use of cash transfers. The framework also requires timely, complete and accurate follow-up of financial findings from assurance activities. Whilst the Country Office timely completed all required assurance activities, the audit team identified the following weaknesses:  Programmatic visits: Programmatic visits serve as a key mechanism for providing programmatic assurance, verifying the efficient use of resources in activity implementation, and ensuring the effective prevention of sexual exploitation and abuse (PSEA). The audit reviewed an audit sample of 15 programmatic visits completed during the audit period and made the following observations:  •   In five cases, the reports did not include rationale and justifications for the report conclusions.  •   In three cases, the person who performed the programmatic visits and the person who reviewed the report were the same.  •   Four out of 14 reports relating to CSO partners did not include mandatory PSEA monitoring.  Weaknesses in programmatic visits could reduce the Country Office’s ability to identify issues and take corrective measures, or to make necessary adjustments to agreed activities, leading to reputational damage.  Root Causes:   Insufficient oversight led to weaknesses in programmatic visit reporting that went undetected and unmanaged.  Spot checks: The audit noted recurring issues during spot checks regarding the treatment of accrued expenses, particularly concerning statutory staff entitlements. External auditors, guided by the UNICEF spot checks guidance, considered expenses eligible only if incurred and paid within the same reporting period. Partners, however, maintain accrual- based accounting records in line with IPSAS and national standards. Whilst the partners, based on guidance from the Country Office, aim to cover all expenses during the project periods, they face particular challenges with accruals for statutory staff entitlements such as vacation leave, salaries, taxes, and social security payments. These differences in requirements between the UNICEF spot check guidance and IPSAS and national standards adhered to by implementing partners have resulted in repeated findings of ineligible expenses, increasing operational workload and reducing efficiency.

13  Root Cause:   There is a difference in expense recognition between the UNICEF spot check guidance, which applies a cash-based approach (delivered and paid), whilst IPSAS and national standards require an accrual-based approach, recognising expenses as they occur.  AGREED ACTIONS  The Country Office should:  i.   Develop and implement a procedure for programmatic visits, including a  requirement for adequate rationale to support conclusions, establishing a clear  segregation-of-duties protocol and PSEA monitoring for all CSO programmatic  visits.  ii.   Seek guidance from DAPM and DFAM to instruct external auditors to accept  expenses accrued based on national accounting standards as eligible expenses. Warehousing   Medium  Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food (RUTF) was stored under suboptimal conditions that could lead to contamination, reduced nutritional value or loss.  The general provisions of the UNICEF procedure on warehouse and inventory management require country offices to provide clean storage, free from significant temperature variations, with established pest control protocols. Further, to ensure temperature-controlled cold storage for nutrition goods.  The audit team visited the Yerevan warehouse, where US$ 25,570 worth of Ready to Use Therapeutic Food (RUTF) was stored. The audit team noted that the RUTF was stored under suboptimal conditions. Specifically, the warehouse lacked temperature control, was dusty, and had no established process for regular cleaning or pest control, with rodent droppings visible on some cartons. Further, some of the RUTF boxes were exposed to direct sunlight, and the summer temperatures would likely exceed the maximum storage temperature of 30 degrees Celsius. The current RUTF storage conditions pose a risk of contamination, reduced nutritional value, or loss.  Root Causes: The country office lacked sufficient oversight of storage conditions and the specific requirements for storing RUTF.

14  AGREED   ACTION  The Country Office should:  i.   Establish and implement procedures to assess and comply with inventory-  specific storage requirements.  Staff Responsible: Procurement Associate, Operations Manager  Implementation Date:   March 2026

15  Definition s   of Audit   Observation   Ratings  To assist management in prioritizing the actions arising from the audit, OIAI ascribes a rating to each audit observation based on the potential consequence or residual risks to the audited entity, area, activity or process or to UNICEF as a whole. Individual observations are rated as follows:  Low  The observation concerns   a potential opportunity for improvement in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes. Low- priority observations are reported to management during the audit but are not included in the audit report. Action in response to the observation is desirable.  Medium  The observation relates to a weakness or deficiency in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes that requires resolution within a reasonable period to avoid adverse consequences for the audited entity, area, activity or process.  High  The observation concerns a fundamental weakness or deficiency in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes that requires prompt/immediate resolution to avoid severe/major adverse consequences for the audited entity, area, activity or process, or for UNICEF as a whole.  Definitions of Overall Audit Conclusions  The above ratings of audit observations are then used to support an overall audit conclusion for the area under review, as follows:  Satisfactory   The assessed governance, risk management, and control processes were adequate and functioning well.  Partially  Satisfactory,  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management, and control processes were generally adequate and functioning but needed improvement. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified were unlikely to have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  Partially  Satisfactory,  Major  Improvement  Needed  The assessed governance, risk management, or control processes needed major improvement. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified could have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  Unsatisfactory  The assessed governance, risk management or control processes were not adequately established or did not function well. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified could have a severely negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process.  APPENDIX
