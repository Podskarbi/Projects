EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  3  The Office of Internal Audit and Investigations (OIAI) conducted an audit of the UNICEF Dominican Republic Country Office, covering the period from January 2024 to September 2025. The audit was conducted in September 2025 in conformance with the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global Internal Audit Standards. The objective of the audit was to assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the governance, risk management and control processes related to a selection of significant risk areas of the Dominican Republic Country Office. The specific risks evaluated are set out in the Audit Objectives, Scope and Approach section of this report. The audit also contributes to OIAI’s broader regional assurance and risk assessment activities for the Latin America and the Caribbean region.  The Dominican Republic Country Office implements a significant proportion of its programme activities through partnerships with government entities and civil society organizations (CSOs), as well as outsourced service arrangements. During the audited period, the office transferred approximately US$6.2 million to implementing partners, representing a considerable share of programme expenditure, and procured approximately US$4.4 million in supplies and services through external vendors. In parallel, the office expanded its private sector fundraising (PSFR) operations, generating approximately US$2.3 million in revenue in 2024 alone. These modalities entail inherent risks related to partner oversight, procurement integrity, safeguarding and management of high-volume financial transactions and donor data. The audit therefore sought to assess whether the Country Office had established and applied adequate governance, risk management and control measures to manage these risks effectively.  Overall Conclusion  Based on the audit work performed, OIAI concluded that the assessed governance, risk management or control processes were Partially Satisfactory, Improvement Needed, meaning that the assessed governance, risk management, and control processes were generally adequate and functioning but needed improvement. The weaknesses or deficiencies identified were unlikely to have a materially negative impact on the performance of the audited entity, area, activity or process. C ONTEXT  8  The Dominican Republic is an upper-middle-income country with an estimated population of approximately 11.3 million, of whom children and adolescents account for about one third. Over the past decade, the country has made progress in several child-related outcomes, including access to basic health services and education. Persistent disparities remain, however, particularly affecting children living in poverty, those in rural and border areas, children of migrant backgrounds, and children exposed to violence and protection risks. These disparities continue to shape UNICEF’s programme focus and delivery modalities.  The country is exposed to recurrent natural hazards, including hurricanes, floods and earthquakes, which periodically disrupt essential services and increase vulnerabilities for children and families. In addition, the country context presents a moderate risk environment for governance, integrity and safeguarding, requiring strong internal controls, partner oversight and risk management mechanisms, particularly where service delivery relies on third parties.  UNICEF Country Programme  The Dominican Republic Country Programme is guided by the 2023–2027 Country Programme Document, which focuses on child survival and development, education and adolescent development, child protection, social policy and programme effectiveness. Across these areas, UNICEF emphasizes systems strengthening, policy advocacy, institutional capacity development, and the use of digital solutions to improve equity and sustainability of results. Programme delivery relies largely on partnerships with government institutions and CSOs, making effective partnership management and assurance mechanisms central to programme performance.  Between January 2024 and September 2025, the Country Office collaborated with 19 implementing partners, 5 government entities and 14 CSOs, through which approximately US$6.2 million was transferred in cash, with CSOs receiving the majority of transfers. During the same period, the office procured approximately US$4.4 million in supplies and services, primarily through service contracts. In parallel, private sector fundraising activities expanded under a regional cluster model, generating approximately US$2.3 million in gross revenue in 2024. These delivery modalities involve significant reliance on external partners and vendors, as well as high-volume financial transactions, heightening the importance of strong governance, risk management, safeguarding and internal control processes. AUDIT OBJECTIVES ,   SCOPE   AND APPROACH  10  The objective of the audit was to assess the adequacy and effectiveness of the governance, risk management and control processes related to a selection of significant risk areas of the Dominican Republic Country Office.  The audit scope was determined during the audit planning process based on an assessment of the inherent risks 4   of the Dominican Republic Country Office and focused on the following areas:  •   Governance  •   Risk management, including fraud risk management  •   Private sector fundraising  •   Partnership management  •   Harmonized approach to cash transfers  •   Procurement of services and supplies  The audit fieldwork was conducted from 15 to 22 September 2025 in conformance with the Institute of Internal Auditors’ Global Internal Audit Standards. For the purpose of audit testing, the audit covered the period from January 2024 to September 2025.  The audit involved a combination of methods, tools and techniques, including interviews with management and staff, review of policies, procedures and documentation, analysis of financial and operational data, testing of selected transactions and assurance activities, and validation of preliminary observations with the Country Office.  4   Inherent risk refers to the potential adverse event that could occur if management takes no actions, including internal control activities. OBSERVATIONS AND MANAGEMENT ACTION PLAN  11  The key areas where actions are needed are summarized below.  1. Risk and fraud risk management   Medium  The Country Office has strengthened its risk management framework by establishing a risk management committee and regularly updating its register. The framework remains incomplete, however, as key fraud risks (e.g., bribery, conflicts of interest, misconduct) are not fully captured and assessed, staff awareness and training are insufficient, and risk ownership is not clearly defined or documented. These gaps may weaken the overall effectiveness of risk and fraud prevention in an environment with moderate-to-high corruption risk.  UNICEF offices must implement enterprise and fraud risk management in line with organizational policies. Risks across strategic, operational and fraud areas must be identified, assessed and monitored, with fraud risks such as bribery, misuse of assets and conflicts of interest clearly documented, rated and linked to mitigation actions. Offices must also maintain staff awareness, training and reporting mechanisms to uphold integrity and accountability.  The Dominican Republic Country Office has taken positive steps to strengthen its risk management framework. The 2024 and 2025 risk registers show progress in consolidating and monitoring risks, with entries covering key areas such as natural disasters, funding, human resources, information and communication technology, business continuity and implementing partner oversight. A risk management committee meeting every two months was established in 2025 following the Regional Office peer review feedback, providing a structured forum to review identified risks and mitigation measures. Yet several gaps remain.  Despite the generally high-risk operating environment, the risk register does not include fraud-related categories such as bribery, conflicts of interest or internal misconduct, focusing instead only on the misappropriation or misuse of funds and supplies. The office explained that certain risks were excluded because they were considered already mitigated; however, such risks should still be documented with inherent risk levels, related controls and residual ratings. Staff awareness of fraud reporting procedures was limited, with some staff members unaware of their obligation to report suspected misconduct to OIAI. There is no centralized documentation recording the participation in fraud awareness training, or tracking whether fraud risks are discussed in Country Management Team or all-staff meetings. In addition, risk owners were not consistently aware of their assigned responsibilities, leading to uneven accountability for mitigation actions.

12  The absence of comprehensive fraud risk coverage, weak staff awareness and unclear ownership limit the effectiveness of the office’s risk management framework. These gaps increase the likelihood of undetected fraud, misuse of resources or delayed response to integrity incidents and reduce management’s ability to make risk-informed decisions in a moderate-to-high corruption environment.  Root cause: These weaknesses stem from the evolving maturity of the office’s risk management processes, which have focused on compliance and procedural updates rather than embedding systematic fraud risk assessment, documentation and accountability into daily operations.  RECOMMENDED ACTIONS  The Dominican Republic Country Office should   strengthen its risk and fraud  management framework by embedding systematic fraud risk assessments into daily  operations, expanding the risk register to include relevant fraud categories, clarifying  risk ownership, documenting fraud awareness and training activities, and ensuring that  staff are aware of the proper channels for reporting misconduct.  Staff Responsible: Operations Officer and People & Culture Officer  Implementation Date: 30 September 2026  2. Harmonized approach to cash transfers   Medium  The Country Office has an established HACT framework and assurance plan, but significant delays and quality gaps were identified in the implementation and documentation of financial and programmatic assurance activities. Incomplete spot checks, missing micro-assessments, and weak verification and reporting by the external assurance firm limit assurance over the use of funds and the financial integrity of implementing partners.  The harmonized approach to cash transfers requires UNICEF offices to conduct and document assurance activities (such as micro-assessments, spot checks and programmatic visits) based on implementing partner risk ratings. These activities are intended to ensure that funds are used for their intended purposes and that partner financial controls are adequate. Spot checks should be sufficiently detailed to verify compliance with partner rules and agreements, the alignment of expenses with work plans, and consistency between financial documentation, accounting records and the Funding Authorization and Certification of Expenditure (FACE) form.  Between January 2024 and September 2025, the Country Office collaborated with 19 implementing partners, 5 government entities and 14 CSOs that received approximately

13  US$6.2 million in cash transfers, with CSOs realizing 84 per cent of the total or US$5.2 million. The office conducted programmatic monitoring internally and outsourced financial assurance activities to an external audit firm under a long-term agreement. While this arrangement ensured access to technical capacity and supported segregation of duties, the execution and documentation of assurance activities revealed significant weaknesses in both timeliness and quality of the work completed.  Micro-assessments: At the time of the audit, six implementing partners that each received more than US$100,000 in cash transfers had not undergone the required micro- assessments to evaluate their financial and supply management capacity. These omissions may have limited the Country Office’s ability to determine appropriate risk ratings and assurance modalities. The delays were attributed to the late contracting with the external audit firm following the United Nations Development Programme bidding process, compounded by delays from implementing partners in providing the necessary documentation.  Spot checks: Implementation of financial assurance activities was delayed, with only 23 per cent (3 out of 12) of the planned spot checks completed at the time of the audit for the period under review. In 2024, two implementing partners that had each received over US$50,000 in direct cash transfers were not subject to spot checks, contrary to minimum HACT requirements. A review of five completed spot-check reports revealed that they were primarily checklist-based, containing “yes/no” responses without narrative analysis or supporting evidence. The reports lacked details on the procedures performed, and they did not include explanations of how the spot-checkers verified compliance with implementing partner rules and agreements, the linkage between expenditures and work plans, or the proper authorization and recording of transactions. Similarly, there was no documentation on whether expenses reported in FACE forms matched accounting records, whether supporting documentation met HACT requirements or whether prices paid were consistent with UN rate standards.  Across the 21-month review period, none of the 11 spot checks conducted identified ineligible expenditures or control deficiencies. While this may reflect adequate financial management by implementing partners, the audit noted limited documentation on the procedures performed to support this conclusion. The external firm did not provide its standardized workpapers for review despite a formal audit request, which reduced transparency over the scope of testing and the evidence underpinning the assurance outcomes. Additionally, there was no indication that the Country Office regularly reviews the assurance deliverables.  Programmatic visits: At the time of the audit, the Country Office had completed 50% (15 out of 30) of the required programmatic visits for 2025. A review of a sample of five programmatic visit reports showed that four of these consisted primarily of minutes of meetings or exchange of documents, which do not qualify as programmatic visits. At the

14  same time, one included only photographs from a meeting with community leaders without specifying the activities verified. The limited execution and documentation of programmatic visits reduce the reliability of evidence supporting progress against targets and weaken the assurance that funds were used for intended programme results.  Delays and poor quality in financial and programmatic assurance activities may hamper the Country Office’s ability to confirm that funds were used for their intended purposes, assess partner financial capacity or identify ineligible expenditures. This could increase exposure to financial loss, misreporting and reputational risk, while potentially weakening the reliability of partner risk ratings and assurance planning.  Root causes: The observed weaknesses stem from delays in contracting the external audit firm, insufficient internal oversight of third-party deliverables, and limited staff understanding of HACT procedures and documentation standards.  RECOMMENDED ACTIONS  The Dominican Republic Country Office should:  i.   Ensure that all pending micro-assessments, spot checks and programmatic visits  are timely completed.  ii.   Strengthen quality control over third-party assurance work by documenting  regular reviews of the deliverables for completeness, evidence and compliance  with UNICEF HACT guidance.  iii.   Enhance staff capacity and accountability for planning, conducting and  documenting programmatic visits to ensure they provide verifiable assurance of  results and fund utilization. Procurement   Medium  The Country Office has introduced positive procurement control measures, including an electronic platform and pilot initiatives to strengthen oversight of low-value procurement. Gaps remain, however, in bid safeguarding, Contract Review Committee training and contract award documentation. These weaknesses increase the risk of non-compliance with procurement principles, compromise transparency and reduce assurance that vendor selection and contract awards achieve best value for money.

15  Under UNICEF policy requirements, all procurement processes must ensure fairness, transparency and value for money through proper solicitation, bid handling, evaluation and approval procedures. The CRC is responsible for independently reviewing and endorsing procurement submissions above established thresholds, and its members are required to complete mandatory procurement training. Offices must also maintain adequate segregation of duties and secure processes for the receipt, storage and opening of bids.  Between January 2024 and August 2025, the Country Office procured approximately US$0.5 million in programme supplies and US$3.9 million in services, primarily for PSFR and consultancy activities. The audit noted several good practices, including the introduction of an electronic procurement platform that improved segregation of duties between the Operations and Programme teams, and a pilot project to strengthen oversight of low-value procurement. These initiatives demonstrate the office’s efforts to enhance internal controls and compliance maturity.  Despite efforts to strengthen oversight, significant weaknesses remain in procurement governance and documentation. Training compliance among CRC members was insufficient, as only one of the 10 members had completed the mandatory “Procurement at UNICEF” course. This limits the CRC’s ability to provide effective, independent and informed review of procurement submissions and increases the risk of procedural errors or inconsistent application of evaluation criteria.  Controls over solicitation and bid handling were also weak. For procurements below US$100,000, bids were received via email accessible to a single staff member, who also performed the financial evaluation. For higher-value procurements, vendors deposited their submissions into an unlocked box at the United Nations compound reception, which was not continuously monitored or secured. These practices do not provide adequate safeguards for bids and create risks of unauthorized access, alteration or substitution of bids before opening.  The audit also identified deficiencies in contract award documentation. In one of six service contracts reviewed, the award was made to a vendor that did not achieve the highest combined technical and financial score, and no sufficient justification for this decision was recorded. Additionally, the financial evaluation documents were missing from the CRC submission package, preventing a full review of the award recommendation. Such documentation gaps reduce transparency and compromise assurance that vendor selection decisions are based on best value for money and comply with UNICEF procurement principles.  Insufficient CRC training, weak bid safeguarding and incomplete procurement documentation undermine transparency and accountability in the procurement process. These weaknesses increase the risk of procedural non-compliance, conflicts of interest or

16  vendor favouritism and may result in suboptimal use of resources and reputational harm to UNICEF.  Root causes: The observed gaps stem from limited enforcement of procurement procedures, inadequate segregation of duties in the solicitation and evaluation stages, and insufficient clarity and accountability in procurement oversight roles.  RECOMMENDED ACTIONS  The Dominican Republic Country Office should:  i.   Ensure that all Contract Review Committee members complete the mandatory  procurement training as required.  ii.   Strengthen the process for the receipt and safeguarding of bids, ensuring  controlled access and segregation of duties between bid receipt, evaluation and  award.  iii.   Maintain all required documentation concerning procurement submissions to the  CRC and ensure that contract awards are based on documented evaluation  results, with written justification for any deviations.  Staff Responsible: Operations Officer, Supply Associate and CRC Secretary  Implementation Date: 30 June 2026  4. Implementing partnerships   Medium  The Country Office has taken steps to strengthen partnership management, including completing a mapping exercise of civil society organizations to identify new and qualified partners. However, the absence of systematic partner performance assessments and limited documentation of the Partnership Review Committee (PRC) review feedback reduce the effectiveness of oversight and learning from partnership implementation and may hinder the selection of the most capable and accountable partners.  UNICEF’s partnership policy framework recommends that partner selection and management be based on transparent, competitive and well-documented processes. All programme documents under programme cooperation agreements must undergo appropriate review by the PRC, particularly when the value of UNICEF’s contribution exceeds US$100,000. The PRC is expected to provide documented comments on key aspects of each partnership proposal, such as the framework, budget realism, risk assessment and value-for-money considerations, and make informed recommendations to the head of office. In addition, offices must perform partner performance assessments upon completion of programme documents to evaluate results achievement, compliance and partnership effectiveness.

17  From January 2024 to September 2025, the office collaborated with 19 implementing partners, 5 government entities and 14 CSOs, receiving approximately US$6.2 million in cash transfers. The audit noted a positive practice through the completion of a CSO mapping exercise in 2024, which broadened the Country Office’s understanding of the national partnership landscape and potential new partners.  Despite this progress, key oversight mechanisms in partnership management were not fully applied. A review of five programme documents revealed that in three cases where a performance assessment of the implementing partner was due, it had not been conducted. The absence of these assessments limits the Country Office’s ability to evaluate partner performance, capture lessons learned and inform future partnership decisions.  In addition, the review of PRC minutes and documentation showed that, in three of the programme documents presented to the committee, no written comments were recorded by PRC members in critical areas, including the results framework, budget analysis, value- for-money considerations or implementation risks. This absence of documented feedback suggests that the PRC’s review process may have been cursory, reducing the quality of its recommendations to the head of office and the robustness of the partnership approval process.  Weak PRC oversight and the lack of systematic partner performance assessments limit evidence-based decision-making and institutional learning. These gaps may result in partnerships that do not fully align with UNICEF’s value-for-money principles, increase the risk of recurring implementation weaknesses and reduce accountability for programme results.  Root causes: These weaknesses stem from a limited understanding of roles and responsibilities among staff involved in partnership management, including the PRC, and from insufficient emphasis on the systematic use of performance assessments as a management and learning tool.  RECOMMENDED ACTIONS  The Dominican Republic Country Office should:  i.   Undertake performance assessments for all finalized partnerships to evaluate  partner capacity, results achievement and compliance.  ii.   Strengthen the Partnership Review Committee’s oversight by ensuring that its  reviews are evidence-based and documented, with written feedback and  recommendations addressing key areas of programme design, budget and risk;  as well as providing training to committee members.

Protection from sexual exploitation and abuse   Medium  The Country Office has taken positive steps to strengthen its PSEA framework, including drafting a standard operating procedure to clarify reporting protocols and processes. However, gaps in staff awareness, focal point capacity, partner oversight and harmonized training practices reduce the effectiveness of these measures and increase the risk that SEA concerns will go unreported or unmanaged in a consistent, victim-centred manner.  UNICEF requires each country office to maintain a robust PSEA framework that protects staff, partners and community members from harm. This includes clearly defined governance and accountability structures, the designation of trained PSEA focal points, regular awareness and training for staff and implementing partners, incorporation of PSEA risks into routine monitoring, and the maintenance of safe, confidential, survivor- centred reporting and referral mechanisms.  The Country Office has taken steps to strengthen its internal PSEA framework. A draft SOP has been developed by the PSEA focal point, outlining procedures for receiving, documenting, managing and closing reports of sexual exploitation, sexual abuse, harassment, abuse of authority and other forms of prohibited conduct involving UNICEF staff, partners or beneficiaries. The draft SOP includes sequential steps for handling allegations and emphasizes the importance of a survivor-centred approach that is consistent with organizational safeguarding practices.  Interviews and document review indicated that understanding of PSEA reporting lines and responsibilities varied across staff, with some personnel unclear about expected actions when confronted with potential SEA concerns. The designated PSEA focal point carries multiple functional responsibilities, and the office has not yet assessed the extent to which current workloads affect the ability to carry out PSEA coordination, training and oversight tasks.  Training for implementing partners occurs but is not yet systematic. Smaller non- governmental organizations, in particular, do not consistently receive training on PSEA reporting expectations, risk mitigation measures or survivor-centred response. Documentation of partner training – covering who was trained, when, and on which topics – is incomplete.

19  Monitoring of PSEA compliance during programme visits was limited. Staff reported that their current practice focuses primarily on capturing whether partners have reported SEA cases. The monitoring of broader safeguarding measures, such as the existence and visibility of reporting channels, staff awareness of reporting procedures, or the presence of information materials at service delivery points, is not consistently documented.  Engagement with other UN agencies or international actors on PSEA occurs on an ad hoc basis. While the Country Office has participated in interagency training sessions, there is no standing interagency PSEA coordination structure through which the Country Office regularly exchanges information or harmonizes approaches to safeguarding and community reporting mechanisms.  The gaps in staff awareness, partner training and monitoring of PSEA compliance reduce the effectiveness of the Country Office’s safeguarding framework and increase the risk that SEA concerns will not be identified, reported or managed in a timely and consistent manner. Limited documentation and oversight also weaken assurance that implementing partners maintain accessible and survivor-centred reporting mechanisms, potentially exposing beneficiaries to harm and increasing reputational and operational risks for the office.  Root causes: These weaknesses stem from insufficient clarity of PSEA roles and responsibilities within the office, competing workload demands on the designated PSEA focal point, and the absence of structured approaches to standardize partner training, documentation and monitoring of PSEA compliance across programmes.  RECOMMENDED ACTIONS  The Dominican Republic Country Office should:  i.   Review the resourcing and sustainability of the PSEA focal point role and clarify  staff responsibilities to ensure effective coordination and oversight.  ii.   Implement a structured and harmonized PSEA training and awareness approach  for implementing partners, with particular attention to smaller non-  governmental organizations.  iii.   Strengthen PSEA monitoring by enhancing staff guidance on partner compliance  checks and engaging in interagency mechanisms, in coordination with the  Resident Coordinator’s Office.  iv.   Work with the Resident Coordinator’s Office to strengthen PSEA monitoring and  interagency coordination.  Staff Responsible: Deputy Representative, PSEA Focal Point, Operations Officer and  Programme Team.  Implementation Date: 31 December 2026

20  Definition s   of Audit   Observation   Ratings  To assist management in prioritizing the actions arising from the audit, OIAI ascribes a rating to each audit observation based on the potential consequence or residual risks to the audited entity, area, activity or process or to UNICEF as a whole. Individual observations are rated as follows:  Low  The observation concerns a potential opportunity for improvement in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes. Low- priority observations are reported to management during the audit but are not included in the audit report. Action in response to the observation is desirable.  Medium  The observation relates to a weakness or deficiency in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes that requires resolution within a reasonable period to avoid adverse consequences for the audited entity, area, activity or process.  High  The observation concerns a fundamental weakness or deficiency in the assessed governance, risk management or control processes that requires prompt/immediate resolution to avoid severe/major adverse consequences for the audited entity, area, activity or process, or for UNICEF as a whole.  Definitions of Overall Audit Conclusions  The above ratings of audit observations are then used to support an overall audit conclusion for the area under review, as follows:  Satisfactory   The assessed governance, risk management, and control processes were adequate and functioning well.
